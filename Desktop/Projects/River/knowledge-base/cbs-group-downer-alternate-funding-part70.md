Application for Accreditation – Part B

Method Details – Removal of Old Appliances 

	Purpose of this form

This form is used to apply for accreditation as an Accredited Certificate Provider or ‘ACP’ for the Removal of Old Appliances sub-method (RoOA).  It should be accompanied by Application Form – Part A (general details) and other supporting documentation as indicated in these forms. 

Refer to the Removal of Old Appliances Method Guide to assist you in completing this form.  Essential information about the requirements of applicants and ACPs is also set out in the:

ACP Application Guide, and 

Record Keeping Guide. 

Your application information must be consistent with and meet the requirements set out in these documents.  More information on the application process can be found on the ESS website.

	Meaning of key terms, icons and colours in this form

Words which are defined in the ESS Rule and used in this Application form have the same meaning in this Application Form as in the ESS Rule, unless the context requires otherwise.

Key terms

ACP means accredited certificate provider.  If this application for accreditation is approved, you will be an ACP. 

Activity definition means an activity as specified in a Schedule to the ESS Rule.  Activity definitions for RoOA are set out in Schedule C of the ESS Rule.

ESC means energy savings certificate.  If this application for accreditation is approved, you may be able to create ESCs from the activities conducted under the relevant RESA.

ESS Rule means the Energy Savings Scheme Rule of 2009.  The ESS Rule sets out the specific requirements of each calculation method under the ESS.  Requirements specific to RoOA are set out in clause 9.7 of the ESS Rule.  The ESS Rule is available on the ESS website.

EUE means end-user equipment. EUE is equipment, processes, or systems which consume electricity, gas, or a combination of electricity and gas. This includes the equipment directly consuming electricity and/or gas as well as equipment or products that cause, control or influence the consumption of electricity and/or gas.  

Implementation means the delivery of a RESA at a site.

RESA means recognised energy saving activity.  If this application for accreditation is approved, you will be accredited in respect of a specific energy saving activity.

Site means the location of the EUE included in a RESA, as defined by:

an address; or

a unique identifier, as specified for the relevant implementation that identifies the affected EUE; or

a method accepted by the Scheme Administrator.

The NSW Energy Savings Scheme

The NSW Energy Savings Scheme (ESS) seeks to reduce energy consumption in NSW by creating financial incentives for organisations to invest in energy saving projects. 

Energy savings are achieved by installing, improving, or replacing energy savings equipment.

The Energy Savings Scheme is governed by NSW legislation. It places a mandatory obligation on Scheme Participants to obtain and surrender energy savings certificates, which represent energy savings.  The policy framework is the responsibility of the Department of Planning, Industry and Environment (DPIE). 

The Energy Savings Scheme is now legislated to run until 2050. Target for the ESS will be reset in 2030.

The ESS Rule

The Energy Savings Scheme Rule of 2009 or the ESS Rule sets out how energy saving certificates can be created and prescribes:

•	Types of eligible activities

•	Types of ineligible activities

•	Eligible applicants

•	Detailed calculation methodologies.

The ESS Rule is amended from time to time. Rule changes occur to keep the Scheme up to date and to manage changes in the market and the emergence of new technologies.

However, the broad requirement is that an activity must reduce energy consumption without reducing production levels or quality of service.  Specifically, eligibility can be defined as either:

•	Modifying End-User Equipment

•	Modifying usage of End-User Equipment

•	Replacing End-User Equipment

•	Installing new End-User Equipment

•	Removing End-User Equipment.

Key Roles within the ESS

Key Roles within the ESS include 

IPART as the Scheme administrator and regulator, 

Original or Nominated Energy Savers: the owner or controller of the energy savings

Accredited Certificate Providers (ACPs) who can create and register Energy Savings Certificates.  

Scheme Participants purchase ESCs and surrender them to the Scheme Regulator to meet obligations they have under the Scheme

And the Audit Services Panel who perform some of the Scheme Administrators statutory functions under The Electricity Supply (General) Regulation 2014 (s55 and s56) to ensure the ongoing compliance of Scheme Participants and Accredited Certificate Providers. 

Audits are regularly conducted to protect the integrity of the scheme by verifying that certificates represent real energy savings.

Energy savings certificates (ESC) and Pricing

An energy savings certificate (ESC) is a tradeable certificate created under Division 7 of Part 9 of the Electricity Supply Act 1995. Each ESC represents one notional megawatt hour (MWh) of energy and acts as a tradeable certificate created under the Energy Savings Scheme. 

The ESC price varies depending on market conditions. There is no maximum price for an ESC. However, the penalty price acts as a practical maximum price. If a Scheme Participant does not surrender the required number of certificates (excluding any shortfall it is allowed to carry forward to the next compliance year), it must pay a penalty. 

The prices of ESS have been increasing and may be further impacted when NSW remove lighting upgrades from eligibility. In the short-term removing lighting eligibility may reduce supply of ESS Certificates

The ESS Registry 

The Registry is an online database which records the registration, transfer and ultimate surrender of energy savings certificates.  The functions of the Registry include:

Facilitating the registration and transfer of energy savings certificates

Listing details and tracking ownership of energy savings certificates

Allowing Scheme Participants to surrender energy savings certificates to meet mandatory obligations or company-specific offset schemes.

When a certiﬁcate is created, the Registry records information about that certiﬁcate including the entity, activity and activity type associated with it, and the vintage and creation date.  The Registry also tracks the certiﬁcate status (live, surrendered, or forfeited) and ownership history.  One certiﬁcate represents one notional megawatt hour attributable to energy savings arising from a recognised energy savings activity. (RESA)

The role of CBS Group

CBS Group acts as Downer’s representative and partners with

Accredited Certificate Providers (ACPs) to create and sell energy savings certificates.

Noting:

ACPs can only create certificates from recognised energy savings activities for which they are accredited for;

Energy savings certificates can only be created for projects that were implemented in association with an ACP. 

A nominated energy saver must be nominated as at the implementation date.

CBS Group will ensure Downer implement the energy savings activity in accordance with the Act, Regulation and the ESS Rule, and comply with any accreditation notices specified.  CBS will take responsibility for complying with the requirements of the ESS. 

This includes:

•	Maintaining and storing records of the energy savings projects 

•	Reporting to the Energy Savings Scheme administrator on a regular basis

•	Having the energy savings projects audited by an auditor approved by IPART.

Our Approach

The Energy Saving Certificates (ESCs) under the ESS provide incentive funding for large scale energy efficiency projects in NSW. 

The funding may be accessed yearly in arrears after the energy savings are realized using a Metered Baseline Method (MBM) or in a lump sum using forward creation of up to 10 years using the Project Impact Assessment with Measurement and Verification (PIAM&V) method.   

The Sampling Method is a sub-method of PIAM&V that allows for multiple site ESC creation using a sampling method where there is a population of similar ECMs.  

This sampling/PIAMV method is our recommended option for the upgrades. 

Noting this project will be the first to use this method in NSW, our team has successfully used this method in Victoria for a Yarra Trams project. The options for using this method are:

Current Preferred Option 1: Perform Sampling Method M&V on a tranche of trains sets and claim the energy savings for each tranche.  This method allows for shorter claiming periods and maintains claims under the 50,000 ESC limit.

Option 2: Perform Sampling Method M&V across the entire fleet and claim energy savings for train sets upgraded on a periodic basis (e.g., quarterly).  This method is likely to hit caps on total ESC creation under the current guidelines.

Option 3: Metered Baseline Method.  This involves measuring the energy savings at the boundary of the train network and claiming the actual measure energy savings each year in arrears with a regression model accounting for production variations.  This method is likely to be difficult to implement.

As further exploratory work is performed in development of the M&V Plan, all options will remain under consideration to ensure the most effective option is selected.
