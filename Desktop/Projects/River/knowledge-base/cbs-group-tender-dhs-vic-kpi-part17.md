1. Executive Summary

Situation: The Victorian Department of Health oversees 77 public health services operating under diverse maintenance models — from fully in-house teams to fully outsourced arrangements. This diversity has produced inconsistent asset performance outcomes and fragmented reporting, limiting the Department's ability to benchmark performance, allocate capital effectively, intervene where clinical and safety risks are emerging, or hold health services accountable against common standards.

Complication: Without standardised maintenance KPIs, the Department cannot systematically identify where aging building stock is deteriorating, where essential services infrastructure (HVAC, medical gases, backup power, nurse call systems) is at risk, or where maintenance teams are struggling with inadequate resources. The Department has invested in developing a comprehensive KPI framework — 12 KPIs across two implementation phases, supported by a 7-level maturity model (Level 0: Ad Hoc through Level 7: Transforming) detailed in the September 2025 Maintenance Standards Guidelines Performance Monitoring document. However, translating this framework from policy into operational practice across 77 organisations — each with different CMMS capabilities, workforce capacity, building ages, service profiles, and competing clinical priorities — is a real implementation challenge.

Question: How can the Department implement its KPI framework in a way that is practical, scalable, and enduring — achieving adoption rather than compliance paperwork — while minimising the additional reporting burden on health service maintenance teams that are already operating under resource constraints?

Answer: CBS Group brings Australia's most proven methodology for phased KPI implementation across complex, multi-stakeholder asset management environments. Our proprietary CAPITAL framework has been successfully deployed to implement performance metrics across diverse organisations with varying maturity levels, delivering over $1 billion in validated savings for Transport for NSW across road tunnel assets managed by multiple contractors with fundamentally different capabilities. We propose a team of senior asset management, systems engineering, and change management specialists — led from our Melbourne office — who will apply this proven methodology to the Department's specific health infrastructure context. Our approach is designed around three commitments: segmenting health services by actual maturity (not size or geography) to ensure support is proportionate and relevant; co-designing implementation pathways with health services so they own the framework rather than having it imposed; and integrating KPI data capture with existing maintenance systems and workflows to minimise administrative burden on operational teams.

Our team includes professionals who have worked within Australia's leading health facility management organisations — Broadspectrum (now Ventia), Downer, and Cushman Wakefield — bringing direct understanding of how hospital maintenance is contracted, delivered, and reported at the operational level. Nicole Caranguian designed and built asset management systems for health facilities including the Royal Adelaide Hospital and Royal Adelaide Children's Hospital; Kate Heath developed KPI frameworks for competitive health FM contracts at Broadspectrum. This is not transferred methodology — it is direct health infrastructure experience.

3.2.1 Maturity-Based Segmentation and Tailored Support

The Department's Performance Monitoring guidance establishes a 7-level maturity model progressing from Level 0 (Ad Hoc — reactive, unplanned maintenance with no formal processes) through to Level 7 (Transforming — maintenance function drives organisational strategy with predictive analytics and innovation). CBS Group has extensive experience in applying maturity-based segmentation to calibrate implementation approaches.

Our segmentation methodology integrates two dimensions from the Department's framework:

Dimension 1: Asset Management Maturity Level (Levels 0–7). During Phase 2, CBS will assess each health service against the Department's maturity model using the dual-dimension scoring methodology specified in Attachment 3 — evaluating both System Status (how well the asset management system is documented, structured, and embedded) and Effectiveness of Application (how consistently the system is applied and delivering outcomes), with the final maturity level being the lower of the two scores. This ensures maturity assessments are rigorous and reflect actual practice, not just documentation.

Dimension 2: ESS Category (Category 1, 2, 3). The Department classifies health services by Essential Services category based on infrastructure scale, service scope, and operational complexity. As outlined in the Department's target calibration framework (Attachment 3, Section 4.4), ESS Category directly influences realistic KPI target-setting: Category 1 facilities (major metropolitan hospitals) are generally positioned to begin at higher maturity levels and may already be capable of meeting intermediate targets in the short term; Category 2 facilities may start at mid-range maturity with targets evolving from basic to advanced; and Category 3 facilities may begin at Level 0 with initial targets focused on foundational practices. Our segmentation will cross-reference maturity level with ESS Category to produce a two-dimensional cohort classification that determines both implementation support intensity and target ambition.

Minimum maturity level alignment. Each KPI has a defined minimum maturity level for implementation (specified in the Department's KPI Dictionary). Our pilot selection and rollout sequencing will respect these thresholds: Level 0 minimum KPIs (MTTR, Cost per Bed/sqm, PMP, Major Failures) are applicable to all health services and form the core of Phase 1 rollout; Level 1 minimum KPIs (Asset Register Completeness) require at least basic system use; Level 3 minimum KPIs (Work Order Backlog, Repeated Failures, Planned Task Completion, OEE) require defined processes and standardised systems; and Level 5 minimum KPIs (RCA Effectiveness) are appropriate only for advanced services with optimised processes.

For this engagement, we propose segmenting the 77 health services into three to four maturity cohorts during Phase 2, using the dual-dimension assessment described above. The segmentation will be based on genuine capability (data systems, workforce skills, process maturity, asset register completeness) rather than proxies such as health service size, budget, or metropolitan/regional location. A 120-bed regional hospital with a skilled facilities manager running a well-configured CMMS may be at a higher maturity level than a metropolitan tertiary hospital with a larger but less structured maintenance operation.

Each cohort receives a differentiated implementation pathway aligned to the Department's target calibration framework (Attachment 3, Tables 4–6):

Foundational cohort (Levels 0–2, typically Cat 2/3 facilities): Health services in this cohort are likely operating with minimal formal maintenance management systems — potentially using spreadsheets, paper-based work orders, or basic systems with incomplete data. The implementation focus for these services is on establishing the basic data capture infrastructure needed to report against Level 0 minimum KPIs (MTTR, Cost per Bed/sqm, PMP, Major Failures) and progressing toward Level 1 and Level 3 KPIs as systems mature. Support will be intensive and hands-on, including on-site assistance to configure or establish minimum viable data capture processes, pragmatic workarounds for systems that cannot natively produce KPI data (such as structured manual collection templates), and training focused on fundamental maintenance management practices. KPI targets will be calibrated as "Basic to Intermediate" per the Department's target ambition framework, with a focus on establishing visibility and baseline measurement rather than achieving portfolio benchmarks.

Developing cohort (Levels 3–4, typically Cat 1/2 facilities): These health services have functioning maintenance management systems and processes. All Phase 1 and most Phase 2 KPIs are applicable at this maturity level. The implementation focus is on connecting existing data to the KPI framework, automating where possible, establishing formal target tracking, and building analytical skills. Support includes targeted system configuration assistance, data quality improvement workshops, and coaching on using KPI data for decision-making rather than just reporting. Targets will be calibrated as "Intermediate to Advanced" with formal benchmarking against peer services.

Advanced cohort (Levels 5–7, typically Cat 1 facilities): These health services have mature asset management practices and strong data systems. All 12 KPIs are applicable, including RCA Effectiveness (Level 5 minimum). The implementation focus is on optimisation — refining KPI measurement, external benchmarking against best practice, and extending to advanced analytics and combined KPI insights. Support is lighter-touch, focused on peer leadership (these services can mentor foundational cohort services), continuous improvement, and stretch targets calibrated as "Advanced to Transformational" per the Department's framework.

3.2.2 Engagement with Specific KPIs — Health Infrastructure Context

CBS Group recognises that the Department's 12 KPIs require careful contextualisation for health infrastructure. The following examples show our understanding of the specific implementation challenges:

KPI 6: Maintenance Cost per Bed or Square Metre. This normalisation metric presents particular challenges across the health service portfolio because cost drivers vary fundamentally by facility type. A metropolitan tertiary hospital with operating theatres, intensive care units, and specialised medical equipment has structurally different maintenance cost profiles from a community health centre or a rural sub-acute facility. Our benchmarking approach will establish peer groups based on facility function and complexity, not just size, ensuring that benchmarking produces meaningful comparisons rather than misleading rankings. We will work with the Department to define the normalisation methodology during Phase 2, drawing on our experience in normalising lifecycle costs across transport assets with different operating profiles.

KPI 9: Overall Equipment Effectiveness (OEE). This Phase 2 KPI originates from manufacturing contexts where "availability x performance x quality" has clear quantitative definitions. Its application in health facilities requires careful adaptation. In a hospital context, we propose defining OEE components as: availability (proportion of time critical assets are operational and available for clinical use), performance (whether assets are operating at their designed capacity and specification), and quality (whether asset performance meets clinical and regulatory requirements — such as theatre ventilation air change rates, medical gas purity, and temperature control within specified ranges). This adaptation ensures OEE captures meaningful asset performance information without forcing health services into an artificial manufacturing paradigm.

KPI 1: Mean Time to Repair (MTTR). In health facilities, MTTR has direct clinical risk implications that differ by asset criticality classification. A failure in a general office HVAC system has a different urgency profile from a failure in theatre ventilation or backup power. Our implementation will align MTTR measurement with essential services classifications and clinical risk categories, ensuring the KPI captures the information that matters most for patient safety and service continuity.

3.4.1 Understanding of Key Issues and Risks

CBS Group has identified the following key issues and risks based on our analysis of the RFP, the Department's Performance Monitoring guidance document, and our experience implementing KPI frameworks across complex multi-stakeholder environments.

1. Data quality, availability, and system diversity. Health services at lower maturity levels are likely to have incomplete, inconsistent, or inaccessible maintenance data. Phase 1 KPIs (MTTR, Reactive Maintenance SLA Compliance, Maintenance Work Order Backlog, Repeated Failures, Planned Maintenance Task Completion Rate, Maintenance Cost per Bed/sqm) assume a level of systematic data capture that may not exist uniformly. Some health services may use enterprise CMMS platforms (such as MEX, Maximo, or Pronto), others may use basic systems or spreadsheets, and some may rely on paper-based processes. Our approach addresses this through early data readiness assessment in Phase 1, pragmatic data collection workarounds for lower-maturity services, and a system-agnostic data requirements specification that defines what information is needed rather than prescribing which system must produce it.

Mitigation: Phase 1 Discovery includes a specific data readiness assessment for each pilot health service, cataloguing the CMMS platform in use and its current configuration. Phase 2 develops minimum viable data collection templates for services that cannot natively produce KPI data from existing systems. CBS will work directly with common CMMS platforms used across Victorian health services — including MEX (widely deployed in Australian health and government), IBM Maximo (used by larger health services and shared services arrangements), and Pronto Xi (common in regional health services) — to configure standard KPI extraction reports. For services using spreadsheet-based or manual systems, we will develop structured data collection workbooks with built-in validation and automated KPI calculation that can be progressively migrated to a CMMS as the service matures. Our approach does not require health services to change their systems — we configure reporting within whatever platform they currently use.

2. Organisational resistance and competing priorities. Health service maintenance teams are typically lean and focused on keeping facilities operational. KPI implementation may be perceived as an additional compliance burden imposed by head office, particularly where maintenance budgets have been under sustained pressure. Clinical priorities will always — and should always — take precedence over reporting activities.

Mitigation: Our co-design approach (Phase 3) involves health services in shaping how KPIs apply to their context, building ownership rather than imposing obligations. We design data capture to integrate with existing workflows rather than creating parallel processes. We frame KPIs as tools that strengthen the case for maintenance investment, not as surveillance mechanisms.

3. Normalisation and benchmarking validity. Meaningful benchmarking across 77 health services requires appropriate normalisation that accounts for facility type, age, complexity, service profile, and geographic context. Without sound normalisation, benchmarking will produce misleading comparisons that undermine confidence in the framework.

Mitigation: Phase 2 develops facility-type peer groups and normalisation methodologies that account for structural differences in cost drivers and performance profiles. Benchmarking is presented as a tool for identifying improvement opportunities, not as a league table.

4. Essential services and clinical risk. Health facility maintenance has direct patient safety implications that distinguish it from other infrastructure contexts. Asset failures in critical clinical systems (theatre ventilation, medical gases, backup power, sterilisation, nurse call) carry clinical risk that must be reflected in KPI prioritisation and escalation frameworks. Health services are also subject to specific Australian Standards governing essential services maintenance, including AS 1851 (Routine Service of Fire Protection Systems and Equipment), AS 2896 (Medical Gas Systems), and AS/NZS 3003 (Electrical Installations in Patient Care Areas), which impose mandatory maintenance frequencies and performance requirements that must be reflected in KPI measurement.

Mitigation: Our KPI implementation will align with essential services classifications, clinical risk categories, and applicable Australian Standards maintenance requirements. The Victorian Health Building Authority (VHBA), which plays a central role in health infrastructure planning and standards across the state, provides additional guidance on facility performance expectations that will inform our benchmarking and target-setting methodology. KPI measurement frameworks will be cross-referenced against mandatory maintenance obligations under AS 1851, AS 2896, and AS/NZS 3003 to ensure that essential services compliance is visible within the KPI reporting structure — enabling health services to demonstrate compliance performance alongside operational performance in a single framework.

5. Sustaining momentum beyond pilot. The transition from a supported pilot environment to self-sustaining sector-wide adoption is the highest-risk phase. Without ongoing support structures, early adoption gains are likely to decay.

Mitigation: Phase 5 establishes self-sustaining support mechanisms — peer networks, KPI champion training, resource libraries, and escalation pathways — designed to maintain momentum after the engagement concludes.

Phase 1: Discovery and Assessment (Weeks 1–4)

This phase establishes the evidence base for a targeted implementation. CBS will conduct a systematic assessment of each pilot health service's maintenance model, systems, processes, and data capabilities. The assessment is structured around four dimensions aligned to the Department's maturity model:

Organisational readiness — governance structures, management commitment, workforce skills and capacity, maintenance team structure and resourcing levels.

Operational readiness — maintenance management processes, planned versus reactive maintenance mix, work order management, contractor management arrangements.

Data capability — CMMS maturity (system type, configuration, data completeness), ability to extract KPI data from existing systems, data quality and consistency, reporting capability.

Resource availability — workforce capacity to support KPI implementation alongside operational duties, training needs, system upgrade requirements.

The assessment will be conducted through a combination of structured interviews with facilities managers and maintenance leads (60–90 minutes, on-site or video), system walkthroughs to understand data capture workflows, and document review (maintenance plans, asset registers, recent performance reports where available). We will use the Department's stakeholder details to plan engagement and will supplement this with our own stakeholder mapping where gaps are identified.

Key deliverables: Discovery Report documenting the current state across pilot health services; Stakeholder Engagement Plan; Data Readiness Assessment for each pilot service; and Risk Register with mitigation strategies.

Phase 2: Data Analysis and Benchmarking (Weeks 3–8)

Overlapping with late Phase 1 to maintain momentum, this phase gathers, validates, and analyses maintenance data from participating health services. The overlapping timeline reflects that data collection can commence for early-assessed services while discovery continues for later services.

CBS will benchmark current performance against the Department's 12 KPIs (focusing on Phase 1 KPIs initially) and relevant standards, including alignment with ISO 55001 (Asset Management) and ISO 41001 (Facility Management) where applicable. Where data permits, we will apply Reliability-Centred Maintenance (RCM) analysis principles — drawing on Jim Ellwood's Masters qualification in Maintenance and Reliability Engineering and his extensive experience delivering FMECA and RCM programmes across infrastructure, defence, and utilities sectors — to validate whether current maintenance task frequencies and strategies are appropriate for the health service's asset criticality profile. This analytical rigour ensures that KPI targets are grounded in engineering evidence, not arbitrary benchmarks. A critical output is the maturity segmentation — classifying health services into cohorts using assessment criteria mapped to the Department's 7-level model. The segmentation informs pilot group composition and tailored implementation pathways for each cohort.

For health services with limited data, we will establish minimum viable baselines using available information supplemented by structured estimation where necessary. This ensures every health service has a starting point for measurement, even if initial data is imperfect — recognising that perfect data is not a prerequisite for beginning to improve.

A critical challenge in benchmarking across the 77 health services is the diversity of building stock — from recently constructed facilities to buildings over 100 years old, with varying configurations, service profiles, and asset condition. CBS will normalise benchmarking data using a peer group methodology based on three dimensions: facility complexity (determined by service profile — tertiary/specialist, general acute, subacute/rehabilitation, community health), asset age band (grouped into post-2010, 1980–2010, pre-1980 cohorts reflecting distinct construction standards and asset lifecycles), and maintenance model (in-house, partially outsourced, fully outsourced). This segmentation produces meaningful peer groups where like is compared with like, preventing misleading benchmarks that penalise older facilities or reward lower-complexity services. Where a health service spans multiple building ages or configurations, we will benchmark at the building or precinct level where data permits, aggregating to the health service level for portfolio reporting.

Key deliverables: Benchmarking Report with performance baselines and peer group normalisation methodology; Maturity Segmentation Model mapping each pilot health service to the Department's 7-level maturity model; pilot group definition and selection rationale (proposing 6–10 health services spanning maturity cohorts and facility types); and Gap Analysis identifying priority improvement areas for each cohort.

Phase 3: Engagement and Co-Design (Weeks 6–12)

This phase is the critical bridge between analysis and implementation. CBS will facilitate stakeholder engagement through three structured mechanisms:

Co-design workshops (half-day, in-person where possible). Facilitated sessions with health service maintenance managers and key stakeholders, structured around the practical question: "How will these KPIs work in your facility?" Workshops are organised by maturity cohort to ensure peer relevance. A typical workshop follows this structure:

Sample half-day co-design workshop agenda (3.5 hours):
Session 1 (45 min) — Context setting: overview of the KPI framework purpose, what the Department needs, and how KPIs will support investment decisions and operational improvement. Facilitated discussion on current maintenance challenges — building rapport and identifying shared pain points within the cohort.
Session 2 (60 min) — KPI walk-through: practical review of Phase 1 KPIs using de-identified data examples from Phase 2 analysis. For each KPI: what it measures, what data is needed, where that data sits in their current systems, and what "good" looks like at their maturity level. Participants work through a sample KPI calculation using their own facility context.
Session 3 (45 min) — Data and systems: hands-on exercise identifying data sources and collection methods within participants' current systems (CMMS, spreadsheets, or manual). CBS presents draft reporting templates for group feedback and refinement. Focus on minimising additional workload — what can be automated versus what requires manual input.
Session 4 (45 min) — Target setting and action planning: co-design of realistic KPI targets for each maturity cohort. Participants identify their top three implementation priorities and potential barriers. CBS documents agreed actions, responsibilities, and timelines for each participant.

Participants include: health service facilities/maintenance manager, one maintenance team representative, and (where available) a finance or performance reporting contact. DH Asset Planning Unit staff attend as observers. CBS provides a facilitator (Nicole Caranguian) and a technical adviser (Jim Ellwood) for each workshop.

Structured interviews (45–60 minutes, on-site or video). Targeted one-on-one engagement with facilities managers who cannot attend workshops or who have specific implementation challenges requiring individual attention. These interviews capture granular insights about local barriers and innovations.

Online survey. A brief, targeted survey distributed to all 77 health services to capture baseline information about maintenance management systems, processes, and perceived barriers to KPI adoption. This extends engagement beyond the pilot group and provides portfolio-wide intelligence for the full rollout plan.

Key deliverables: Co-Design Outcomes Report; cohort-specific Implementation Action Plans (including KPI targets, data collection methods, reporting templates, training plans, and timelines); tailored KPI reporting templates and data collection guides for each maturity cohort; and Training Needs Analysis.

Phase 4: Pilot Implementation (Weeks 10–18)

CBS will implement the KPI framework with selected pilot health services (recommended 6–10 services spanning maturity cohorts and facility types: metropolitan, regional, specialist). The pilot is structured to test every element of the implementation approach in a supported environment before sector-wide deployment.

Pilot support includes: on-site or virtual working sessions with each pilot service to configure data collection, test reporting, and troubleshoot issues; fortnightly check-in calls with pilot service maintenance managers; a shared online workspace for pilot participants to exchange experiences and solutions; and CBS team availability for ad-hoc support as issues arise.

The pilot is monitored against defined success criteria including: ability to produce Phase 1 KPI reports using existing systems and configured processes; data quality and completeness levels; time investment required from maintenance staff (targeting less than 2 hours per month of additional effort); and maintenance team satisfaction with the implementation approach.

Key deliverables: Pilot Implementation Plan; pilot monitoring reports (fortnightly); Pilot Evaluation Report with quantitative and qualitative outcomes and lessons learned; and revised Implementation Action Plans incorporating pilot feedback.

Phase 5: Full Implementation and Continuous Improvement (Weeks 16–22)

Key activities include:

Finalised implementation framework — incorporating all pilot learnings, refined KPI reporting templates, data collection guides, training materials, and support resources packaged for deployment across all 77 health services.

Change management deployment — development and delivery of a communication and engagement strategy for sector-wide adoption (detailed in Section 3.5).

Self-sustaining support structures — establishment of a "KPI Champions" network (trained representatives from each health service or regional cluster who provide peer support and serve as first-line guidance for their colleagues); a resource library (hosted on the Department's existing platforms) containing all implementation tools, templates, guides, and FAQs; defined escalation pathways for issues that cannot be resolved at health service or peer network level; and a recommended ongoing review cycle (quarterly portfolio performance review, annual framework refresh).

3.6 Response to Criterion 5: Robust Change Management and Communication Planning

Implementing KPIs across 77 health services with diverse maturity levels is a change management challenge first and a technical one second. The technical KPI framework is well-developed in the Department's Performance Monitoring guidance. The implementation requires sustained behavioural and cultural change across organisations with competing clinical priorities and constrained maintenance resources.

3.6.1 Change Management Approach

CBS Group's change management approach draws on three lessons from our experience implementing performance frameworks across complex multi-stakeholder environments.

Principle 1: Co-ownership over compliance. KPIs that are imposed generate resistance; KPIs that are co-designed generate ownership. Our Phase 3 co-design process is not a consultation exercise — it is a collaborative design process where health service maintenance managers shape how the framework applies to their facility. Practically, this means: workshops are structured around the question "How will this work in your facility?" rather than "Here is what you must do"; health services choose which data collection method works best within their existing systems; KPI targets are negotiated based on current maturity, not imposed as universal benchmarks; and local innovations and workarounds are captured and shared across the portfolio.

Principle 2: Minimise burden, maximise value. Every element of the KPI implementation is designed to minimise additional administrative workload for maintenance teams. Specifically: data capture is integrated with existing maintenance management systems and workflows (not parallel reporting); for services with capable CMMS platforms, we configure automated KPI reports that require minimal manual intervention; for services with basic systems, we design structured data collection templates that require less than 2 hours per month of additional effort; and KPI data is framed as a tool that strengthens the business case for maintenance investment (helping facilities managers advocate for the resources they need), not as a top-down surveillance mechanism.

Principle 3: Visible early wins. Momentum comes from early wins. Our pilot implementation (Phase 4) is designed to generate visible, communicable wins that can be shared across the sector. By selecting pilot sites that span different maturity levels and facility types, we create relatable proof points for every cohort. CBS will produce a Pilot Outcomes Bulletin (2–4 pages) at the conclusion of Phase 4, co-authored with pilot health service facilities managers, documenting specific wins such as: a regional service discovering through MTTR analysis that a simple change to parts stocking eliminates 40% of repeat callouts; or a metropolitan service using backlog data to secure approval for an additional maintenance technician. This bulletin will be distributed by the Department to all 77 health services via existing communication channels (email distribution, intranet, and asset management circulars) and presented at the Phase 5 webinar briefings. Each win will be framed as "Health Service X used KPI Y to achieve Z" — concrete, relatable, and attributable.

Principle 4: Managing resistance constructively. Not all stakeholders will welcome external involvement in measuring their maintenance performance. CBS has extensive experience engaging resistant stakeholders — at TfNSW, introducing performance-based contracts with established asset managers required navigating organisational resistance from contractors who viewed KPI transparency as a threat to their commercial position. Our approach to resistant health service stakeholders follows a structured escalation pathway: first, listen and acknowledge — resistance often signals legitimate concerns about resource constraints, data quality, or fear of unfair comparison; second, reframe the value proposition — for a facilities manager under pressure, the most powerful argument for KPI adoption is that it strengthens their case for additional maintenance funding ("your data shows you need two more technicians, and now you have the evidence to make that case to your CEO"); third, adjust the approach — for health services where resistance is driven by real capacity constraints, we will work with the Department to agree a reduced-scope initial implementation (fewer KPIs, longer timeline) rather than forcing full compliance; and fourth, escalate only if necessary — if a health service remains resistant after sustained engagement, we will advise the Department on the appropriate escalation pathway, noting that our role is to support adoption, not to enforce compliance. CBS will identify stakeholder engagement risk early (Phase 1 assessment) and flag high-risk services to the Department for proactive management.

3.6.3 Change Management Risks

Beyond the technical delivery risks identified in Section 3.4.1, CBS has identified three change-specific risks that require distinct management strategies.

Change Risk 1: Health service disengagement during pilot. There is a risk that pilot health services initially committed to participation may lose momentum during the 8-week pilot, particularly if staff turnover, competing clinical priorities, or operational pressures reduce available maintenance team capacity. Mitigation: CBS will identify a named KPI Champion at each pilot site during Phase 3 (co-design) and establish a direct, regular relationship with that individual. If a pilot service shows signs of disengagement (missed data submissions, cancelled check-ins), CBS will escalate to the Department within one reporting cycle and propose remediation — which may include temporarily reducing the scope of KPI data collection or providing additional on-site support to get the service back on track.

Change Risk 2: Executive-level resistance to transparency. Health service CEOs or boards may resist KPI reporting if they perceive it as exposing poor maintenance performance to the Department, particularly if there is concern about funding implications or reputational comparison with other services. Mitigation: The executive communications strategy (developed in Phase 3) will explicitly frame KPIs as an evidence base for increased maintenance investment, not as a performance compliance mechanism. CBS will work with the Department to ensure that early communications from senior DH leadership reinforce this message. Benchmarking data will be presented to individual health services as "your performance relative to your cohort" rather than published league tables.

Change Risk 3: Data integrity manipulation. Once KPIs are implemented, there is a risk that some health services may adjust data entry practices to achieve favourable KPI results without genuine performance improvement — for example, closing work orders prematurely to reduce MTTR, or reclassifying reactive work as planned maintenance to improve planned maintenance completion rates. Mitigation: CBS will build data validation checks into the KPI reporting templates (cross-referencing related KPIs to detect anomalies — e.g., if MTTR improves dramatically but repeat failure rates remain high, this suggests premature work order closure rather than genuine improvement). The continuous improvement framework (Phase 5) will include periodic data quality audits as a standard component of the Department's ongoing portfolio oversight.

Pilot success stories packaged as case studies for sector-wide dissemination during Phase 5, showing practical value across different facility types and maturity levels.

3.6.4 Sustainability Beyond the Engagement

We have designed the approach to leave the Department and health services self-sufficient after the engagement concludes. This works through: full documentation of all implementation tools, templates, processes, and methodologies; a trained network of "KPI Champions" capable of providing first-line peer support and serving as the ongoing link between health services and the Department; an established community of practice with defined governance, communication channels, and meeting cadence; a continuous improvement framework with clear review cycles, escalation pathways, and a process for updating KPI targets as health services mature; and a transition plan for Phase 2 KPI implementation, building on the infrastructure established during this engagement.

KPI Champion identification and recruitment. We acknowledge that health service facilities managers and maintenance leads are already resource-constrained, and asking them to take on an additional "Champion" role requires a practical and realistic approach. CBS will identify suitable Champions during Phases 1 and 3 by looking for individuals who demonstrate: an interest in data-driven maintenance improvement (often the person already producing informal reports or advocating for system upgrades); the trust of their maintenance team (the Champion needs peer credibility, not seniority); and a baseline comfort with their CMMS or data tools. The Champion role is defined as a maximum of 2 hours per month — attendance at the quarterly community of practice forum, plus ad hoc peer support within their health service. This is not a formal appointment requiring management approval but a voluntary, peer-recognition role that confers professional development value. For health services where no suitable individual is identified during the engagement, the Department will serve as the direct support link until a Champion emerges through the community of practice network.

Community of practice transition. We acknowledge that self-sustaining professional networks typically require 12–18 months of active facilitation before they operate independently. A 5-month engagement cannot fully mature a community of practice, and we do not claim otherwise. Our approach establishes the infrastructure, governance, and initial momentum during the engagement, with the following staged transition: during the engagement (Months 1–5), CBS convenes and facilitates all community of practice activities, establishing the cadence, format, platform, and norms; in Months 6–9 (post-engagement), the Department's Asset Planning Unit takes the facilitation role using the materials, templates, and facilitator guides CBS has provided — CBS can be available for light-touch advisory support during this transition if the Department wishes to extend the engagement on a limited basis; and from Month 10 onwards, peer governance emerges, with rotating facilitation among experienced KPI Champions and the Department providing strategic oversight rather than operational facilitation. CBS will include a recommended governance charter and transition plan as a specific deliverable (within D17: Final Implementation Framework) to give the Department a clear roadmap for this transition.
