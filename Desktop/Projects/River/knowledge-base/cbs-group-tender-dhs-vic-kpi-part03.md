Executive Summary

Situation: 

The Victorian Department of Health oversees 77 public health services operating under maintenance models ranging from fully in-house to fully outsourced. This diversity has produced inconsistent asset performance, fragmented reporting, and limited ability to benchmark services, allocate capital effectively, or identify where clinical and safety risks are emerging.

Without standardised maintenance KPIs, the Department cannot systematically determine where building stock and facilities are deteriorating, where essential services infrastructure — HVAC, medical gases, backup power and nurse call systems — is at risk, or where maintenance teams lack adequate resources to maintain safe, reliable operations.

To address this, the Department has developed a comprehensive KPI framework comprising 12 KPIs across two implementation phases, supported by a 7-level maturity model (Level 0: Ad Hoc through Level 7: Transforming), as detailed in the September 2025 Maintenance Standards Guidelines Performance Monitoring document.

Complication: 

To realise the benefit of the Department’s KPI Framework, it must be ‘operationalised’ - from policy into operational practice across 77 organisations — each with different CMMS capabilities, workforce capacity, building ages, service profiles, and competing clinical priorities.

Challenge: How can the Department implement its KPI framework in a way that is practical, scalable, and enduring — achieving widespread adoption and value realisation for the services and the Department.   How can this benefit be realised while minimising the additional reporting burden on health service maintenance teams that are already operating under resource constraints?

At the other end, we understand the Department’s KPI framework, the problems it is designed to solve, and are well placed to ensure that these ambitions are met.

Worked Example — MTTR Implementation in a Regional Health Service

The following worked example illustrates how KPI 1 (Mean Time to Repair) would be implemented end-to-end in a hypothetical Level 2 maturity regional health service, showing CBS Group's practical approach.

Facility Profile: Goulburn Valley District Hospital (hypothetical). A 120-bed regional health service with a maintenance team of 3 FTE (1 supervisor, 2 technicians), supplemented by external contractors for specialist trades. The facility is approximately 45 years old with two major refurbishments. The maintenance team currently uses MEX (basic configuration) for work order management, with inconsistent data entry practices. Current maturity assessment: Level 2 (Emerging — some formal processes established but inconsistently applied).

Phase 1: Discovery (What We Find): During the site assessment, CBS identifies the following: MEX is installed but work orders are not consistently closed out with completion timestamps; approximately 60% of reactive work orders have both a "reported" and "completed" timestamp, while 40% are missing one or both; no distinction is made between asset criticality levels in work order priority; and the maintenance supervisor tracks urgent clinical equipment failures mentally but does not record response times systematically.

Phase 2: Data Analysis (What We Learn): From the 60% of work orders with usable timestamps, CBS extracts 12 months of MTTR data and calculates: overall MTTR across all assets of approximately 14.2 hours; however, this figure masks significant variation — general building maintenance MTTR is approximately 22 hours (acceptable), while essential services MTTR is approximately 4.8 hours (needs validation against AS 1851 and clinical requirements). CBS also identifies that 35% of reactive work orders are repeat failures on the same assets, suggesting underlying reliability issues that Repeated Failures (KPI 4), Planned Maintenance Task Completion Rate (KPI 5), and the ensuing root cause analyses will help address.

Phase 3: Co-Design (What We Agree): In the co-design workshop (attended with other Level 2 cohort services), CBS works with the maintenance supervisor to establish the following: MTTR will be measured in three tiers aligned to asset criticality — Critical (essential services: theatre ventilation, medical gases, backup power, fire systems, nurse call) with a target MTTR of less than 2 hours; High (clinical environment: ward HVAC, hot water, lifts, sterilisation) with an example target MTTR of less than 8 hours; and Standard (non-clinical: general lighting, office HVAC, grounds) with a target MTTR of less than 48 hours.

To capture the data, CBS configures two changes in MEX: a mandatory "Asset Criticality" field on the work order form (pre-populated from the asset register with a dropdown of Critical/High/Standard); and a workflow rule that prevents work order closure without both "Reported" and "Completed" timestamps. These changes require approximately 2 hours of MEX configuration and 30 minutes of training for each technician.

Phase 4: Pilot (What Happens): During the 8-week pilot: the data capture rate improves from 60% to 92% within 4 weeks (the mandatory fields make it easier to comply than to skip); the maintenance supervisor reports that the criticality tiering "makes sense" and reflects how the team already prioritises informally; the fortnightly check-in identifies that the "Completed" timestamp is being entered when the technician finishes work, not when the asset is returned to service — CBS adjusts the field label to "Asset Returned to Service" to capture the correct endpoint; and the additional data entry burden is measured at approximately 3 minutes per work order, equating to approximately 45 minutes per week — well within the 2-hour monthly target.

Phase 5: Outcomes: After the pilot, the health service has: a functioning MTTR measurement system integrated into existing MEX workflows; baseline MTTR data segmented by asset criticality, enabling meaningful performance tracking; visibility of the repeat-failure pattern (feeding into KPI 4: Repeated Failures), which the maintenance supervisor uses to build a business case for targeted asset replacement; and a practical, sustainable reporting process that adds minimal burden to the maintenance team.

The Department has: comparable MTTR data from this service that can be benchmarked against other Level 2 cohort services; visibility of essential services response performance against clinical risk thresholds; and evidence of improvement trajectory that informs both the service's maturity advancement and the Department's capital allocation decisions.

Worked Example — OEE Adaptation for Health Essential Services

Overall Equipment Effectiveness (OEE) is a Phase 2 KPI that requires adaptation for health infrastructure. In manufacturing, OEE = Availability × Performance × Quality. In a health context, CBS adapts this as Essential Services Effectiveness (ESE):

ESE = Availability × Performance × Compliance

Availability measures the proportion of time essential services systems are operational and available for clinical use — calculated as (Total scheduled hours minus unplanned downtime hours) divided by Total scheduled hours. For a hospital operating theatre ventilation system scheduled 24/7, 8 hours of unplanned downtime in a month (720 hours) yields an Availability of 98.9%.

Performance measures whether the system delivers to its design specification when operational — for example, whether the HVAC system maintains the required temperature and pressure differentials in a theatre environment, or whether the medical gas system delivers at the required pressure and flow rates. This is measured through periodic performance testing against AS 1851 (fire systems), AS 2896 (medical gas), or design specifications.

Compliance replaces the manufacturing "Quality" component and measures the proportion of scheduled maintenance tasks completed to specification and on time — directly linking to KPI 5 (Planned Maintenance Task Completion Rate) and mandatory maintenance frequencies under the relevant Australian Standards.

Practical implementation: For a Level 3 (Developing) health service with basic CMMS capability, CBS would initially measure ESE for the top 5 essential services systems only (backup power, fire detection and suppression, medical gases, theatre ventilation, and nurse call). Data sources include: CMMS downtime logs for Availability, existing essential services testing records for Performance, and planned maintenance completion records for Compliance. This focused approach provides a meaningful ESE measure without requiring data across all building systems — the scope can be expanded as the health service matures.

Phase 1: Discovery and Assessment (Weeks 1–4)

This phase establishes the evidence base for a targeted implementation. Our approach is designed to engage with, and rapidly build a clear, evidence-based understanding of the current maintenance environment across the 77 Victorian health services. 

CBS will conduct a systematic assessment of each health service's maintenance model, systems, processes, and data capabilities. The assessment is structured around four dimensions aligned to the Department's maturity model:

Leveraging the Department's stakeholder details to plan engagement, we will conduct targeted engagement with operational leads, engineering managers and asset management teams to validate findings and ensure the local context is fully understood.

The initial assessment will be conducted through a combination of structured interviews with facilities managers and maintenance leads (60–90 minutes, on-site or video), and a self-assessment questionnaire, followed up with system walkthroughs to understand data capture workflows, and document review (maintenance plans, asset registers, recent performance reports where available).

Key deliverables: Discovery Report documenting the current state across the 77 health services; Stakeholder Engagement& Communications Plan; Data Readiness Assessment for each pilot service; and Risk Register with mitigation strategies.

Phase 2: Data Analysis and Benchmarking (Weeks 3-8)

Overlapping with late Phase 1 to maintain momentum, this phase gathers, validates, and analyses maintenance data from participating health services. The overlapping timeline reflects that data collection can commence for early-assessed services while discovery continues for later services. Our team will undertake rigorous data validation and cleansing to ensure accuracy and comparability across health services.

CBS will benchmark current performance against the Department's six (6) Foundational KPIs and relevant standards, including alignment with ISO 55001 (Asset Management) and ISO 41001 (Facility Management) where applicable. This analytical rigour ensures that KPI targets are grounded in engineering evidence, not arbitrary benchmarks. A critical output is the maturity segmentation — classifying health services into cohorts using assessment criteria mapped to the Department's 7-level Asset Management Maturity model. This segmentation, combined with the organisational and operational readiness information gathered in Phase 1 will provide the Department meaningful information to determine the ongoing pilot group composition, ensuring the pilot group is representative, scalable and positioned to generate meaningful insights for sector wide implementation.

For health services with limited data, we will establish minimum viable baselines using available information supplemented by structured estimation where necessary. This ensures every health service has a starting point for measurement, even if initial data is imperfect — recognising that perfect data is not a prerequisite for beginning to improve.

A critical challenge in benchmarking across the 77 health services is the diversity of building stock — from recently constructed facilities to buildings over 100 years old, with varying configurations, service profiles, and asset condition. CBS will normalise benchmarking data using a peer group methodology based on three dimensions: facility complexity (determined by service profile — tertiary/specialist, general acute, subacute/rehabilitation, community health), asset age band (grouped into post-2010, 1980–2010, pre-1980 cohorts reflecting distinct construction standards and asset lifecycles), and maintenance model (in-house, partially outsourced, fully outsourced). This segmentation produces meaningful peer groups where like is compared with like, preventing misleading benchmarks that penalise older facilities or reward lower-complexity services. Where a health service spans multiple building ages or configurations, we will benchmark at the building or precinct level where data permits, aggregating to the health service level for portfolio reporting.

Key deliverables: Benchmarking Report with performance baselines and peer group normalisation methodology; Maturity Segmentation Model mapping each pilot health service to the Department's 7-level maturity model; pilot group definition and selection rationale (proposing 15 health services spanning maturity, facility types and, where possible, equitable metro/regional representation); and Gap Analysis identifying priority improvement areas for each cohort – Foundational, Established and Advanced.

Phase 3: Engagement and Co-Design (Weeks 6-12)

This phase is the critical bridge between analysis and implementation, focusing on direct engagement and collaborative design to ensure the KPI framework is practical, adoptable and aligned with operational realities. Our codesign approach ensures that health services feel ownership of the solution, that implementation is grounded in real world practice, will provide enhanced visibility and accountability, provide data driven decision making and add ongoing value to their service through the benefits realisation.

CBS will facilitate stakeholder engagement and co-design through the following structured mechanisms:

Codesign workshops (2-2.5 hours) –with each service including executive stakeholders, asset / facility managers, key engineering and maintenance team members and other key stakeholders, structured around the practical question: "How will these KPIs work in your facility?".  The workshops will incorporate:

Context setting: overview of the KPI framework purpose, what the Department needs, and how KPIs will support investment decisions and operational improvement. Facilitated discussion on current maintenance challenges ie local barriers and innovations — building rapport and identifying shared pain points within the team.

KPI walk-through: practical review of Phase 1 KPIs using de-identified data examples from Phase 2 analysis. For each KPI: what it measures, what data is needed, where that data sits in their current systems, and what "good" looks like at their maturity level. Participants work through a sample KPI calculation using their own facility context.

Data and systems: hands-on exercise identifying data sources and collection methods within participants' current systems (CMMS, spreadsheets, or manual). CBS presents draft reporting templates for group feedback and refinement. Focus on minimising additional workload — what can be automated versus what requires manual input.

Target setting and action planning: co-design of realistic KPI targets for each maturity cohort. Participants identify their top three implementation priorities and potential barriers. CBS documents agreed actions, responsibilities, and timelines for each participant.

Site visits: Targeted one-on-one engagement with asset/facilities managers who have specific implementation challenges or complexities requiring additional investigation and understanding.

Follow up virtual / telephone calls:  for clarification or where additional information may be required.

Key deliverables: Co-Design Outcomes Report; cohort-specific Implementation Action Plans (including KPI targets, data collection methods, reporting templates, training plans, and timelines); tailored KPI reporting templates and data collection guides for each maturity cohort; and Training Needs Analysis.

Phase 5: Full Implementation and Continuous Improvement (Weeks 16–22)

Incorporating pilot feedback, CBS will finalise the implementation framework for sector-wide adoption. This phase addresses the critical transition from supported pilot to self-sustaining sector-wide operation.

Key activities include:

Finalised implementation framework — incorporating all pilot learnings, refined KPI implementation and reporting templates, data collection guides, training materials, and support resources packaged for deployment across all 77 health services.

Change management deployment — development and delivery of a communication and engagement strategy for sector-wide adoption (detailed in Section 3.5).

Self-sustaining support structures — establishment of a "KPI Champions" network (trained representatives from each health service or regional cluster who provide peer support and serve as first-line guidance for their colleagues); a resource library (hosted on the Department's existing platforms) containing all implementation tools, templates, guides, and FAQs; defined escalation pathways for issues that cannot be resolved at health service or peer network level; and a recommended ongoing review cycle (quarterly portfolio performance review, annual framework refresh).

Key deliverables: Final Implementation Framework; Change Management and Communication Strategy; Continuous Improvement Framework; sector-wide KPI reporting templates, data collection guides, and training materials; KPI Champions training package and network establishment; and Project Close-Out Report with recommendations for sustained adoption.

Key lessons from the TfNSW implementation directly informed the methodology we propose for the Department:

Early in the TfNSW engagement, we designed a performance reporting framework that was technically rigorous but required asset managers to produce data their systems could not readily generate. The result was manual data extraction, delayed reporting, and compliance-driven rather than value-driven engagement. We redesigned the framework to start with what existing systems could produce, then progressively built data capture capability over 12 months. This lesson directly shapes our "system-agnostic" approach for health services — start with what the CMMS can deliver today, then build. 

We initially set uniform KPI targets across all asset managers regardless of their operational maturity. Higher-maturity managers saw the targets as unambitious; lower-maturity managers saw them as unachievable. Both disengaged. We introduced maturity-calibrated targets that challenged every manager at their level while providing a clear advancement pathway. This lesson is embedded in our proposed maturity-based segmentation and cohort-specific target-setting approach for health services.

We found that KPI data collection and reporting waned when staff could not see a direct line of sight between the KPI and improved operational outcomes.  Where outcomes were not linked, the measures were perceived as additional administrative burden rather than performance enablers. We refined the framework to explicitly link each KPI to tangible service, financial or risk outcomes, and where possible, to surface quick wins that demonstrated value early. We also identified owners for the outcomes, and therefore, the KPIs; ensuring the KPIs are “championed” and implementation is sustained over the long term. This lesson is embedded in our approach to KPI design and rollout for health services, ensuring that every measure is outcome-anchored, decision-useful and demonstrably beneficial to those accountable for delivery, thereby sustaining implementation beyond compliance.

Understanding of Key Issues and Risks

CBS Group has identified the following key issues and risks based on our analysis of the RFP, the Department's Performance Monitoring guidance document, and our experience implementing KPI frameworks across complex multi-stakeholder environments.

Data quality, availability, and system diversity. Health services at lower maturity levels are likely to have incomplete, inconsistent, or inaccessible maintenance data. Phase 1 KPIs (MTTR, Reactive Maintenance SLA Compliance, Maintenance Work Order Backlog, Repeated Failures, Planned Maintenance Task Completion Rate, Maintenance Cost per Bed/sqm) assume a level of systematic data capture that may not exist uniformly. Some health services may use enterprise CMMS platforms (such as MEX, Maximo, or Pronto), others may use basic systems or spreadsheets, and some may rely on paper-based processes. 

Our approach addresses this through early data readiness assessment in Phase 1, pragmatic data collection workarounds for lower-maturity services, and a system-agnostic data requirements specification that defines what information is needed rather than prescribing which system must produce it.

Mitigation: Phase 1 Discovery includes a specific data readiness assessment for each pilot health service, cataloguing the CMMS platform in use and its current configuration. Phase 2 develops minimum viable data collection templates for services that cannot natively produce KPI data from existing systems. CBS will work directly with common CMMS platforms used across Victorian health services — including MEX (widely deployed in Australian health and government), IBM Maximo (used by larger health services and shared services arrangements), and Pronto Xi (common in regional health services) — to configure standard KPI extraction reports. 

For services using spreadsheet-based or manual systems, we will develop structured data collection workbooks with built-in validation and automated KPI calculation that can be progressively migrated to a CMMS as the service matures. Our approach does not require health services to change their systems — we configure reporting within whatever platform they currently use.

Organisational resistance and competing priorities. Health service maintenance teams are typically lean and focused on keeping facilities operational. KPI implementation may be perceived as an additional compliance burden imposed by head office, particularly where maintenance budgets have been under sustained pressure. Clinical priorities will always — and should always — take precedence over reporting activities.

Mitigation: Our co-design approach (Phase 3) involves health services in shaping how KPIs apply to their context, building ownership rather than imposing obligations. We design data capture to integrate with existing workflows rather than creating parallel processes. We frame KPIs as tools that strengthen the case for maintenance investment, not as surveillance mechanisms.

Normalisation and benchmarking validity. Meaningful benchmarking across 77 health services requires appropriate normalisation that accounts for facility type, age, complexity, service profile, and geographic context. Without sound normalisation, benchmarking will produce misleading comparisons that undermine confidence in the framework.

Mitigation: Phase 2 develops facility-type peer groups and normalisation methodologies that account for structural differences in cost drivers and performance profiles. Benchmarking is presented as a tool for identifying improvement opportunities, not as a league table.

Essential services and clinical risk. Health facility maintenance has direct patient safety implications that distinguish it from other infrastructure contexts. Asset failures in critical clinical systems (theatre ventilation, medical gases, backup power, sterilisation, nurse call) carry clinical risk that must be reflected in KPI prioritisation and escalation frameworks. 

Health services are also subject to specific Australian Standards governing essential services maintenance, including AS 1851 (Routine Service of Fire Protection Systems and Equipment), AS 2896 (Medical Gas Systems), and AS/NZS 3003 (Electrical Installations in Patient Care Areas), which impose mandatory maintenance frequencies and performance requirements that must be reflected in KPI measurement.

Mitigation: Our KPI implementation will align with essential services classifications, clinical risk categories, and applicable Australian Standards maintenance requirements. The Victorian Health Building Authority (VHBA), which plays a central role in health infrastructure planning and standards across the state, provides additional guidance on facility performance expectations that will inform our benchmarking and target-setting methodology. 

KPI measurement frameworks will be cross-referenced against mandatory maintenance obligations under AS 1851, AS 2896, and AS/NZS 3003 to ensure that essential services compliance is visible within the KPI reporting structure — enabling health services to demonstrate compliance performance alongside operational performance in a single framework.

Sustaining momentum beyond pilot. The transition from a supported pilot environment to self-sustaining sector-wide adoption is the highest-risk phase. Without ongoing support structures, early adoption gains are likely to decay.

Mitigation: Phase 5 establishes self-sustaining support mechanisms — peer networks, KPI champion training, resource libraries, and escalation pathways — designed to maintain momentum after the engagement concludes.

Maturity-Based Segmentation and Tailored Support

The Department's Performance Monitoring guidance establishes a 7-level maturity model progressing from Level 0 (Ad Hoc — reactive, unplanned maintenance with no formal processes) through to Level 7 (Transforming — maintenance function drives organisational strategy with predictive analytics and innovation). CBS Group has extensive experience in applying maturity-based segmentation to calibrate implementation approaches.

Our segmentation methodology integrates two dimensions from the Department's framework:

Dimension 1: Asset Management Maturity Level (Levels 0–7). During Phase 2, CBS will assess each health service against the Department's maturity model using the dual-dimension scoring methodology specified in Attachment 3 — evaluating both System Status (how well the asset management system is documented, structured, and embedded) and Effectiveness of Application (how consistently the system is applied and delivering outcomes), with the final maturity level being the lower of the two scores. This ensures maturity assessments are rigorous and reflect actual practice, not just documentation.

Dimension 2: ESS Category (Category 1, 2, 3). The Department classifies health services by Essential Services category based on infrastructure scale, service scope, and operational complexity. As outlined in the Department's target calibration framework (Attachment 3, Section 4.4), ESS Category directly influences realistic KPI target-setting: Category 1 facilities (major metropolitan hospitals) are generally positioned to begin at higher maturity levels and may already be capable of meeting intermediate targets in the short term; Category 2 facilities may start at mid-range maturity with targets evolving from basic to advanced; and Category 3 facilities may begin at Level 0 with initial targets focused on foundational practices. Our segmentation will cross-reference maturity level with ESS Category to produce a two-dimensional cohort classification that determines both implementation support intensity and target ambition.

Minimum maturity level alignment. Each KPI has a defined minimum maturity level for implementation (specified in the Department's KPI Dictionary). Our pilot selection and rollout sequencing will respect these thresholds: Level 0 minimum KPIs (MTTR, Cost per Bed/sqm, PMP, Major Failures) are applicable to all health services and form the core of Phase 1 rollout; Level 1 minimum KPIs (Asset Register Completeness) require at least basic system use; Level 3 minimum KPIs (Work Order Backlog, Repeated Failures, Planned Task Completion, OEE) require defined processes and standardised systems; and Level 5 minimum KPIs (RCA Effectiveness) are appropriate only for advanced services with optimised processes.

For this engagement, we propose segmenting the 77 health services into three to four maturity cohorts during Phase 2, using the dual-dimension assessment described above. The segmentation will be based on genuine capability (data systems, workforce skills, process maturity, asset register completeness) rather than proxies such as health service size, budget, or metropolitan/regional location. A 120-bed regional hospital with a skilled facilities manager running a well-configured CMMS may be at a higher maturity level than a metropolitan tertiary hospital with a larger but less structured maintenance operation.

Each cohort receives a differentiated implementation pathway aligned to the Department's target calibration framework (Attachment 3, Tables 4–6):

Foundational cohort (Levels 0–2, typically Cat 2/3 facilities): Health services in this cohort are likely operating with minimal formal maintenance management systems — potentially using spreadsheets, paper-based work orders, or basic systems with incomplete data. The implementation focus for these services is on establishing the basic data capture infrastructure needed to report against Level 0 minimum KPIs (MTTR, Cost per Bed/sqm, PMP, Major Failures) and progressing toward Level 1 and Level 3 KPIs as systems mature. Support will be intensive and hands-on, including on-site assistance to configure or establish minimum viable data capture processes, pragmatic workarounds for systems that cannot natively produce KPI data (such as structured manual collection templates), and training focused on fundamental maintenance management practices. KPI targets will be calibrated as "Basic to Intermediate" per the Department's target ambition framework, with a focus on establishing visibility and baseline measurement rather than achieving portfolio benchmarks.

Developing cohort (Levels 3–4, typically Cat 1/2 facilities): These health services have functioning maintenance management systems and processes. All Phase 1 and most Phase 2 KPIs are applicable at this maturity level. The implementation focus is on connecting existing data to the KPI framework, automating where possible, establishing formal target tracking, and building analytical skills. Support includes targeted system configuration assistance, data quality improvement workshops, and coaching on using KPI data for decision-making rather than just reporting. Targets will be calibrated as "Intermediate to Advanced" with formal benchmarking against peer services.

Advanced cohort (Levels 5–7, typically Cat 1 facilities): These health services have mature asset management practices and strong data systems. All 12 KPIs are applicable, including RCA Effectiveness (Level 5 minimum). The implementation focus is on optimisation — refining KPI measurement, external benchmarking against best practice, and extending to advanced analytics and combined KPI insights. Support is lighter touch, focused on peer leadership (these services can mentor foundational cohort services), continuous improvement, and stretch targets calibrated as "Advanced to Transformational" per the Department's framework.

Engagement with Specific KPIs — Health Infrastructure Context

CBS Group recognises that the Department's 12 KPIs require careful contextualisation for health infrastructure. The following examples show our understanding of the specific implementation challenges:

KPI 6: Maintenance Cost per Bed or Square Metre. This normalisation metric presents particular challenges across the health service portfolio because cost drivers vary fundamentally by facility type. A metropolitan tertiary hospital with operating theatres, intensive care units, and specialised medical equipment has structurally different maintenance cost profiles from a community health centre or a rural sub-acute facility. Our benchmarking approach will establish peer groups based on facility function and complexity, not just size, ensuring that benchmarking produces meaningful comparisons rather than misleading rankings. We will work with the Department to define the normalisation methodology during Phase 2, drawing on our experience in normalising lifecycle costs across transport assets with different operating profiles.

KPI 9: Overall Equipment Effectiveness (OEE). This Phase 2 KPI originates from manufacturing contexts where "availability x performance x quality" has clear quantitative definitions. Its application in health facilities requires careful adaptation. In a hospital context, we propose defining OEE components as: availability (proportion of time critical assets are operational and available for clinical use), performance (whether assets are operating at their designed capacity and specification), and quality (whether asset performance meets clinical and regulatory requirements — such as theatre ventilation air change rates, medical gas purity, and temperature control within specified ranges). This adaptation ensures OEE captures meaningful asset performance information without forcing health services into an artificial manufacturing paradigm.

KPI 1: Mean Time to Repair (MTTR). In health facilities, MTTR has direct clinical risk implications that differ by asset criticality classification. A failure in a general office HVAC system has a different urgency profile from a failure in theatre ventilation or backup power. Our implementation will align MTTR measurement with essential services classifications and clinical risk categories, ensuring the KPI captures the information that matters most for patient safety and service continuity.
