2 HYDROFOIL TECHNOLOGY OVERVIEW

2.1 Fundamental Hydrofoil Principles
Hydrofoil technology represents a revolutionary advancement in marine propulsion, utilising underwater wings
(foils) to lift the vessel hull above the water surface during operation. This fundamental design principle delivers
transformative improvements in speed, efficiency, and environmental impact compared to conventional
displacement or planing hull vessels.

The physics of hydrofoil operation centres on the generation of hydrodynamic lift through carefully designed
underwater wing surfaces. As the vessel accelerates, water flow over the hydrofoil creates lift forces that
progressively raise the hull above the water surface. Once "on foil," the vessel experiences dramatically reduced
drag, enabling higher speeds with significantly lower energy consumption.

This principle, successfully demonstrated in racing yachts and military applications, has been refined for
commercial passenger transport through advanced materials, control systems, and electric propulsion
integration.

The Water Roads implementation leverages two complementary hydrofoil technologies from global leaders
Candela and Artemis Technologies. These manufacturers have pioneered the integration of hydrofoil design
with electric propulsion systems, creating vessels specifically optimised for urban water transport applications.

The combination of proven hydrofoil principles with cutting-edge electric propulsion and advanced control
systems delivers unprecedented performance in speed, efficiency, comfort, and environmental impact.

2.3 Competitive Technology Analysis
•    Conventional Ferry Comparison:

Conventional Ferry Comparison:
Traditional ferry systems utilise displacement or semi-displacement hull designs that push through water rather
than rising above it. This fundamental design limitation creates significant drag forces that increase
exponentially with speed, requiring substantial engine power and fuel consumption to achieve modest speed
improvements. Conventional ferries typically operate at 15-20 knots maximum speed, with energy
consumption rates of 150-200 kWh per nautical mile for passenger capacities comparable to hydrofoil vessels.

The hydrofoil advantage becomes pronounced at higher speeds, where conventional vessels experience
dramatic increases in energy consumption and operational costs. While conventional ferries may achieve
acceptable efficiency at low speeds, their performance degrades rapidly as speed increases. Hydrofoil vessels
maintain consistent efficiency across their operational speed range, enabling sustained high-speed operation
with lower energy consumption than conventional vessels operating at significantly lower speeds.




•    Alternative High-Speed Marine Technologies:

Alternative High-Speed Marine Technologies:
Several alternative technologies compete in the high-speed marine transport market, including air-cushion
vehicles (hovercraft), surface-effect ships, and advanced catamaran designs. Each technology presents distinct
advantages and limitations compared to hydrofoil systems.

Hovercraft technology enables operation over various surfaces but requires significant energy for air cushion
maintenance, resulting in higher operational costs and noise levels. Surface-effect ships achieve high speeds
but require calm water conditions and specialised infrastructure. Advanced catamarans deliver improved
stability and passenger comfort but cannot match hydrofoil efficiency at high speeds.

Hydrofoil technology offers the optimal balance of speed, efficiency, environmental impact, and operational
flexibility for urban water transport applications. The ability to operate in various sea conditions while
maintaining high speed and low energy consumption positions hydrofoil technology as the preferred solution
for metro-style water transport services.
