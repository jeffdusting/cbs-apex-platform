# CBS Value-Based Pricing Model for NSW Government
## Commercial Framework for Performance-Based Engagement

### Executive Summary

**Insight**: Traditional fee-for-service procurement models create misaligned incentives, with suppliers rewarded for time spent rather than value delivered. CBS Group's implementation of outcome-based commercial models has demonstrated that aligning payment with measurable value creation delivers superior results for government.

**Market Context**: NSW Government faces a $10+ billion annual infrastructure maintenance liability with constrained budgets. Current procurement approaches typically achieve only 60-70% of potential value, leaving significant efficiency gains unrealised across the State's asset portfolio.

**Problem Definition**: Agencies require specialist expertise to optimise asset performance and reduce whole-of-life costs, but traditional procurement models provide no incentive for suppliers to minimise costs or accelerate delivery, creating a fundamental commercial misalignment.

**Approach**: The CBS Value-Based Pricing Model addresses this through a two-component structure: base payments at public service rates for service delivery, plus pre-agreed sharing of verified savings. This creates powerful incentives for innovation while protecting agencies from risk.

**Core Findings**: Implementation of this model through the CAPITAL framework has delivered $180 million in verified savings on the Western Harbour Tunnel project alone, demonstrating 30% efficiency improvements are achievable when commercial incentives align with government objectives.

**Takeaway**: Agencies can achieve significantly better infrastructure outcomes within existing budgets by adopting value-based commercial models that reward performance rather than presence.

## Part A: Model Framework

### 1. Model Overview and Principles

#### 1.1 Core Structure
The CBS Value-Based Pricing Model operates through two integrated payment components:
- **Base Service Delivery**: Monthly payments for CBS staff at public service equivalent rates
- **Performance Component**: Pre-agreed percentage of verified savings, paid within 30 days of confirmation

#### 1.2 Foundational Principles
The model is built on three key principles:
- Value must be quantifiable through agreed metrics and independently verifiable against baselines
- Risk allocation places accountability with the party best positioned to manage each risk
- Payment for performance occurs only after value is confirmed and documented

### 2. Value Definition and Measurement Framework

#### 2.1 Financial Value Categories
Financial value is captured through the following measurable outcomes:
- Capital expenditure reductions through design optimisation and value engineering
- Operating expenditure savings from process improvements and efficiency gains
- Whole-of-life cost reductions through strategic asset management
- Schedule acceleration benefits quantified through time-value calculations
- Risk mitigation savings from avoided costs and reduced contingencies

#### 2.2 Strategic Value Categories (Catalyst Framework Aligned)
Strategic value encompasses broader government objectives:
- Regulatory compliance value including emissions reductions and safety improvements
- Community benefit improvements measured through established frameworks
- Environmental outcomes supporting Net Zero and sustainability targets
- Organisational capability development and knowledge transfer
- Innovation adoption enabling future transformations

#### 2.3 Measurement Methodology
Value measurement follows a structured approach:
- **Baseline Establishment**: Current performance documented using historical data, approved business cases, or industry benchmarks
- **KPI Definition**: Specific, measurable indicators agreed during procurement
- **Performance Tracking**: Regular monitoring against established baselines
- **Verification Process**: Independent validation of claimed savings
- **Value Calculation**: Application of pre-agreed formulas to determine payments

### 3. Risk and Reward Allocation

#### 3.1 Agency Risk Profile
The agency maintains a protected position through:
- Payment obligations limited to standard public service rates for base services
- No success fee exposure without independently verified savings
- Costs capped at traditional delivery model equivalents
- Retention of all decision-making authority

#### 3.2 CBS Risk Profile
CBS accepts performance risk including:
- Investment in innovation without guaranteed return beyond base rates
- Resource deployment risk with payment contingent on results
- Timing risk with value share dependent on successful realisation
- Reputational risk linked to achievement of promised outcomes

#### 3.3 Balanced Incentive Structure
This allocation creates optimal outcomes where:
- Both parties benefit from successful value creation
- Innovation is rewarded while failure is not subsidised
- Long-term thinking is encouraged over short-term gains
- Collaboration replaces adversarial contractor relationships

## Part B: Implementation Framework

### 4. Procurement Pathways

#### 4.1 Competitive Procurement Option
The model can be implemented through standard competitive processes:
- Open tender with value-based pricing as evaluation criterion
- Prequalification based on demonstrated capability for outcome delivery
- Two-stage procurement separating technical and commercial proposals
- Panel arrangements enabling rapid deployment across multiple projects

#### 4.2 Sole Source Procurement Pathway
Direct negotiation may be justified in specific circumstances under section 55(3) of the Public Works and Procurement Act 1912:

**Justification Criteria** include:
- Unique capability through proprietary methodologies (CAPITAL framework)
- Continuity requirements where change would compromise outcomes
- Innovation pilot under Procurement Board Direction 2016-05
- Time-critical initiatives where competitive process would cause unacceptable delay

**Approval Requirements** vary by value:
- Under $1 million: Agency CEO with documented justification
- $1-10 million: Portfolio Minister approval with supporting business case
- Over $10 million: Cabinet approval following Treasury review

**Compliance Steps** must be followed:
- Strategic justification linking to agency plans and government priorities
- Market assessment confirming limited supplier availability
- Value demonstration through comparative analysis
- Governance establishment including probity advisor where appropriate
- Formal approval through required delegation levels

### 5. Contract Implementation Process

#### 5.1 Establishment Phase
The establishment phase includes the following activities:
- Baseline documentation of current costs and performance levels
- Opportunity identification for value improvement initiatives
- KPI development with clear measurement criteria
- Value share negotiation based on risk and opportunity
- Team formation including agency-nominated staff for knowledge transfer

#### 5.2 Delivery Phase
Ongoing delivery encompasses:
- Monthly service provision at agreed resource levels
- Performance tracking against established KPIs
- Value initiative implementation with regular reporting
- Knowledge transfer to embedded agency staff
- Continuous improvement based on performance data

#### 5.3 Verification and Payment Phase
Value realisation follows a structured process:
- Savings calculation against documented baselines
- Independent validation through agreed procedures
- Verification documentation preparation
- Steering committee review and approval
- Payment processing within 30 days of confirmation

### 6. Governance and Oversight

#### 6.1 Steering Committee Structure
Joint oversight is provided through:
- **Agency Representatives**: Executive Director (Chair), Project Director, Finance Representative
- **CBS Representatives**: Engagement Director, Technical Lead, Commercial Manager
- **Independent Members**: Probity Advisor and Technical Expert as required

#### 6.2 Operating Rhythm
Regular governance activities include:
- Monthly operational reviews of progress and performance
- Quarterly strategic assessments of value realisation
- Annual model effectiveness reviews
- Ad-hoc meetings for issue resolution

#### 6.3 Decision Framework
Clear decision rights ensure accountability:
- Agency retains all operational and strategic decision authority
- CBS provides recommendations based on analysis
- Value verification requires steering committee approval
- Disputes escalate through defined resolution process

## Part C: Commercial and Compliance

### 7. Financial Parameters

#### 7.1 Payment Structure
The payment framework includes:
- Base fees at public service equivalent rates paid monthly
- Performance payments calculated as pre-agreed percentage of verified savings
- Engagement caps limiting total payments to 200% of base service fees
- Option for deferred payment structures aligning with budget cycles

#### 7.2 Value Share Ranges
Typical value share percentages depend on:
- Risk profile of specific initiatives
- Level of CBS investment required
- Magnitude of potential savings
- Implementation complexity
- Market precedents (typically 20-50% of verified savings)

### 8. NSW Procurement Policy Alignment

#### 8.1 Value for Money Compliance
The model directly supports value for money objectives by:
- Optimising whole-of-life costs beyond simple lowest price
- Balancing financial and non-financial evaluation factors
- Delivering measurable benefits exceeding traditional approaches
- Providing transparent value calculations

#### 8.2 Policy Objectives Supported
Implementation advances key government priorities:
- **Innovation**: Rewards creative problem-solving and new approaches
- **Economic Development**: Builds local capability and expertise
- **Sustainability**: Incentivises environmental and social outcomes
- **Transparency**: Provides clear audit trails and open-book arrangements

#### 8.3 Business Case Integration
The model aligns with TPP22-04 requirements through:
- Explicit consideration in procurement strategy development
- Quantified benefits in economic appraisals
- Risk-adjusted value calculations in business cases
- Options analysis comparing traditional and value-based approaches

### 9. Risk Management and Probity

#### 9.1 Risk Mitigation Framework
Key risks and controls include:

| Risk Category | Mitigation Measures |
|--------------|-------------------|
| Baseline accuracy | Independent validation during establishment phase |
| Value attribution | Clear causation criteria and counterfactual analysis |
| Budget uncertainty | Engagement caps and contingency provisions |
| Supplier dependency | Embedded agency staff and knowledge transfer requirements |
| Probity concerns | Open-book arrangements and external oversight |

#### 9.2 Probity Safeguards
Integrity is maintained through:
- 12-month cooling-off period for former government employees in value share arrangements
- Full disclosure of all financial calculations and assumptions
- Regular probity reviews by independent advisors
- Compliance with ICAC Direct Dealing Guidelines
- Public reporting of value share payments

### 10. Contract Mechanisms

#### 10.1 Documentation Requirements
The following contract elements are essential:
- Special conditions defining value share arrangements
- KPI schedules with detailed measurement criteria
- Verification procedures and calculation methodologies
- Governance charter establishing decision rights
- Knowledge transfer and exit management provisions

#### 10.2 Performance Management
Ongoing management includes:
- Regular performance reviews against KPIs
- Corrective action plans for underperformance
- Value tracking and reporting systems
- Continuous improvement processes
- Annual contract effectiveness reviews

## Part D: Evidence and Application

### 11. Proven Performance - CAPITAL Framework

#### 11.1 Documented Outcomes
The model has delivered verified results across major infrastructure projects:
- Western Harbour Tunnel: $180 million in capital and operational savings
- Sydney Harbour Tunnel: 30-year commercial framework implementation
- M6 Stage 1: Established as repeatable model for tunnel projects

#### 11.2 Framework Recognition
Industry and government endorsement includes:
- Transport for NSW adoption as standard approach for major projects

### 12. Suitable Applications

#### 12.1 Optimal Project Types
The model is most effective for:
- Complex infrastructure requiring specialist expertise
- Long-term asset management arrangements
- Transformation programs with measurable outcomes
- Projects with significant optimisation potential
- Initiatives requiring innovation and creative solutions

#### 12.2 Success Prerequisites
Key requirements for successful implementation:
- Reliable baseline data availability
- Clear outcome definition and measurement capability
- Senior stakeholder commitment to value-based approach
- Collaborative culture supporting innovation
- Robust governance and verification processes

### 13. Verification Methodology

#### 13.1 Verification Principles
Value verification maintains integrity through:
- Independence from both CBS and operational teams
- Transparency in all calculations and assumptions
- Complete audit trails for all claimed savings
- Timely completion enabling prompt payment

#### 13.2 Verification Process Steps
The formal verification process includes:
1. Baseline confirmation against original documentation
2. Performance data collection and validation
3. Value calculation using agreed formulas
4. Quality assurance review by independent party
5. Steering committee review and approval
6. Payment authorisation and processing

### 14. Conclusion

The CBS Value-Based Pricing Model represents a mature, tested approach to achieving superior infrastructure outcomes while maintaining fiscal discipline. By aligning commercial incentives with government objectives, this model transforms the traditional client-supplier relationship into a genuine partnership focused on measurable value creation.

The model's compliance with NSW procurement requirements, combined with its demonstrated success through the CAPITAL framework, provides confidence that agencies can achieve significant efficiency improvements within existing budgetary constraints. With appropriate governance, clear measurement frameworks, and robust verification processes, value-based pricing offers a pathway to extracting maximum value from every dollar invested in infrastructure and services.
