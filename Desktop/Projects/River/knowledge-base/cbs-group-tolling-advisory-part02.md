Our Interest in New Zealand's RUC Transition
The transition to fleetwide RUC represents one of the most significant road charging transformations globally. Based on our work with similar transitions, we recognise that the Ministry is entering a critical phase where independent technical expertise will be essential to:
Consider market input and identify opportunities (and risks) to be explored further
Evaluate vendor proposals and technology claims objectively
Define a target architecture for the scheme and packaging approach
Design regulatory frameworks that genuinely enable sustained competition rather than entrench incumbents
Identify technical and commercial risks before they become expensive problems
Develop commercial models that focus on outcomes and demonstrate value for money to New Zealanders
Protect revenue integrity and user privacy throughout system design
Ensure proposed architectures support future road pricing evolution (location-based, time-based, network-based charging)
We are interested in serving as Technical Authority to the Ministry during this transition, providing the independent technical oversight that will be critical to success.
What We Are NOT Proposing
To be clear, CBS Group does not provide retail services to vehicle owners, does not develop proprietary technology platforms, and does not seek to compete with the retail service providers this RFI is designed to attract.

| Question #4: Do you have access to technology to deliver your proposed RUC service, or is new technology required? If new technology is required, how long would it take to design and deliver it?
CBS Group perspective: Technology cannot be considered in isolation from the broader solution architecture
As technical advisors rather than technology providers, our value is helping the Ministry understand that the RUC transition is fundamentally a solution architecture and packaging challenge, not a traditional technology procurement exercise. The challenge is to determine the optimal architecture / packaging early and be cognisant of technology evolution and asset management risks throughout the procurement and transition.
Getting the solution architecture right
The Ministry will receive compelling proposals from vendors, each promoting their preferred technology: GPS telematics, OBD-II devices, mobile apps, or built-in vehicle systems etc. and likely proposing end-to-end vehicle/roadside, processing and retail services.
The risk is that this limits the development of an overall solution and consideration of packaging, rather than informing an architecture that enables:
A solution that considers road use calculation, trip construction, billing and retail, and compliance services as separate modules or packages to be obtained separately
Multiple technology providers serving different functions and user segments
Integration with current and future road pricing and congestion charging schemes
Incorporation into existing services, accounts and digital identities
Evolution without vendor lock-in or costly system replacement
Interoperability between providers (through for example, a central "clearing house")
The packaging of procurement matters as much as the technology:
Procuring an "end-to-end RUC system" from a single vendor creates lock-in and leads to poor user experience
Enabling multiple road use calculation tools and retail services / products will lead to tailored solutions for different user groups and priorities
Setting outcome-based standards (accuracy, privacy, interoperability) rather than prescribing technology enables innovation
International examples support a more modular architecture for the complex user groups demographics and geographies in New Zealand. There are several examples in Europe where the European Electronic Toll Services (EETS) drives multi-retailer interoperable architectures. Essentially this EU regulation explicitly separates toll chargers, service providers and technology suppliers. Multiple retailers issue different technologies and/or accounts usable across different schemes and countries. Many of these use central national clearing to manage the modular approach to detection and charging, with customers benefiting from diverse service propositions.
Single vendor reliance can be appropriate within a small geography, for example Singapore. There is flexibility in how users pay in these schemes but no retailer competition and no differentiation in customer experience. This limits customer choice and potentially innovation.
Leveraging technology evolution 
The ministry is right to ask vendors about the production readiness of their technology and should expect (even encourage) innovative solutions that may be deployed at scale for the first time in New Zealand. 
A common issue in technology-based services such as RUC (and equally tolling) is the pace at which technical developments and innovations hit the market. Setting requirements early risk constraining future technology but being too loose increases risks inherent with innovation.
The Ministry should expect technology capabilities to evolve significantly between 2027 and full fleet transition. Any regulatory framework should avoid locking in today's technology while ensuring minimum standards for accuracy and fraud resistance.
The Ministry should also consider strategic moves that accelerates the market towards a target outcome, rather than simply waiting for vendors to catch up. For example, introducing a Smart Phone based solution may not meet all user requirements, but by providing it as an option amongst other services will accelerate a broader take up and accelerate towards a ‘zero-infrastructure’ horizon.
Managing the ‘asset’
In this context, the ‘asset’ includes the physical and digital world. 
The total cost of ownership (TOTEX) of the solution will include full lifecycle costs including hardware, installation, replacement cycles and system integration, as well as operating costs from maintenance through manual processing and customer service resources. The solution must optimise what costs fall on government vs. users vs. providers. This is seldom considered in tolling / pricing contexts but can have material impacts on value and sustainability. Caution is advised where vendors proposing new technology cannot demonstrate how solutions will be operated, maintained and managed to give whole of life value for money. 
Schemes of this nature must also demonstrate they are managing the digital assets appropriately. For example, balancing the need for data retention against privacy requirements will materially shape the service differentiation offered (the privacy paradox). Some users will want set-and-forget convenience which requires extensive data collection, account linking, automated systems. Other users will prioritise privacy protection which will drive manual processes and limited automation and data retention. Both user expectations are valid and the eventual service differentiation model must accommodate both.
CBS Group's Role
We can help the Ministry:
Design a solution architecture that enables multiple technologies and customer services to coexist and interoperate
Develop a packaging strategy that leads to optimal services and products for business functions and user scenarios
Develop outcome-based technical standards rather than technology-prescriptive specifications  
Structure procurement to prevent vendor lock-in while ensuring system integrity
Evaluate vendor proposals against user needs and future policy requirements
The goal isn't picking the "best" technology – it's creating the conditions for a diverse, competitive market serving different user needs while protecting government's ability to evolve the system as transport pricing policy develops. |

| Question #5: Beyond the law changes being introduced, are there any other regulatory, technical or commercial barriers to market entry? 
CBS Group perspective: We have experience in identifying and reducing market entry barriers
The New Zealand RUC market should be informed by the evolution of the tolling market in Australia where the MOU group that manages interoperability and technical compliance has inadvertently raised barriers to competition and innovation within what has become a closed retailer market. 
Technical barriers
At this stage of the transition, the Ministry has the ability to shape a market that prioritises interoperability of retail products, system integration and data security without compromising open access and low barriers to entry. By adopting open, transparent interface requirements and outcome-based standards the costs for entry will be reduced. The Ministry will ensure potential vendors can manage the technical risks of entry and not advantage any future incumbents.
This is particularly important if the Ministry aims to leverage existing government (or private) services and accounts. If the scheme’s interface requirements are too constrained, perhaps by an incumbent or single vendor architecture, the technical cost of integration will be prohibitively high.
Regulatory barriers
The Ministry needs to establish clear, objective, measurable criteria for RUC provider approval. Criteria that are subjective or unclear create barriers because potential entrants can't assess whether they're likely to be approved, increasing risk and causing the approval process to become expensive and time-consuming. 
There is also a risk that overly prescriptive technical standards favour existing technology / approaches rather than outcomes. For example, if standards specify "GPS-based distance measurement with 99% accuracy" this prevents alternative approaches (OBD, telematics, etc.) that might achieve the same outcome differently. A better approach would be to adopt outcome-based standards.
Commercial barriers
The Ministry must consider the commercial risks and barriers that vendors and service providers will face, specifically the uncertainty on return of investment and the demand for specific services within the overall scheme. 
The transition of the existing fleet into the scheme, and the rate of new connected vehicles entering the market will determine customer volume and revenue growth trajectory. For providers requiring significant capital investment (hardware development, distribution networks, system development), uncertainty about customer volumes and service demand makes ROI calculation difficult.
Question #6: What enablers or safeguards need to be in place to protect users and revenue?
CBS Group perspective: Revenue comes from focusing on compliance, not enforcement
Revenue protection is a major challenge in this transition. FED is nearly impossible to evade; RUC requires active compliance. The system must be designed to minimize revenue leakage while remaining user-friendly. In our experience, effective compliance mechanisms are multi-layered, prioritise behavioural design and differentiate between accidental and intentional non-compliance.
Road tolling models are typically built on the assumption of verifying every transaction at the point of travel (toll gantries) whereas public transport is based more on the core assumption that passengers want to comply and invariably do without verification – appetite to comply with the threat of a spot check is enough. Schemes that adopt a public transport approach often experience better value for money from enforcement and improved customer experience / sentiment.
Multi-Layered Verification
Systems that work combine multiple layers of verification, such as automated detection (ANPR cameras, GPS-based enforcement, data analytics), and periodic verification (mobile enforcement, rego renewal checks). Similarly, a multi-layered or escalating consequence (reminders, first offence leniency, admin charges, penalties etc.) will support increased compliance.
Behavioural Design
Behavioural design is essentially about making compliance easier than non-compliance. The most effective systems don't rely primarily on enforcement - they design compliance to be the path of least resistance. For example:
Auto-renewal and subscription options (set and forget)
Proactive reminders before RUC runs out
Convenient payment methods (linked to registration, fuel purchases, etc.)
Simple user interfaces, combining into existing digital identities where possible
Consequences that escalate gradually (not zero-to-lawsuit)
Differentiated Enforcement
Research shows compliance systems fail when they treat all non-compliance the same rather than designing systems around different types on non-compliance, for example:
Accidental / unintentional – need reminders and an easy path back to compliance
Economic offenders – are minimised through balanced consequences that push them to compliance, rather than intentional evaders
Intentional / repeat evaders – need detection and serious consequences
Systems that send everyone directly to harsh penalties drive "forgetters" into non-compliance permanently (why pay penalty plus back-RUC when you can just keep not paying?). |

| Question #8: How long might it take for a market to develop? What are the dependencies?
CBS Group Insight: Road User Charging is NOT an extension of Road Tolling
It is tempting to look to the global tolling industry for potential solutions for Road User Charging and whilst there is likely to be valuable input in some aspects, the Ministry is encouraged to consider other lateral industries.
Tolling is built around a traditional architecture of roadside detection > back-office processing (trip construction) > billing > customer retail services > enforcement, with a ‘middle-man’ between roadside and the customer to undertake back-office processing. 
Each of these modules generates complexity and cost but the relatively restricted deployment and high value of tolls mean value for money is achievable. 
Road Charging does not have this luxury with all vehicle users on all roads requiring a service and a necessity for low-cost collection (vs. almost zero FED collection costs). From Government’s perspective, as complexity increases across an increasing number of operators (who need paying), the harder it becomes to develop a fit-for purpose commercial model that achieves value for money.
CBS Group’s view is that this is achievable by looking at parallel industries such as utilities or telcos. These achieve value by restricting usage measurement to the source (the phone or meter) and reporting this directly to the customer service provider (telco or energy retailer). The benefits of this include:
Means of measurement (metering) can be varied depending on the user, vehicle and priorities. For example, connected vehicles, on-board diagnostic devices, separate RUC OBUs or mobile phones.
Compliance can be achieved through low-cost verification methods. 
The model is adaptable for future policy (e.g. how telcos adapt the retail model for higher internet costs, lower SMS costs, and how energy retailers have introduced green tariffs)
More importantly, by adopting alternative models, the Ministry can look at opportunities for road charging to leverage existing customer services, reducing up-front and operational costs.
Overall, this leads to a market with reduced complexity and a more value for money based commercial framework. 
Question #9: How would your proposed RUC service support the future transition of petrol vehicles to RUC? What do you see as the challenges that market suppliers may require government support to resolve?
CBS Group Insight: Focusing on the cost to collect and customer experience will best support transition 
A transition roadmap will be dependent on technology evolution and the perceived value of a change in excise calculation and collection. 
Collecting FED costs virtually nothing (<1% of revenue) but is a relatively blunt tool. Collecting RUC will incur greater costs but can be offset by incorporating other policy measures that are valued by users, (congestion / demand management, environmental benefits etc.)
The Cost to Collect issue is best addressed by putting in place a solution that reduces dependency on expensive, dedicated back-office functions (see question #8). Even so, the initial means of metering vehicles will drive high costs. Connected vehicles will not hit critical mass for a number of years and even if on-board diagnostic devices were opened up by manufacturers, the age of the NZ fleet would suggest a high percentage of vehicles would be reliant on external on-board units. 
Introducing a Smart Phone application to support metering and charging will fill a significant technology gap, and will accelerate the adoption of smarter digital methods. As connected vehicles increase in the fleet, the cost of collection and enforcement will naturally fall.
The perceived value of the scheme by users and society more generally is also a significant factor. There are learnings from the NSW Independent Toll Review. Specifically on the perceived value and equity of schemes, and the transparency of how funds are spent (high collection costs and high profits vs. investment in public transport and environmental improvements). Much of this is out of scope for this response, but the Ministry should consider that high optionality, low customer friction and seamless transactions is critical in achieving perceived value and strong customer sentiment / trust. As referenced in question #8, a tolling legacy as a starting point for road charging will struggle to achieve this. |
