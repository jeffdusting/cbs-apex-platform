Foundations: North East Link Act 2020 

The North East Link Act 2020 is described in its explanatory memorandum as having five main purposes. These are: 

To establish the North East Link State Tolling Corporation (STC) as a body corporate with an official seal.  The Bill stipulates it may acquire, hold and dispose of real and personal property, can sue and be sued, and certifies that all amounts collected by the Corporation, including tolls, administration fees, and any other fees or charges, are the personal property of the Corporation. 

To provide the Corporation with powers to fix, collect and enforce tolls and administration fees on the North East Link, as well as the operation and management of the North East Link. In this respect, the Bill mirrors the provisions and reforms to toll enforcement adopted in the West Gate Tunnel (Truck Bans and Traffic Management) Act 2019. 

To provide for the tabling and amendment of North East Link tolling agreements between the Victorian Government and the State Tolling Corporation, which authorise and regulate tolling on the North East Link. Parliament will have oversight of these agreements, which may be revoked by a resolution of both Houses. 

To amend and modify the operation of the Road Management Act 2004 in relation to the North East Link road, which includes excluding the North East Link State Tolling Corporation from the definition of State road authority. 

To make several minor amendments to the EastLink Project Act 2004, the Melbourne City Link Act 1995, and the West Gate Tunnel (Truck Bans and Traffic Management) Act 2019, that aligns the regulation and practices of tollways in Victoria. To make the North East Link State Tolling Corporation operational, the Bill also amends the Accident Towing Services Act 2017, the Borrowing and Investment Powers Act 2014, the Children Youth and Families Act 2005, the Criminal Procedure Act 2009, the Fines Reform Act 2014, the Heavy Vehicle National Law Application Act 2013, the Infringements Act 2005, the Road Safety Act 1986, and the Transport Integration Act 2010. 

As it relates to this report, the Act provides for:

Flexibility with regards to the definition of toll zones and tolls payable, including administration fees

Exempt vehicles

Provisions for the management / protection of a ‘tolling device’

Evidentiary provisions (Part 4, Division 6)

Makes it clear that a Tolling device is not surveillance device - Surveillance Devices Act 1999.

The requirement to establish roaming agreements with Victorian operators, note that the Bill as passed explicitly lists Victorian tollway operators with no reference to interstate operators.

Section 1 | Executive Summary

Situation

State Toll Co (STC) will be established to, amongst other responsibilities, collect tolling revenue for the North East Link (NEL) from 2027.  

It is also possible that STC will take on a broader revenue collection mandate - responsible for all sources of revenue earned from the NEL, including but not limited to monetisation of data collected, on-road and online advertising.

In anticipation of establishment of the STC, the North East Link Project (NELP) are developing a tolling strategy that will inform; the tolling solution, the STC organisation, commercial frameworks and risk.  More immediately, the tolling strategy will inform the planned market sounding designed to verify / inform the final STC Tolling Strategy 

Complication

The tolling industry has been very stable in Australia over the last 20 years, with no significant change in technology or business model, with significant consolidation in the industry participants.   

In contrast, the international tolling market has continued to evolve, with new entrants and technologies, and a consistent merging of tolling with mobility more broadly.  The Australian tolling industry has remained insular, and blind to the slow extinction of tolling as a market segment with the rise of the aggregated transportation market segment and Mobility as a Service (MaaS).

The NEL Tolling strategy must balance:

The current and emerging trends in the context of a further 6 years in solution evolution until NEL opens

Government risk appetite

The need to maximise the efficiency of toll and other revenue collection.

Question

This paper seeks to answer the following questions:

What are the relevant industry trends?

What are the viable tolling and revenue collection options available to STC?

What are the relative merits (revenue collection efficiency and risk) for these options?  

What uncertainties should inform further analysis and the market sounding?

What if any impact is there on the tolling solution selected by the tolling topology (closed / open / hybrid) adopted by STC?

Answer

Traditional approaches to tolling are founded in technologies and customer management strategies of the 1990s – today we see the decline in DSRC Tag use, the increase in mobile applications for mobility, and an ever-increasing video analytics capability.

Over the past 10 years there have been significant changes in technology and customer sentiment that makes it possible for STC to consider new Tolling Solutions that:

Reliably deliver toll revenue at lowest total cost

Create opportunities for additional revenue to be earned by NEL and collected by STC

Reduce the capital and operational costs associated with tolling

Improve the public perception and modernise customer engagement

Summary of Observations

With significant supporting detail provided in the Appendices, the following is a summary of the key macro factors effecting the Tolling industry – considered in the development of the Tolling System Evaluation Framework and underpinning our recommendations.

Australian / Victorian Tollroad Market

Australian and the Victorian toll road market is mature – with established processes, low levels of innovation, and low levels of competition.

Tolling is a subset of other dynamic systems 

Tolling systems are subsystems of both specific roadway management systems and of the larger context, Transport. The subsystem, tolling,  and its containing system , Transport, are subject to the influences of larger systems of social and cultural organisation, of governance, and technology.

As a new market entrant STC’s business strategies will have both direct and rebound effects on key systems; political, economic, social, and direct impacts with the containing system; Transport. While outside the immediate scope of this report, STC’s commercial strategy will establish core technology requirements, and be a key factor in determining the final tolling solution. Therefore, for this report we consider STC’s strategic field to be its’ choices for positioning within Systems of influence, and specifically STC’s selection of its commercial modes, technology, and its’ assessment of customer value.  

The market is changing 

Bye Bye Boomer. Generation Z, the ‘digital integrators,’ who have never lived without Google, Apple, Facebook, and Amazon will become NEL’s largest customer base. For this and every following generation, technology is ubiquitous effortlessly integrated into, and permeating all areas of life.

The future will be ruled by the toll operators who deliver the most efficient, seamless and integrated experiences to these customers.  
Adopting payment systems that seamlessly integrate with all other forms of transport, and the rest of life, is an integral step forward. Transport does not require its own currency or payment channel when integrated, multi modal payment systems, based on location aware smart devices are now the norm. 

Customers are more powerful than ever before. They expect to be understood and appealed to in their full complexity. All interactions, products and services are expected to be personalised. This places a premium on customer data collection and analytics. 

Tolling is technology Led

Historically tolling has evolved with roadside technology – stuck in a series of incremental arcs where there is a direct trade between cost and revenue performance (see diagram to the right)

Today as part of a global trend to digitisation the tolling state is becoming lightweight and mobile as the person, watch, phone or vehicle becomes the edge device.

Current and emerging technologies drive hyper connectivity, new transport/social eco systems, and new commercial opportunities for NEL:  

Mobility, 5G/6G, V2X, V2V, Blockchain and Gamification create new passage and operational technologies, and new types of customer relationships, including peer – to -peer. 

Emerging transport concepts | Multi-Modal

Ultimately, we see a world where Operators’ control access to road networks and eventually, start to serve as the integration layer across all modes of transport, as mass transit becomes more intermodal and personal transit becomes more centrally managed.  

Current level of Market Innovation 

In 2019 KPMG published a survey of 65 public and private toll operators representing a total of 184 tolling facilities in the America’s Europe and Asia (not Australia/New Zealand). Their data suggests that significant opportunities for improved performance exist, and that market leaders are those that tirelessly pursue the technological advantage.   They also understand that customer expectations are changing, customers now expect conveniences they experience in one part of their life to be available in everything they do — online payment capabilities and mobile payment systems are quickly becoming table stakes. 

Emerging customer relationships and commercial models

Tolling’s modern antecedents, drive-by, coins in a bucket, established a utility view of the road user (‘customer’) centred on trip transactions. 
For STC, as a new entrant into a monopoly tolling market, and at a tipping point of social and technological change within Australia, there are significant opportunities for innovation in Customer relationships and corresponding commercial models. Absent an agreed commercial strategy for STC this report offers uses a theoretical construct to reflect the presence of these decisions within our analysis.

Divergent status of tolling – Australia and International

As an operating monopoly, Transurban (ASX: TCL) established, and now dominates the market. It is worth remembering that TU was required to inculcate tolling behaviours into the road user market. The consequences manifest in early design choices that emphasises roadside infrastructure as an important signifier in tolling awareness and compliance. It established a customer relationship that was highly personalised for scofflaws and contentedly transactional with other road users. More recently Transurban’s corporate attention has shifted to maintaining market share within the tolling market is established and still dominates. Increasingly this relies on social capital and the political cover this engenders. TU is now re-positioning as a ‘customer-centric’ organisation, a strategy that underpins its application development. Linkt, and its move towards more seamless, less obtrusive infrastructure.

Gamification  

Simply defined, gamification is the use of game elements – point-scoring, levels, competition with others, measurable evidence of accomplishment, ratings and rules of play – in non-game contexts.  Gamification introduces competition and social activity into behavioural interventions so, for example, participants become "players" who can win individual or group rewards if they adjust their behaviour. 

Games deliver an instantaneous, visceral experience of success and reward, and they are increasingly used in the workplace to promote emotional engagement with the work process, to increase workers’ psychological investment in completing otherwise uninspiring tasks, and to influence, or “nudge”, workers and customer behaviour.

The implications for tolling are clear, gamification holds the potential to increase app uptake and customer loyalty, trip frequency, improve driver behaviour and reduce scofflaws. 

There is a growing body of evidence to suggest that gamifying business operations has real, quantifiable effects. Lyft’s “Accelerate Rewards” programme encourages drivers to level up by completing a certain number of rides per month to unlock special rewards like fuel discounts from Shell (gold level) and free roadside assistance (platinum level). 

The design of gaming products in the field of transport, a field which is perceived as having derived demand, is largely underdeveloped.  Yet the implications for tolling are clear, gamification approaches can be used to improve driver and community engagement, increase motivation and encourage participation in specific programs to engender travel behaviour change.  

Key Attributes and Definitions

In selecting the preferred Tolling Solution, several key solution attributes and definitions must be agreed.  These are detailed below.

Leakage

Proportion of tolling traffic likely to be missed, all toll systems will have a level of traffic that is not able to be billed, leakage by solution may require a system of solutions to close the gap.

Technical Leakage

Advances in technology have continuously reduced technical leakage to fractions of a percent, with ever increasing costs for diminishing returns from further performance improvement.

Increasingly, Technical Leakage from system outages and failures is of greater concern than system design performance.

Technical leakage is a failure to detect and correctly identify a vehicle using the toll road and excludes lost revenue because of business rules and decisions.

Of interest is that internationally the OnBoard Device (OBD) is a contributor to technical leakage as the requirement to have multiple OBD’s for different toll operators, the requirement to switch them between cars, the difficulty in using OBD’s in rental vehicles hinders accurate account and trip calculations. In response a range of application providers have emerged to replace the OBD with a mobile phone and GPS technology.  

System Leakage

The Trip and Toll Determination (TTD) solution may be the cause of revenue leakage (failure to correctly calculate the trip or applicable toll), and able to recover toll revenue through the re-construction of trips from incomplete passage data.

System Leakage is the sum of Technical Leakage and any leakage resulting from a failure of the TTD.

Commercial Leakage

Business rules may result in a loss of revenue (waiving of debts, discounts, customer goodwill etc).   This is assessed as commercial leakage in this report.

Customer Leakage (Scofflaws)

Leakage as a business issue is fundamentally linked to the transactional antecedents of the tolling customer relationship. When demand is relatively inelastic and the market is serviced by few providers, or is a monopoly, the user, facing subpar options for substitution, will willingly or grudgingly, pay for the service. 

However, a relatively small number will not pay. The reasons for non-payment vary; new users and visitors may have made no arrangements, trip calculations or payment mechanisms may fail, and there may be deliberate avoidance for social or psychological reasons.  These leakage levels have a significant impact on efficiency and profitability. Leakage reduces revenues; and increases the cost and complexity of enforcement. While there is no agreed definition of leakage, we use the term to address both the difficulty and cost in collecting tolls as commercial leakage, and the challenge of capturing 100% of eligible events as technical leakage.

Error Rates

Proportion of tolls likely to be issued in error, where tolling and enforcement are based on reading of number plates, either by a human or ANPR. 

Errors also include the incidence of duplicate / incorrect tolling – largely because of the passage identification technology or TTD.  

Solutions based on tags, apps or phone data may lead to duplication of travel events where multiple devices are present in/on a vehicle as it travels.

Legislation and enforcement

The KPMG Industry survey found that most surveyed toll road operators suggest they are being hampered by either inadequate legislation or lax enforcement. Fines tended to be the most common form of legal recourse available to toll operators in our survey. Those international systems with the lowest levels of leakage tended to be the ones that used fines and vehicle registration suspensions as enforcement tools  

Cost

Interestingly, many respondents cite the high cost of collecting tolls as a reason for not collecting at all. Many operators also seem to suggest that enforcement costs may outweigh the benefits. To assess the validity of this perception, KPMG compared three different operators: one with low leakage levels and average enforcement costs; one with high leakage and no enforcement; and one with average leakage and high enforcement costs. What KPMG found was that operating margins tended to remain the same in the first two instances but drop significantly in the third. So, while there may be a point where the costs outweigh the benefits of pursuing every violator, it is clear there is also a point of balance that allows toll operators to cost-effectively maximize revenues.  

In Australia, commercial leakage is managed via customer segmentation with non-paying customers a Target group, subject to high levels of personalisation relative to the Transactional, paying customers. The outreach to customers is typically negative targeting, backed up by civil compliance procedures. 

 This target group is a revenue centre for operations, principally through the imposition of fees and charges, including lucrative video matching fees. 

We note very little has been done to positively-target tolled customers and propose a review of gamification options which are likely to reduce scofflaws, minimise the costs of collection, and preserve and build the social capital required for long term operations.

Video Matching Fees

Video matching fees are an example of an administration fee charged by tollway operators nationally.  They provide a mechanism for the toll road operator to charge fee for the additional video tolling costs historically associated with ‘processing’ images associated with each passage – charged in addition to the regulated tolls.   

The North East Act 2020 allows STC to set tolls and administration fees and provides no restrictions upon STC in this regard.  It is however, useful to note that an industry ‘rule’ has been that tolls should be all inclusive with an administration fee applied for exceptional processes – it is in this framework that we would expect that if STC is to charge a video matching fee, it would also offer road users an option that does not attract this administration fee.

With the steady growth of video tolling (approximately 25% of CityLink traffic is video tolled, the rapid decline in video tolling costs, administration fees such as video matching fee revenue is significant and often included as a revenue line.

Further complication arises through the inter-operability arrangements between the toll roads where it could be reasonably be expected that NEL customers (if they exist and do not have a tag) would be charged a video matching fee when travelling on other toll roads.    

Once again, the impact of Video match and administration fees upon STC will be determined by the customer strategy and consequentially the pricing strategy.

It is recommended that STC develop and formalise its pricing strategy, with particular attention to the application of administration fees, including but not limited to fees associated with video matching and no arrangement travel.

Once a passage is detected and the road user correctly identified, the Trip and Toll Determination Engine determines:

The North East Link Bill 2020 is the ability to differentiate the toll based on vehicle class and toll zones – all other detail is to be defined in the yet to be confirmed North East Tolling Agreements.

These elements are used in tolling schemes to both create demand (time of day and day of week discounts) and inhibit demand (flag fall). The following are elements of a toll pricing scheme that are utilised on other toll roads: 

Vehicle Class Allows for varied tolls for different classes of vehicle based on the measured/video/registered class of vehicles travelling on the road (Specifically called out as an element of tolling on the North East Link Bill 2020) 

Vehicle height Allows for determination between light and heavy vehicles by real time height measurement (less definitive and potentially subject to some anomalies such as loaded vehicles being charged less due to suspension sag)  

Loaded status Allows for loaded/unloaded tolling of vehicles based on the as travelling weight (requires additional equipment to measure the vehicles loaded status with WIM sensors)  

Account type Reflects the simplicity with which the toll road user can be identified as travelling and pay the toll (tag/video/no arrangement) 

Flag fall Ensures that short trips have an acceptable tolling return 

Local discount Makes short trips cheaper to encourage road usage over parallel local routes 

Toll segments Reflects a user pays arrangement with longer travel costing more (and usually tunnel travel is more expensive)  

Time of Day pricing Drives travel behaviour by placing a premium on peak usage/discount on off peak usage 

Day of week Weekend discounts encourage additional demand  

Trip Cap Used on long toll roads to maintain reasonable tolls on short segments but not excessively penalise longer trips 

Video matching fee Reflects the added effort relating to video tolling over tag-based tolling 

Dynamic pricing Allows management of traffic volume to reduce congestion 

Open/Closed systems Relates to the location of tolling gantries entrance/exit versus mainline locations and whether the trip based on a measured entry and exit or by detection at several points in the roadway. 

Fixed Price Monthly/weekly subscription schemes that limit the total cost of travel.

Compliance

Compliance comprises the mechanisms that can be used to compel a road user who has travelled on the road without a valid ‘arrangement’ to make payment.

Identify road users travelling without an arrangement

Discourage scofflaws looking to avoid toll payment.

To support the enforcement processes by providing an evidence pack recognised by the court system.

Evidence Requirements

There are established regulations that define the court systems’ requirements for evidence, including chain of custody verification, encryption, device gazettal and registers.
