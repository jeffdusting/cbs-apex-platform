Step 2: Establishing a contract

Two types of contracts:

Fixed delivery contracts: agreeing to a set number of ACCUs at a set price for the duration of the contract. Schedule the ACCUs to be delivered across the duration of the contract. Contract starts when Clean Energy Regulator notifies you that you had a successful bid at auction and ends when the final delivery of ACCUs has occurred and all payments have been made

	Each date in schedule should allow for audits, reporting, etc.

	If you cannot delivery the agreed quantity for a fixed delivery contract, you are required to source the different from other projects you run or from the secondary market.

Option to delivery contract: 

provides the right, but not the obligation, to sell carbon abatement to the Commonwealth at an agreed price, within a set time. Allows holders to better manage their price and supply risks to encourage more abatement projects.

Option contracts may be used in negotiations with lenders or other parties to demonstrate what the Commonwealth will pay for abatement from a specified project, with no contractual barrier to seeking more lucrative contracts from other buyers.

Flexible duration of up to 10 years. Scheduled milestones that are not delivered against will lapse.

Could have both fixed and option contract at some time as long as combined contract does not exceed what could be generated by the project.

Auction qualification

Before bidding in an auction must first qualify to participate.

Auction qualification forms must be completed through client portal at least 20 days prior to an auction. If approved, auction qualification is valid for 12 months (as long as no information changes).

Estimating abatement quantity

All Emissions Reduction Fund projects must provide a forward abatement estimate (FAE) in tonnes of carbon dioxide equivalent (tCO2-e) at project registration

For larger projects (1,000,000 ACCUs +), should consider contacting the Clean Energy Regulator to discuss your project and types of evidence required in advance of the auction qualification deadline

The IAQ is indicative only and can be revised at auction registration when you provide the agreed quantity (the amount you agree to provide).

The agreed quantity (AQ) is the quantity of Kyoto ACCUs to be delivered over the duration of the contract and is provided to the Clean Energy Regulator at auction registration

Evidence required to provide at auction qualification is forecast modelling (for our project type)

Contract lengths:

Standard contract: he most common standard contract length is seven years. Contracts associated with projects that have a crediting period of 10 years or more can apply for a standard contract length of 10 years

Short-term contract: requires multiple Kyoto ACCU deliveries within a period of less than seven years. Contracts associated with projects that have a crediting period of 10 years or more can apply for a short-term contract of less than 10 years.

A project can have a maximum of three short-term contracts associated with it throughout the project’s crediting period. A short-term contract may cover part of the project’s abatement within the contracted period.

Immediate delivery contract: where the nominated Kyoto ACCUs will be delivered within 10 days of the auction. You can only bid for an immediate delivery contract if you have the required number of Kyoto ACCUs in your Australian national register of emissions units account.

You can have a maximum of 10 immediate delivery contracts per project over the project’s crediting period.

The minimum volume for immediate delivery contracts is at least 10 per cent of the forward abatement estimate for the project supplying the Kyoto ACCUs or 15,000 Kyoto ACCUs (whichever is lower).

Reporting and auditing

Reports:

All participants must report on their projects at regular intervals

Reports may be accompanied by an audit report

Under the Emissions Reduction Fund, you may choose when to report on your project as long as you adhere to the minimum and maximum reporting periods (periods between reports). 

The first reporting period begins at the start of your project’s crediting period.  Each new reporting period begins immediately after the previous reporting period. All reporting periods must be within your project’s crediting period. 

In general, a reporting period can be as short as six months, as long as two years for emissions avoidance projects

Project report will be processed within 90 days

Audits:

Audits must be prepared by a registered category 2 greenhouse and energy auditor. Must be independent to prevent conflict of interest.

Minimum of 3 scheduled audits across 7-year crediting period

All audits will need to establish reasonable assurance that the abatement achieved and reported on is accurate.

Costs of audits are on project… - best to engage auditor early so can factor in pricing into bidding

Recommend choose different auditors (at least two) from different firms over the lifespan of the project. Clean Energy Regulator will have more faith in the project if this is adhered to, and project is less likely to be selected for additional audit if they meet this requirement.

Audit type frequency (info into what each type requires is on site):

Initial audits: must be submitted with first report for your project, 6 months to 2 years into crediting period.

Subsequent audits: must be submitted in line with the project audit schedule received at time of project registration. Intended to ensure your project is audited across periods of peak abatement, providing assurance over the maximum number of ACCUs issued across a crediting period. These audits will cover a minimum reporting period of 12 months.

Unscheduled additional audits: 3 triggers for these audits include a single report claiming over 100,000 tCO2-e abatement, + other reasons…

Clean energy regulator will set an audit schedule for your project and is provided at time of project registration. Sets out level of assurance, frequency, and scope of audits required for project.

For most projects, minimum of 3 scheduled audits across the 7 year period is required. Number will depend on project size in terms of average annual abatement expected to be generated (based on forward abatement estimate)

Applying for ACCUs (Claiming ACCUs)

Once project has generated carbon abatement can apply

Apply through Client Portal using the Emissions Reduction Fund Project Report and Crediting form

Will need to provide:

Eligible offsets report covering the reporting period

A signed application for ACCUs

An audit report (if required by your audit schedule)

Method specific supporting documentation

Clean energy regulator has up to 90 days from receipt of a complete and accurate application to process crediting applications

Once application for ACCUs has been approved, the ACCUS will be deposited in your ANREU account. 

Can either sell these on secondary market or be used to meet contractual obligations

Delivering units and receiving payment

Transfer ACCUs to the Clean Energy Regulator’s ANREU account, to receive payment send an invoice. Payment will be received within 20 business days from receipt of invoice provided it is complete and accurate

ANREU stands for Australian National Registry of Emissions Units

Overview:

From the FE04 HVAC Energy Efficiency Report there are two main advantages to the climate system in terms of savings; reduction of carbon emission and reduction of energy consumption. These four initiatives cater towards these advantages and together, allow Downer to maximise the reward for implementing these changes in the Waratah fleet.

The FED Emissions Reduction Fund rewards businesses for a reduction in CO2 emission by awarding an Australian Carbon Credit Unit (ACCU) per tonne of Carbon Dioxide Equivalent (tCO2-e) stored or avoided by the project. These Carbon Credits can then be sold to the government via a carbon abatement contract or sold on the secondary market. Via this method, an additional revenue stream of $166,320 to $268,128 could be added as a result of the HVAC upgrade. These prices are indicative and can vary depending on the demand set by the government via a carbon abatement contract, or the supply and demand in the secondary market.

A carbon abatement contract is not required to participate in the Emissions Reduction Fund. ACCUs can be issued to a project without one and sold on the market. Alternatively, a contract can be secured later via participation in an online auction.

This scheme is advantageous as it provides quantitative methods of calculating the additional revenue stream while offering multiple markets and potentially prices for these ACCUs to be sold. This initiative does not provide any form of upfront funding.

It should be noted that eligibility for this fund relies on three main requirements: the project does not commence before it has been registered with the Clean Energy Regulator and a response has been received in writing; the project is not required to be undertaken by or under a Commonwealth, State, or Territory Law; the project is not being undertaken under another Commonwealth, State or Territory government program in the absence of registration under the Emissions Reduction Fund. A Forward Abatement Estimate covering the crediting period for the project is required to secure a carbon abatement contract.

A Crediting period is the timeframe a project is able to claim ACCUs and is 7 years for FE04 HVAC project type. The Reporting period is the timeframe for which a report is prepared for submission to the Clean Energy Regulator and is at discretion of the individual responsible for the project. Maximum reporting period for emissions avoidance projects is every 2 years.

With these (and several other) requirements satisfied, the project outlined in the FE04 HVAC Energy Efficiency Report is suitable for this form of funding and should be applied for as ‘reductions in emission intensity of transport is a valid project type.

NSW Energy Saving Scheme awards financial incentives for organisations investing in energy saving projects that achieve reduced energy consumption by installing, improving, or replacing energy savings equipment. An Accredited Certificate Provider offers energy saving certificates (currency in the scheme) for each notional MWh of energy that investments save which can be sold back to electricity retailers, scheme participants and other intermediary agents.

There are multiple calculation methods that are eligible to determine the about of MWh saved and the method used in the FE04 HVAC Energy Efficiency Report will need to be verified as being an eligible calculation method or modified to utilise on of the eligible methods. Each MWh saved is valued at one ESC to be sold.

Organisations register for this scheme via the ESS Registry, an online database which records the registration, transfer, and surrender of energy savings certificates.

*penalty must be paid if don’t surrender certificates by time
