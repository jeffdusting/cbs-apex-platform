## NAT and enforcement notes.pptx

### Slide 1
Increase compliance through account and product streamlining
Expand toll accounts beyond tolling, creating a network effect that gives the account utility well beyond road charging. This substantially increases the incentive to hold a valid account, as it becomes part of daily life rather than just a toll-payment device.
Expand ability to pay tolls to other retail channels that are more likely to be well managed and maintained by the customer. This should target other services that either use (or could use) an LPN for account identity e.g. parking or loyalty schemes (limited) or digital wallets (PayPal or Apple Pay), or that already monitor a vehicles location e.g. EV charging accounts. Consider dropping VMFs for some of these accounts.
Introduce new products that address accidental non-compliance. This could be pay-as-you-go, video only, mobile phone etc. that does not require ongoing maintenance or up-front funds.
LPN in accounts as mandatory – and automate this now.

The Via Verde model is the strongest global example of compliance-by-design rather than compliance-by-enforcement. The lesson for operators is that when payment is genuinely easier than non-payment — and when the payment infrastructure is embedded in the broader mobility and retail ecosystem — the need for enforcement action diminishes significantly. The tradeoff is that this requires a concession structure that allows the operator to invest in and benefit from that broader ecosystem, which is easier in a national-network context than in a single-road concession.
A 2025 study (US) found, among drivers who received a violation, 63% contacted support or disputed the charge, increasing costs that often exceed the invoice value. The study quantified the cost of friction: chasing payments through enforcement is more expensive than redesigning the payment experience to prevent non-payment in the first place. Over half of drivers say they would use PayPal (55%) or Apple Pay (50%) for toll payments if offered, yet toll agencies have been slow to offer digital wallets despite this demand. 
The gap between preferred payment methods and available payment methods is a measurable, addressable compliance risk – and one that requires investment in payment infrastructure rather than in enforcement.
For example:
How could existing retail and MOU infrastructure support a Tesla charging account?
Could Linkt adopt a lite-touch LPN+Apple Pay product?
How easy is it for a new third-party retail provider to become a retailer in the MOU? E.g. Optus

### Slide 2
Customer Resentment
Route Issue: The penalty grossly exceeds the original obligation. A toll of $2–$9 can escalate into hundreds or thousands of dollars through a process the customer often doesn't understand is happening until it's well advanced. This disproportionality is the root cause of most of the anger, and everything else flows from it.
The per-7-day compounding mechanism is experienced as predatory. The mechanism feels punitive rather than remedial — it doesn't recover the toll debt, it generates a wholly separate criminal liability on top of it.
The Victorian process moves from operator invoice to Victoria Police infringement with relatively little opportunity for the customer to intercept.
In Victoria, non-payment of a toll is a criminal offence for which an infringement record is created.
Extend low cost processes that recover the toll only. Cost neutral mechanism that can be presented as non-punitive, and that targets accidental NAT (account mis-management etc.)
Digitise all notices as much as possible to ensure registered owners are aware early in the process. Include integration with other Gov Services (ServiceNSW)
Lobby to decriminalise toll non-payments

### Slide 3
Customer Resentment (cont.)
Administration fees that compound faster than most customers expect. Administration charges are often more than the toll charge itself and are added to each unpaid toll. These can quickly build up to a large amount. 
Both states rely heavily on postal mail to addresses held by the transport authority. The deeper issue is that both systems were designed primarily from the operator's perspective — to recover money efficiently — rather than from the customer's perspective, where the primary experience is surprise, disproportionality, and opacity.
NSW's parallel enforcement tracks — the operator's civil debt pathway and Revenue NSW's penalty notice pathway — create genuine confusion for customers about who to deal with. 
Customers who contact the toll operator after a penalty notice has been issued sometimes find the operator can no longer help them in the way they expected. The handover point is opaque from the customer's perspective, and navigating two separate bureaucracies with different processes, timelines, and hardship criteria is genuinely difficult.
Stat Dec process is overly burdensome
Consolidate toll notices to reduce admin fees.
Make it easier and quicker to nominate alternative drivers or challenge a toll. If someone can show evidence, can the toll-notice be reversed there and then?


### Slide 4
Customer Resentment (cont.)
The deeper issue is that both systems were designed primarily from the operator's perspective — to recover money efficiently — rather than from the customer's perspective, where the primary experience is surprise, disproportionality, and opacity. 
The customers who generate the most enforcement activity are often not wilful evaders but inadvertent defaulters who fall behind and then cannot catch up once the fee structure compounds.
Designing the system to identify and re-engage those customers early — before the first administration fee, before the first infringement, before the first police notice — would likely reduce both enforcement volume and customer resentment simultaneously. 
That is a different operating model from the one either state currently operates, and it is closer to what the best performers in global case studies have built.

### Slide 6
Hypothesis
Transurban is spending enormous amounts of money operating a collection and enforcement function that is not its core business, using tools (notices, fees, police referrals) that are expensive, slow, and customer-hostile.
Abolish the Toll Notice Entirely — Move to Pure Account-Based Billing
Surrender the Enforcement Function Entirely to Government — Become a Pure Revenue Collector
Sell the Debt Immediately — At Origin, Not After Escalation
Essentially a clearing house with the state taking accountability for debt
Make Non-Payment Structurally Impossible Through Embedded Finance
Link a payment credential to a registration, Non-payment becomes a matter between the motorist and their bank
A Tiered Amnesty Engine — Running Continuously, Not as One-Off Events
The system continuously identifies accounts with debt above a threshold age, scores the likelihood of recovery based on vehicle type, registration history, account behaviour, and other signals, and makes automated settlement offers — declining percentage of fees waived as debt ages, with the floor being full debt.
Outsource Enforcement to the Insurance Industry
