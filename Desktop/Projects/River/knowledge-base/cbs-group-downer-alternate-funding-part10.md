1               HVAC Energy Efficiency Hardware Fleet Enhancement
1.1             Executive Summary
The train’s Climate Control System (CCS) has one of the most significant impacts to customer and driver
comfort. The current HVAC units on the Waratah and SGT Fleet are a proven design with a sound
performance and reliability history. However, from an operational cost and environmental impact
perspective, the amount of energy used to operate the Heating, Ventilation, and Air Conditioning (HVAC)
system is a significant factor.
The HVAC hardware design has been in service for more than 10 years utilising technology that by
today's standards, is no longer energy efficient. Over this period the technology and reliability of rolling
stock HVAC units and their hardware components have improved allowing Downer to offer an
enhancement that will deliver significant benefits in energy efficiency and optimisation.
Downer and our HVAC supply partner Sigma have analysed operational service data logs to develop
simulation models using today's latest proven energy-efficient technology. The study showed significant
improvements in energy efficiency with a corresponding reduction in greenhouse gases with these new
components. These outcomes can be achieved without affecting the system cooling/heating
performance or modifying temperature control strategies.
Downer and Sigma propose three hardware enhancements that can be implemented as part of a
Hardware Energy Efficient HVAC (HEE HVAC) without major impact to the HVAC configuration or
performance. These are:
1. Replacing the existing condenser coil with a coil utilising 5mm diameter tubing;
2. Replacing the existing condenser fan with EC motor technology;
3. Replacing existing supply air fan with EC motor technology.
Based on the simulations, Sydney Trains can save between 16.5 and 26.6 GWh in energy per year
across the Waratah and SGT Fleets (119) with a corresponding reduction in emissions equivalent to
13,860 – 22,344 tonnes of CO2.
Further energy savings have been identified through making HVAC software control updates, and these
will be outlined in Fleet Enhancement proposal FE04 HVAC Energy Efficiency – Software.

1.2             Scope
This proposal outlines the program of work to complete the design and verification of the hardware
design. This will be achieved through:
   Finalising the engineering, designing and building the HEE HVAC prototype;
   Testing that the prototype design meets the required energy performance outcomes in a controlled
    climate chamber;
   Running an operational trial on an SGT Set over a 6-month period.
This work will verify the proposed solution:
   Achieves the targeted energy savings;
   Can be cost effectively upgraded;
   Maintains or exceeds specified heating and cooling performance;
   Achieves the required reliability in-service;
   Can deliver similar energy savings for Millennium through component hardware upgrades.

four key objectives:
1. The HEE HVAC solution can be implemented on Waratah and SGT with no change to the train side
   configuration;
2. The predicted energy savings can be proven quantitatively;
3. There is no reduction in performance at any of the specified environmental condition;

Downer is proposing three component enhancements that can be implemented without major change to
the HVAC configuration:
    Replace the existing condenser coil with a coil utilising 5mm diameter tubing;
    Replace the existing condenser fan with EC motor technology;
    Replace existing supply air fan with EC motor technology.

4.1             Interfaces (Other Systems and Sub-Systems)

The component upgrades do not change the functionality and performance of the HVAC during normal

4.4             Specialist Engineering Review Input (Initial Human Factors Review)
The component upgrades are all internal within the HVAC and therefore have no impact to Human

4.5             Reliability
Based on the EC fan supplier responses, components have equivalent MTBF. Sigma have experience
with EC fans used for these functions on other projects.

4.6             Technical Dependencies and Constraints
The component upgrades currently do not have any significant technical dependencies and constraints.

4.7             Technical Assumptions Made as Part of the Enhancement Proposal
Three key technical assumptions have been discussed between Downer and Sigma:
1. There is a need to conduct cooling capacity type testing to verify the major component upgrades.
   Typing testing will be undertaken in Sigma’s climate chamber and ‘Similarity’ proposals will be
   provided to minimise on train testing. EMC Type Testing will be undertaken for the new components;
2. The condenser coil and condenser fan are considered one system and therefore both will be
   upgraded;
3. Whilst the EC motor fans provide immediate energy savings, the simulations only consider the
   current fixed speed application.

4.8             Detailed Design Engineering Scope
Minimal detailed design engineering required as the Fleet Enhancement focuses on straight replacement
of the major components.

5               Implementation
5.1             Scope
The scope of the HEE HVAC Hardware proposal will be delivered in the Design Phase. The Design
Phase will include an operational trial on a SGT Set for a 6-month period.
The roadmap for implementation is illustrated in Figure 3.




                                          Figure 3: Design Phase Roadmap
Downer and Sigma have developed a low-risk implementation strategy to ensure that the transition of
hardware changes from the Baseline HVAC design to the new HEE HVAC design has minimal impact to
Sydney Trains operations and fleet availability.
The plan involves testing the new design as a prototype in the laboratory (Climate Chamber) under
controlled conditions from mid-2020 and if successful, operationally trialling 4 prototype units in-service
from Q3 2020.
The timing for the Design Phase assumes the Early Works Program (EWP) rolls into the Design Phase
from 3 February 2020 to optimise design delivery timeframes and maintain momentum.
If required, Downer proposes that the EWP is extended through February to continue developing the
SDR should the variation require a period of negotiation and/or clarification. The approved variation will
be adjusted to account for this period.
The design process will progress from SDR to DDR to manage the degree of change proposed. Downer
is not planning to complete a PDR as the design is internal to the HVAC and does impact the Train Level
Systems.
Once the performance predictions are proven under laboratory conditions, an operational trial will be
conducted to verify the performance in-service. The trials will be evaluated, and the results used to
inform completion of the Design Phase. Based on the success of the trial a Go/No Go decision will be
made on whether to continue, modify or cancel the remaining Design Phase work.

5.3.3           Operational Train Trials (In-Service)
Following static trials, in-service trials will validate HVAC power consumption and ongoing operation in
actual operational scenarios.
Data collected from the HEE HVAC and Baseline HVAC installed on the same train will enable a direct
comparison of the results since the single train will experience the same environmental variables.
Analysis of on-train power data will be provided by Downer’s own TrainDNA software and additional data
logging hardware installed on monitored HVACs. Data collected will include:
     HVAC unit status;
     Power consumption.
With TfNSW and Sydney Trains' support, Downer will run operational trials over a 6-month period to
include one transitional season and one summer period. The HEE HVAC will operate in all operational
settings (peak/off-peak; running/terminal/stabling; 20-24/7) and across a wide range of environmental
conditions (city/suburban temperatures; tunnels/above ground).
