Overview:

From the FE04 HVAC Energy Efficiency Report there are two main advantages to the climate system in terms of savings; reduction of carbon emission and reduction of energy consumption. These four initiatives cater towards these advantages and together, allow Downer to maximise the reward for implementing these changes in the Waratah fleet.

The FED Emissions Reduction Fund rewards businesses for a reduction in CO2 emission by awarding an Australian Carbon Credit Unit (ACCU) per tonne of Carbon Dioxide Equivalent (tCO2-e) stored or avoided by the project. These Carbon Credits can then be sold to the government via a carbon abatement contract or sold on the secondary market. Via this method, an additional revenue stream of $166,320 to $268,128 could be added as a result of the HVAC upgrade. These prices are indicative and can vary depending on the demand set by the government via a carbon abatement contract, or the supply and demand in the secondary market.

A carbon abatement contract is not required to participate in the Emissions Reduction Fund. ACCUs can be issued to a project without one and sold on the market. Alternatively, a contract can be secured later via participation in an online auction.

This scheme is advantageous as it provides quantitative methods of calculating the additional revenue stream while offering multiple markets and potentially prices for these ACCUs to be sold. This initiative does not provide any form of upfront funding.

It should be noted that eligibility for this fund relies on three main requirements: the project does not commence before it has been registered with the Clean Energy Regulator and a response has been received in writing; the project is not required to be undertaken by or under a Commonwealth, State, or Territory Law; the project is not being undertaken under another Commonwealth, State or Territory government program in the absence of registration under the Emissions Reduction Fund. A Forward Abatement Estimate covering the crediting period for the project is required to secure a carbon abatement contract.

A Crediting period is the timeframe a project is able to claim ACCUs and is 7 years for FE04 HVAC project type. The Reporting period is the timeframe for which a report is prepared for submission to the Clean Energy Regulator and is at discretion of the individual responsible for the project. Maximum reporting period for emissions avoidance projects is every 2 years.

With these (and several other) requirements satisfied, the project outlined in the FE04 HVAC Energy Efficiency Report is suitable for this form of funding and should be applied for as ‘reductions in emission intensity of transport is a valid project type.

The Clean Energy Innovation Fund is the largest dedicated Australian investor in its sector that targets technologies and businesses beyond the research and development stage which can benefit from early stage seed of growth capital to help them progress to the next stage of their development. The fund uses CEFC financing to provide primarily equity finance to innovative clean energy projects and business which involve renewable, energy efficiency and low emissions technologies. 

The Sustainable Cities Investment Program as a subsection of this fund invests to accelerate the development and deployment in clean energy projects in Australia’s 50 largest cities, having committed 2.9 billion to 5000 Sustainable Cities opportunities as of 30 June 2019. Finance can be used across the urban environment to delivery clean energy benefits to a range of sector including low emissions transport and energy efficient large-scale economic infrastructure, and to encourage the switch to energy efficient vehicles.

The eligibility of the project and amount of funding is at the discretion of the CEFC and can take the form of direct investments, investments in investment funds, and specialist asset finance programs. Financing varies from $10,000 to 5 million for small scale projects and $20 million and above for large-scale projects and each opportunity is considered on a case by case basis. 

In scope of the FE04 HVAC Energy Efficiency Report, this source of funding would be advantageous as an upfront method of minimising the costs before the project begins. However, funds cannot be estimated, and it is uncertain whether the HVAC upgrade projects fits with the Clean Energy Innovation Fund’s ethos which mainly targets businesses that are not yet established or of sufficient maturity, size or otherwise ready to attract sufficient private sector capital.

Consequently, the FE04 HVAC Energy Efficiency is eligible as an Australian-based investment that is attempting to implement energy efficient and low emission technologies. However, the nature of the project and Downer as a company is less compatible with the fund’s investing strategy and eligibility is therefore unlikely.

Energy Performance Contracting (EPC) relies on contracting an Energy Service Company (ESCO) to evaluate the defined project and its scope, and provide recommendations into cost-effective improvements, work to implement recommendations, and guarantee the resulting savings will cover all project costs. The Energy Performance Contract is negotiated with the Energy Service Company and the savings from the recommendations cover the project costs within 3 to 7 years.

This form of finance provides beneficial upfront funding which can lower/eliminate the HVAC upgrade installation costs. Funds are allocated based on eligibility determined by the Energy Service Company.

The literature suggests that this source of funding is exclusively for a large property or portfolio of properties but should be confirmed by an ESCO.

NSW Energy Saving Scheme awards financial incentives for organisations investing in energy saving projects that achieve reduced energy consumption by installing, improving, or replacing energy savings equipment. An Accredited Certificate Provider offers energy saving certificates (currency in the scheme) for each notional MWh of energy that investments save which can be sold back to electricity retailers,* who are mandatory scheme participants and purchase these to meet their own legislated targets.

There are multiple calculation methods that are eligible [*does the one Downer used apply?] to determine the about of MWh saved. Each MWh saved is valued at one ESC.

Organisations register for this scheme via the ESS Registry, an online database which records the registration, transfer, and surrender of energy savings certificates.

*penalty must be paid if don’t surrender certificates by time
