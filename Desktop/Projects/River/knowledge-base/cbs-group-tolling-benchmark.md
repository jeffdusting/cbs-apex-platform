Methodology and Scope

Introduction

Data Sources

This analysis draws on three categories of data:

Primary sources (toll industry): The primary toll industry data comprises IBTTA (International Bridge, Tunnel and Turnpike Association) technical reports, operator annual reports and ASX/PSE filings (Transurban, 407 ETR, Atlantia), State Department of Transportation reports (E-ZPass agencies), and NHAI (National Highways Authority of India) operational data.

Secondary sources (adjacent industries): Adjacent industry data includes IPART determinations (Australian utilities), TfL (Transport for London) annual reports, and various government service agency performance reports.

Internal data (where indicated): Certain benchmarks draw on operational data from TfNSW contracts, clearly marked as internal data in the companion Excel model.

Range Derivation Methodology

Cost ranges that inform this benchmarking are derived using three methods, depending on the data available:

Observed ranges: Where multiple data points exist (e.g., operator cost ratios), ranges reflect the spread of observed values with ±2 percentage point uncertainty bands applied to account for reporting variations.

Calculated ranges: Where component costs are calculated from aggregated data, low and high estimates reflect conservative and optimistic assumptions about cost allocation.

Estimated ranges: Where direct data is unavailable (e.g., next-gen ANPR operational costs), ranges are derived from contractual specifications, engineering estimates, and analogous technology performance, and are clearly identified as estimates.

Benchmarking Limitations

The following limitations apply to international comparisons and should be considered when interpreting all data presented in this report:

Accounting variations: Operators use different definitions of operating costs, with some capitalising maintenance expenditure. Revenue recognition methods also differ regarding non-toll ancillary revenue.

Scale effects: Operators managing 500+ million transactions annually achieve costs unrealistic for operators at 50 - 100 million transactions. Operators should benchmark against peers with comparable transaction volumes, and comparable interoperability requirements.

Regulatory context: EU operators face more stringent safety and environmental requirements than Asian or Australasian operators. Israeli operators face unique security requirements. Regional regulations on license plate design, format an quality will also impact.

Currency and timing: All costs converted to AUD at December 2024 rates. Operator data reflects FY23 or FY24 results where available. Purchasing power parity adjustments have not been applied, which may affect the comparability of cost levels (as opposed to cost ratios) across jurisdictions.

Scope definition variations: Operators define cost boundaries differently. Some contracts bundle passage detection, back office, and commercial functions into single service fees (e.g., Dartford), while others separate infrastructure maintenance from tolling operations. Direct comparison of operating cost ratios requires understanding what costs are included in each operator's reported figures.

Insourced vs outsourced services: Some operators outsource customer service, compliance, or IT functions while others maintain in-house capabilities. Outsourced functions may be reported differently (as service fees vs operating costs) affecting ratio comparability. Staffing metrics (FTE per transaction) should specify whether outsourced labour is included.

Toll purpose and pricing structure: Tolling systems serve different purposes - infrastructure cost recovery, demand management, or congestion pricing - which influence operating models. Demand management systems (e.g., congestion charges) typically have different transaction patterns and customer behaviours than fixed-rate infrastructure tolls, affecting cost profiles.

Sample size: The ten-operator sample, while geographically and technologically diverse, does not constitute a statistically robust dataset. Cost ranges represent analytical estimates rather than confidence intervals. Individual operator data points may be subject to reporting variations that cannot be fully normalised.

Glossary of Key Terms

AVI (Automatic Vehicle Identification): The detection technology used to identify vehicles at toll points, encompassing both tag-based systems (RFID/DSRC) and video-based systems (ANPR).

Tag-based systems: Electronic toll collection using vehicle-mounted transponders, including RFID (Radio-Frequency Identification, typically 915 MHz passive tags in Americas/Australia) and DSRC (Dedicated Short-Range Communications, typically 5.8 GHz active transponders in Europe). Both require roadside reader infrastructure.

MIR (Manual Image Review): The process of human operators reviewing vehicle images that cannot be automatically processed by OCR systems. MIR rate is a key efficiency metric - lower MIR rates indicate higher automation and lower operating costs.

Trip reconstruction: The back-office process of linking vehicle entry and exit detection events into complete toll journeys, particularly relevant for distance-based or point-to-point tolling systems.

Emerging Technology: Next-Generation ANPR

The international benchmarks presented above reflect legacy and first-generation technology deployments. Recent contract specifications for next-generation ANPR demonstrate that the technology fundamentally changes the cost equation, potentially positioning video-only systems as more cost-effective than tag-based systems.

Case Study: Western Harbour Tunnel (Sydney, Australia)

The Western Harbour Tunnel contract, executed in 2025, specifies next-generation video-only detection with ambitious performance requirements. These represent contractual targets that, if achieved, would demonstrate capabilities beyond current industry norms:

Technology: Video-only (100% ANPR, no tag-based infrastructure).

Automation rate: Expected to exceed 99.5%.

Leakage: Near zero (eliminating the 2 - 5% error rate attributed to legacy video systems).

Equipment: Lead-installed, purpose-built infrastructure (not retrofit).

This will represent one of the first major deployments targeting next-generation ANPR performance at this specification level in a high-volume urban environment. Actual operational performance will need to be validated once the asset enters service.

ANPR performance in adverse conditions: Automation rates for ANPR systems are affected by environmental conditions including heavy rain, fog, low-angle sun glare, and night-time operation. Next-generation systems mitigate these through multi-spectral imaging (visible + infrared), high frame rates (120+ fps), and AI-enhanced processing. However, published automation targets typically reflect average conditions rather than worst-case performance. Operators should specify minimum automation rates under defined adverse conditions (e.g., heavy rain, night-time) alongside average-condition targets.

Motorcycle detection: Motorcycle identification remains a specific challenge for ANPR systems due to rear-mounted plates with variable positioning, smaller plate formats, and greater positional variability within the camera field of view. Current-generation ANPR systems typically achieve 90–95% automation for motorcycles, compared with 99.5%+ for standard passenger vehicles.

Industry Implications

The traditional narrative that "tag-based systems achieve 30 - 45% lower operating costs than video" is based on legacy systems with 95 - 98% accuracy requiring significant manual review, 2 - 5% leakage rates driving compliance costs, and complex customer identification for unregistered vehicles. Next-generation ANPR addresses these structural disadvantages through automation rates exceeding 99.5% that eliminate manual review overhead, near-zero leakage that eliminates revenue loss and dispute costs, and universal detection (all vehicles captured) that simplifies customer identification.

Strategic Implications for Operators and Asset Owners

The analysis supports four strategic positions differentiated by operator type:

New deployments: Next-generation ANPR should be the default technology consideration for greenfield toll facilities. The evidence suggests video-only systems can achieve equal or better operating costs than tag-based systems whilst eliminating tag management complexity. However, operators should specify performance guarantees under adverse conditions and ensure contractual protections if automation targets are not met in operations.

Existing tag-based networks: Operators with mature tag networks should continue leveraging this investment, but should not assume tag superiority when evaluating expansions or replacements. The transition path from tag-based to ANPR-primary requires careful management given that millions of active tags and associated customer accounts are currently in circulation.

Hybrid operators: Operators currently running hybrid tag/video systems should evaluate shifting transaction mix toward video as next-generation ANPR becomes available, potentially retiring tag infrastructure over time. This transition involves managing existing tag customers alongside new ANPR-processed transactions, and should be phased over 3 - 5 years.

Procurement specifications: Asset owners and government agencies should update technology specifications to mandate next-generation ANPR performance standards (99.5%+ automation, near-zero leakage) rather than defaulting to tag-based requirements.

Customer Cost Implications of Technology Choice

Technology selection has direct implications for customer costs and equity considerations that extend beyond operator economics.

Video surcharges: Operators using hybrid tag/video systems typically impose surcharges on video-tolled (non-tagged) transactions to recover higher processing costs and incentivise tag adoption. These surcharges range from modest cost-recovery fees ($0.50 - $2.00 per transaction) to substantial revenue-generating charges ($3.00 - $10.00 per transaction). The latter approach, while commercially attractive, raises equity concerns as it disproportionately affects occasional users, visitors, and lower-income motorists who may not maintain toll accounts.

Tag costs: Tag-based systems impose direct costs on customers including tag purchase/deposit ($10 - $25), account establishment fees, and minimum balance requirements. While regular users amortise these costs over many trips, they create barriers for occasional users.

Equity considerations: Next-generation ANPR potentially offers more equitable pricing by eliminating the tag/video pricing differential and treating all motorists consistently. However, this benefit only materialises if operators choose not to impose account-based discounts that replicate the tag/video pricing gap. Asset owners and regulators should consider customer cost implications alongside operating efficiency when specifying technology requirements.

Digital accessibility: Not all motorists can or will use digital channels. Operators achieving high digital adoption rates must maintain accessible alternatives for customers who are elderly, have limited digital literacy, have disabilities affecting app use, or lack smartphone access. An industry benchmark of 70 - 80% digital adoption implicitly acknowledges that 20 - 30% of customers will continue to require non-digital service channels. Operators should budget for this residual non-digital population rather than assuming 100% migration.

Tolling Product Cost Benchmarks

The preceding analysis benchmarks technology platforms and value chain components. However, the customer-facing product mix (the types of tolling products offered and their relative adoption) is a primary driver of operating cost ratios that the technology analysis alone cannot explain. An operator with 90% tag account penetration and 10% casual tolling has a fundamentally different cost base than one with 60% tag accounts and 40% invoice-based casual tolling, even if both deploy identical detection infrastructure.

This section classifies the principal tolling product types, benchmarks their cost-to-serve, and analyses how product mix influences the overall operating cost ratio.

Product Classification Framework

Tolling products can be classified along three dimensions: whether the customer holds an account, whether the customer carries detection hardware, and whether the charge is distance-based or time-based. The distinguishing characteristic for cost-to-serve purposes is the degree of per-transaction processing required. Products at the top of the list (tag accounts) require detection, matching, and account posting for every trip but carry low per-transaction marginal costs once the account infrastructure is established. Products in the middle (casual/invoice) require the most intensive per-transaction processing because each trip triggers owner identification, invoice generation, postage, and payment collection. Products at the bottom (vignettes and congestion charges) convert variable transaction costs into fixed-period access rights, fundamentally altering the cost structure.

Three observations emerge from this cost comparison:

The per-transaction cost of $1.50–$6.00 for casual tolling reflects owner identification (vehicle register lookup at $0.50–$1.50 per query), invoice generation and postage ($1.50–$3.00 per notice), payment processing, and follow-up collection. Digital vignettes achieve the lowest per-transaction cost of any product category. One operator achieves relatively low operating costs in part because the vignette model for some vehicles eliminates per-transaction costs for the majority of network users.

Interoperable passes carry the highest account maintenance costs due to multi-operator settlement, cross-border data exchange, and OBU lifecycle management. The European EETS framework, now supporting 19 registered providers across the EU, requires toll chargers and service providers to maintain standardised back-office interfaces, reconcile cross-border transactions, and manage interoperability compliance. These costs are partially offset by reduced enforcement overhead (registered OBU users have near-zero evasion rates).

Vignette Model Analysis

The European vignette model warrants dedicated analysis as an alternative tolling product architecture with fundamentally different cost economics to the per-trip models prevalent in Australia, North America, and parts of Asia.

Overview and Adoption

Nine European countries currently operate vignette systems for light vehicles: Austria, Bulgaria, Czech Republic, Hungary, Romania, Slovakia, Slovenia, Switzerland, and (partially) Latvia. The trend across these markets is strongly towards digital vignettes (electronic permits linked to licence plate numbers) replacing physical windscreen stickers. Austria, which introduced its digital vignette in 2018, will discontinue physical stickers entirely from 2027. Hungary, Czech Republic, Slovakia, Slovenia, Romania, and Bulgaria have already transitioned to fully electronic systems.

Cost Structure Advantages

The vignette model's cost efficiency derives from four structural characteristics:

Elimination of per-transaction processing. The most significant advantage. Once a vignette is purchased, no further transaction processing occurs for the validity period. There is no gantry-triggered detection event, no account lookup, no transaction posting, and no billing cycle. The operator's variable cost per trip approaches zero for vignette-holding vehicles.

Simplified customer acquisition. Digital vignettes are purchased through web portals, mobile apps, or retail partners with no account establishment, no hardware distribution, no minimum balance, and no credit assessment. ASFINAG's digital vignette requires only a licence plate number, email address, and payment — a sub-two-minute process. Customer acquisition cost is estimated at $0.50–$2.00 versus $15–$40 for tag account products.

Enforcement-based compliance model. Rather than detecting and billing every trip, vignette systems enforce compliance through periodic ANPR spot-checks — fixed cameras at borders and key interchanges, supplemented by mobile enforcement units. Non-compliant vehicles face immediate penalties (Austria: €120 substitute toll on the spot, up to €3,000 for contested violations). The high penalty-to-price ratio creates strong compliance incentives, and the enforcement cost is spread across a small non-compliant minority rather than applied to every transaction.

Revenue predictability. Annual vignette purchases provide revenue visibility and reduce the cash-flow variability associated with per-trip tolling. Subscription-based renewal (ASFINAG offers auto-renewal for annual digital vignettes) further reduces churn and re-acquisition costs.

Limitations and Trade-offs

The vignette model carries limitations that constrain its applicability:

No distance-based revenue correlation. A motorist who drives 500 km on the motorway network pays the same vignette fee as one who drives 5,000 km. This creates cross-subsidisation between light and heavy users and may be perceived as inequitable. Several European jurisdictions address this by applying vignettes only to light vehicles whilst charging heavy vehicles on a distance basis (ASFINAG's GO-Box, Germany's Toll Collect, Czech Republic's satellite-based HGV system).

Limited demand management capability. Unlike variable per-trip pricing, vignettes cannot be used for congestion management, time-of-day pricing, or route-specific demand signals. Operators seeking to influence traffic patterns require per-trip charging mechanisms.

Transit fairness concerns. Short-duration vignettes (1-day, 10-day) partially address this, but a motorist transiting a country for a single 50 km stretch pays the same as one using the full network for the validity period.

Revenue growth constraints. Vignette price increases are typically regulated and linked to inflation indices, providing less revenue flexibility than per-trip toll escalation mechanisms tied to CPI or concession agreements.

Relevance to Australian Context

Australia's current tolling model — per-trip, tag-based, with casual video tolling for non-account holders — does not employ vignettes. However, the vignette concept has potential application in specific contexts:

Visitor and occasional user products. A time-limited digital pass (analogous to a vignette) could replace the high-cost casual/invoice product for visitors and occasional users. Linkt's existing Sydney Pass product is a partial step in this direction, but retains per-trip processing rather than providing blanket access.

Toll cap implementation. The NSW Government's $60 weekly toll cap, introduced in January 2024, functions as a de facto vignette for heavy users — once the cap is reached, additional trips incur no marginal cost (and no marginal processing cost). Extending this concept to pre-purchased weekly or monthly digital passes could reduce both customer cost and operator processing cost.

Interoperability simplification. A national or state-level digital vignette for light vehicles could simplify the multi-operator, multi-account complexity that currently characterises Australian tolling, where motorists in Sydney may interact with Linkt (Transurban), E-Toll (Transport for NSW), and potentially other operators on a single journey.

Fleet and Commercial Products

Fleet and commercial accounts represent a distinct product category with materially different cost economics to retail accounts. These accounts are characterised by multi-vehicle registrations (typically 5–500+ vehicles per account), consolidated monthly billing, and predictable, high-frequency usage patterns.

The cost advantages of fleet products include consolidated billing (one invoice for multiple vehicles reduces per-transaction billing cost by 60–80%), reduced customer service intensity (fleet managers self-serve via portal; enquiry rates 50–70% lower than retail per vehicle), lower compliance risk (commercial operators have strong payment incentives due to registration renewal linkage and business reputation), and tag lifecycle efficiency (bulk tag issuance, centralised distribution, and systematic replacement reduce per-tag management cost).

The estimated cost-to-serve for fleet accounts is $0.06–$0.10 per transaction — 30–50% lower than equivalent retail tag accounts. However, fleet accounts typically attract negotiated toll discounts of 5–15%, partially offsetting the cost advantage for operators. The net margin on fleet products is generally comparable to retail products, but the lower cost-to-serve contributes to operational efficiency at scale.

Product Strategy Implications

The product cost benchmarks in this section suggest three strategic levers available to operators seeking to reduce operating cost ratios:

Lever 1: Casual-to-account migration. Converting casual/invoice customers to account-based products delivers the single largest per-transaction cost reduction available — from $1.50–$6.00 to $0.08–$0.22 per transaction. For an operator processing 100 million annual transactions with 20% casual penetration, migrating half of casual users to video accounts at $0.17 per transaction would reduce annual operating costs by approximately $25–55 million. Strategies include reducing account setup friction (mobile-first registration), eliminating or reducing account fees, offering first-trip-free incentives for account registration, and implementing progressive pricing that makes account products visibly cheaper than casual tolling.

Lever 2: Digital vignette or pass products for occasional users. For users who toll infrequently (fewer than 10 trips per month), the cost-to-serve exceeds the revenue from individual trips under casual/invoice models. Offering time-limited digital passes — purchasable via mobile app with no account setup — captures this segment at vignette-equivalent cost ($0.50–$2.00 acquisition, near-zero per-trip processing) whilst providing revenue certainty. This approach is particularly relevant for tourist traffic, airport-proximate toll roads, and occasional suburban commuters. Operator’s should note the limitations and downsides when considering this strategy.

Lever 3: Product rationalisation and simplification. Many operators offer multiple overlapping products with different fee structures, terms, and channels. Linkt's Sydney product suite includes tag accounts, tagless accounts, Sydney Passes, and commercial accounts — each with distinct fee schedules (vehicle matching fees of $0.55–$0.75, startup fees of $1.50–$3.30, toll notice fees of $10–20, and toll notice transfer fees of $1.10–$2.90). Simplifying to fewer, clearly differentiated products reduces customer confusion, support enquiry volume, and back-office complexity. The vignette model demonstrates that a single, simple product (digital permit linked to licence plate) can achieve best-in-class operating cost ratios.

These product strategy levers operate independently of, and are complementary to, the technology and mobile app strategies analysed in Parts 3 and 5. An operator implementing next-generation ANPR (Part 3, Section 3.4) alongside a casual-to-account migration programme and a digital pass product for occasional users would capture cost reductions across all three dimensions simultaneously.

Fleet Characteristics

Vehicle fleet characteristics impact ANPR accuracy and associated manual review costs across three dimensions.

Plate design standardisation: European plates follow standardised designs with consistent fonts, sizes, and contrast ratios, enabling higher OCR accuracy (typically 99%+). US plates vary significantly by state with diverse designs, colours, and fonts, reducing OCR accuracy (typically 96 - 98%). Australian plates fall between these extremes with state-based variation but generally consistent sizing.

International vehicle mix: Jurisdictions with high proportions of foreign-registered vehicles face increased owner-lookup complexity and costs. UK crossings (Dartford, Mersey) manage significant European vehicle volumes; Australian urban toll roads have minimal international vehicles. This factor can add 1 - 2 percentage points to compliance costs for high international-traffic locations.

Vehicle class complexity: Mixed fleet compositions (cars, trucks, motorcycles, buses) require more sophisticated classification systems. Motorcycle detection remains challenging for video-based systems due to rear-mounted plates and variable positioning. The cost impact ranges from 2 - 4 percentage points depending on fleet composition and plate standardisation.

Tag Lifecycle Cost Sensitivity

The ANPR versus tag-based cost comparison in Section 3.4.2 attributes $0.006–$0.011 per transaction to tag-related costs (hardware, distribution logistics, and replacement/maintenance). These per-transaction figures are derived from an assumed tag lifecycle cost of $10–$15 per tag, amortised over the tag's useful life. This assumption has not been validated against current operator pricing and is sensitive to several variables.

The following table models the impact of different tag lifecycle cost assumptions on the total cost comparison:

Key finding: The ANPR cost advantage is present across all tag cost scenarios but the magnitude varies. If large-scale operators achieve tag lifecycle costs below $10 (through manufacturing volume and efficient distribution), the ANPR advantage narrows to 5–12%. Conversely, operators using active DSRC transponders ($20–$25 per unit) face a wider cost gap, strengthening the case for ANPR transition. Variables affecting tag lifecycle cost: Tag lifecycle cost is driven by unit manufacturing cost (sensitive to order volume and technology type), distribution channel costs (retail pickup versus postal delivery versus installer network), replacement rates (typically 15–20% annually for passive RFID, higher for battery-dependent active transponders), and end-of-life disposal or recycling costs. Operators with established high-volume tag programmes may achieve costs at the lower end of the range, whilst operators entering tag-based systems or managing smaller tag populations will incur higher per-unit costs.

Introduction

The global toll road industry face increasing pressure to optimise operational efficiency whilst maintaining performance, service quality and regulatory compliance. 

This report provides benchmarking data for toll operators, asset owners, concessionaires, and government transport agencies seeking to assess performance, identify improvement opportunities, and establish realistic cost targets. It draws on data from international toll operators, adjacent industry benchmarks from comparable labour markets, and emerging technology analysis from next-generation deployment specifications.

International Variance

Operating costs (as a proportion of toll revenue, and across the value chain) vary significantly across international operators reflecting variations across, for example, pricing elasticity, technology, scale, climate, and operational maturity. Our analysis has recognised the following factors that complicate direct international comparisons and make it challenging to establish clear operational efficiency targets.

Accounting and reporting variations: Different operators use varying definitions of operating costs, with some capitalising maintenance expenditure whilst others expense it immediately. Revenue recognition methods also differ, particularly regarding whether non-toll ancillary revenue is included in the denominator.

Technology transition costs: Many operators are mid-transition between collection technologies, creating temporary cost inflation. The shift from toll-booth to free-flow and from tag-based to video-based systems, or from mixed to pure electronic tolling, generates significant one-off implementation costs that distort year-on-year comparisons. These transition periods can last 3 to 5 years.

Technology mix: Hybrid tag/video systems typically achieve 28% to 35% operating costs (as a percentage of revenue) versus 22% to 24% for pure tag-based networks or 32% to 40% for legacy video-only systems. However, next-generation ANPR technology fundamentally changes this equation.

Emerging technology advantage: Recent contracts for next-generation video-only detection specify automation rates expected to exceed 99.5% and near-zero leakage, eliminating the structural disadvantages of legacy video systems. This positions advanced ANPR as potentially more cost-effective than tag-based systems for new deployments.

Labour environment: Labour costs vary dramatically across markets. Australian and European operators face costs 3 to 4 times higher than Asian operators and 1.5 to 2 times higher than North American operators, creating structural cost premiums of 5 to 8 percentage points.

Sample and methodology limitations: The sample used, while geographically diverse, is insufficient to establish statistically definitive ranges. Ranges presented in this report reflect analytical estimates informed by available data rather than confidence intervals from large samples. Readers should treat ranges as indicative and test assumptions against their own operational data.

Pricing and elasticity: The level of toll set for each asset will ultimately drive the calculated operational efficiency when compared across operators. However, the elasticity of toll pricing is such that the impact is not as evident as expected with other levers as described above having a greater impact. Ultimately, the comparison of costs across the four value chain components is more insightful (and not impacted by price).

Operating Cost Benchmarks

Our operating cost benchmarking data considers two core dimensions: the Efficiency Tier of operators (based on structural characteristics and assessed by operational costs as a percentage of revenue) and the value chain components (separating the high-level stages through tolling and assessed by individual component costs as a percentage of overall operating costs). 

Efficiency Tiers

Three Efficiency Tiers emerge from international benchmarking that operators can use for self-assessment and target-setting based on their specific operating context. Efficiency Tiers are defined as:

Tier 1 (High Efficiency): Highest transaction volumes across integrated networks or national systems, primarily mature tag and video based systems.

Tier 2 (Moderate Efficiency): High transactions across multiple assets or large single concessions, hybrid technology and established operations.

Tier 3 (Developing Efficiency): Lower transaction volumes across single concessions, mixed technologies and less mature operations.

Key findings related to Operating Costs

Analysing operational tiers and value chain components provides valuable insight for toll operators, asset owners, concessionaires, and government transport agencies seeking to benchmark themselves against the global industry, and more importantly identify and prioritise initiatives to drive value from operations, optimise scale opportunities, or position for new assets and operations.

The below summarises the key findings from a generic assessment of benchmark data. However, greater value is achieved with a comparison to specific local data. 

Retail is the single biggest cost driver, and the biggest opportunity

Retail is consistently the largest component across all Tiers (around 33% of total operating costs) and has the widest performance range of any component (~6.5% gap between best and worst performers). A Tier 3 operator running an inefficient retail function is spending 16.8% of revenue on it alone, versus 6.6% for a best-in-class Tier 1, or a 2.5x difference. For any operator with a reform agenda, retail should be the first target.

The total cost gap between tiers is substantial but the "maturity premium" at best-in-class is small

Tier 1 operators run at ~27% of revenue versus ~37% for Tier 3. However, when you compare the best performers within each tier, the gaps are much narrower: only 0.5–0.9% per component. This suggests that a well-run Tier 3 operator can actually get close to Tier 1 best-in-class costs. The tier classification is more about typical performance than ceiling performance. The real differentiator is operational discipline, not scale per se.

The biggest risk of underperformance is in Passage Detection and Compliance and Enforcement

Passage Detection and Compliance/Enforcement both show a 3.2x gap between the worst Tier 3 and best Tier 1 operator. Given that compliance in particular is deeply tied to revenue recovery (not just cost), underperformance here has a compounded impact as it simultaneously inflates costs and leaks revenue. This makes it arguably the highest-stakes component despite not being the largest in absolute cost terms.

The Back Office is where scale should deliver — but often doesn't

The Back Office is the second-largest cost component and has a wide performance range. A mature Tier 1 should be leveraging consolidation, automation, and volume to drive this well below 6%. Tier 1 operators sitting above this are losing meaningful value. 

Tier 2 operators may be in the most precarious position

Tier 2 operators are consistently closer to Tier 3 than Tier 1, despite presumably having more resources. They don't benefit from the scale of Tier 1, but they also don't have the excuse of structural immaturity. This "stuck in the middle" dynamic suggests Tier 2 operators have the most to gain from targeted cost transformation programmes, particularly in retail and back office where operational efficiencies are only marginally better than Tier 3.

The distinguishing characteristic for cost-to-serve purposes is the degree of per-transaction processing required. Key findings related to Tolling Technology and Products

An assessment of operating costs must be undertaken with an understanding of the technology and products in place, or being considered. Assessing technology benchmark costs gives a useful baseline from which forecast costs and targets can be extrapolated. This provides valuable insight for toll operators, asset owners, concessionaires, and government transport agencies exploring policy, strategic, project and operational transformations.

The below summarises the key findings from a generic assessment of benchmark data. However, greater value is achieved with a comparison to specific local data. 

The conventional wisdom about tag superiority no longer holds. 

The report's data overturns the long-standing industry narrative that tag-based systems achieve 30–45% lower operating costs than video. That conclusion was based on legacy ANPR with 95–98% accuracy, 2–5% leakage rates, and invoice-based billing. Next-generation ANPR, as specified in the Western Harbour Tunnel contract (2024), targets automation above 99.5% with near-zero leakage — matching or exceeding tag-based performance. At that specification level, video-only systems can achieve a total cost of $0.085–$0.124 per transaction versus $0.094–$0.148 for tag-based systems — a 10–15% cost advantage in favour of ANPR.

Technology choice reshapes costs across the entire value chain, not just detection. 

Tag-based systems carry costs that extend well beyond the gantry. Amortised tag hardware, distribution logistics, and tag-related customer service add $0.006–$0.011 per transaction. The commercial back office for tag customers costs $0.039–$0.058 per transaction, versus $0.030–$0.044 for ANPR — driven by account setup, tag issuance, and tag-related enquiry handling. The only component where tags retain a cost advantage is data storage, where ANPR's image-heavy transactions use 10–75x more storage than a tag read.

ANPR contractual targets must be considered and specified for adverse conditions, not just average conditions. 

The report notes that published automation targets typically reflect average operating conditions. Heavy rain, fog, low-angle sun glare, and night-time operation can reduce ANPR automation by 0.5–2.0 percentage points. Operators should require vendors to specify and guarantee minimum automation rates under defined adverse conditions alongside headline targets.

Product mix explains as much of the cost ratio variation as technology. 

The report models five product mix scenarios showing that the operating cost ratio can vary by 15–20 percentage points between operators with identical technology and scale, based solely on the proportion of casual/invoice tolling. An operator with 40% casual penetration (Scenario C) runs at 38–45% of revenue, versus 28–32% for one with 85% tag penetration and 10% casual (Scenario A). Product architecture is not downstream of technology — it is a co-equal driver of operating cost.

Casual/invoice tolling is 10–40x more expensive per transaction than account-based products. 

At $1.50–$6.00 per transaction, casual tolling carries costs from vehicle register lookups ($0.50–$1.50 per query), invoice generation and postage ($1.50–$3.00 per notice), and follow-up collection. The per-transaction cost of a prepaid tag account is $0.08–$0.15. Migrating casual customers to video accounts at $0.17 per transaction for an operator with 20% casual penetration across 100 million annual transactions could reduce operating costs by $25–55 million annually.

The mobile app is the highest-leverage single investment available. 

Across a $100–145M operating cost base, the report estimates $19–27M in annual savings from a comprehensive mobile app platform — a 19–22% cost reduction. The cross-component impact is what makes it distinctive: detection dispute reduction (5–10%), back-office inquiry deflection (15–25%), commercial back-office self-service (25–35%), and in-app compliance payment (25–35%). Most other digital initiatives depend on the mobile platform as their delivery channel, making it foundational rather than discretionary.

Key findings related to adjacent industries

Adjacent industries can provide useful benchmarks

Adjacent industries deploying similar transaction capture and account management technologies provide a useful external reference point for tolling operators. Public transport ticketing, utility back-office operations, and digital-first financial services all confirm that the efficiency benchmarks presented in this report are consistent with what high-performing organisations in comparable fields have already demonstrated is achievable.

Scale is still the single largest differentiator 

Scale across other industries accounts for an approximate 10–15 percentage point spread. The data shows a clear step-change relationship between transaction volume and cost ratio: operators below 10 million annual transactions typically run at 38–45%, while those above 500 million run at 22–26%. The jump from single-asset to multi-asset operation (50M to 200M transactions) typically saves 4–6 percentage points. For Australian operators, the implication is that back-office consolidation across assets — rather than technology alone — is the fastest path to meaningful cost ratio improvement.

Australian labour costs create a structural 5–8 percentage point premium that cannot be eliminated, only partially offset. 

At $80–120k per FTE, Australian labour costs are 3–4x higher than Asian operator equivalents and 1.5–2x higher than North American peers. This premium flows through the commercial back office (3–5pp), operational back office (2–3pp), and compliance (2–3pp) value chain components. The high-performing response is automation above 85–95% and digital adoption above 60–75% — which is why the commercial back office is the component most amenable to closing the gap.

A call to action

The benchmark analysis provides a robust starting point for operators to better understand their own cost allocation and efficiencies across their operating model, technology and tolling product. From there, opportunities and initiatives can be identified and prioritised to target the best ‘bang for buck’ outcomes. 

The first stage for operators is to identify the Efficiency Tier that provides the most suitable comparison, and then to allocate costs across the value chain components. Once a benchmark comparison has been established, combining local knowledge and global trends will drive a more informed view of opportunities. Technology, product and adjacent industry information can provide a useful baseline and comparator for policy, strategic and operational decision-making.

For Operators looking to benchmark and reduce operating costs

All Tiers

Retail digital transformation is essential, but value is only generated when it’s done well

Customer retail typically consumes 30% to 40% of operating costs. Digital migration reduces these costs by 40% to 50% whilst improving customer satisfaction, offering the single greatest improvement lever for operators who are yet to make this change. 

However, the customer journey from first toll encounter through to settled account represents a critical design challenge; operators achieving high digital adoption rates and that show efficiency savings invest in frictionless onboarding and comprehensive functionality rather than simply making digital channels available.

Tiers 1 and 2

Back Office Consolidation will reduce ‘per transaction’ costs

This action seems obvious for Tier 1 and 2 operators that have multiple assets and/or customer bases being served by similar but discrete systems. Mapping shareable functions and activities should deliver efficiencies across the board. Multi-scheme operators that are yet to deliver these opportunities should expect to be setting cost-per-transaction reduction targets of 30% to 40%

There is further opportunity for operators that have already consolidated tolling systems to benefit from scale. Customers are increasingly demanding more joined up services, particularly within government eco-systems, with road tolling accounts an easy target for rationalisation. The emergence of connected vehicles, V2X and in-car payments is driving the appetite for a simpler transaction that ultimately reduces an individual’s online identifiers and removes potential obstacles to compliance. Even the most mature Tier 1 Operators are likely to find opportunities for shared service consolidation outside of their immediate tolling operations.		

Mobile Applications can be cross-cutting efficiency drivers when operating at scale

A comprehensive mobile app delivers cost reductions across all four value chain components simultaneously:

Passage detection: Real-time trip confirmations reduce "I wasn't there" disputes by 5% to 10%.

Back office: Self-service transaction queries reduce inquiry volume by 15% to 25%.

Customer retail: Full account self-service reduces costs by 40% to 50%.

Compliance: In-app violation payment reduces compliance costs by 25% to 35%.

As a comparison, digital banking achieves 95% to 98% app adoption with 60% to 70% cost reduction versus traditional channels. Toll operators should target similar outcomes.

Tier 2

Tier 2 Operators risk being ‘stuck-in-the-middle’ without intentional strategic transformation

A pattern that warrants particular attention is the position of Tier 2 operators. On average, their total costs (33.9%) are significantly closer to Tier 3 (37.0%) than to Tier 1 (26.9%), despite presumably having greater resources, more established operations, and larger revenue bases than Tier 3 operators.

This 'stuck-in-the-middle' dynamic manifests consistently across all four components. Tier 2 midpoints on Retail (11.2%) and Back Office (9.7%) are each only marginally better than Tier 3, suggesting that scale is not being translated into efficiency through process improvement or technology investment.

This may be due to maturity with the efficiencies planned but not yet delivered on. There may also be a growth trajectory that means scaling now would risk future opportunities once the Operator is more aligned to Tier 1 scale.

In all scenarios however, the strategic implication for Tier 2 operators is clear: the path to value is through deliberate efficiency transformation rather than organic growth. Without targeted programmes in retail rationalisation and back-office consolidation, Tier 2 operators risk remaining in an efficiency gap that is difficult to escape as costs compound.

Tiers 2 and 3

Compliance and enforcement reform can return significant bang for buck

Benchmark data suggests that Tier 2 and 3 operators will find opportunity in compliance and enforcement, with in-direct benefits across other value chain components. Initiatives should focus on reviewing the end-to-end compliance cost structure, from notice issuance costs (including printing and postage), escalation rates and administrative costs, and debt recovery yield.

Tier 3 operators may see high enforcement costs reflected in Passage Detection, particularly where operational maturity reflects other factors such as tag penetration. Similarly back-office and retail operations may highlight customer friction that manifests in higher non-compliance. 

With a better understanding of direct compliance and enforcement costs, alongside in-direct factor from other value chains, a strategy to balance investment in ‘compliance’ i.e. improving detection and reducing friction, and investment in ‘enforcement’ i.e. streamlining processes, realigning penalty frameworks etc.

This dynamic is complex, particularly for small and immature operations. The relation between compliance vs. deterrence can vary across geographies and will influence customer trust and sentiment.

The Scale Premium: Maturity Matters More Than Size

One of the more nuanced findings from this data is that the performance ceiling (best-in-class efficiency) does not differ dramatically between tiers. The gap between the best performer in each tier is surprisingly narrow: only 0.5–0.9% per component between Tier 1 and Tier 3 best-in-class operators.

This suggests that the tier classification is not a hard constraint on achievable performance. A well-run Tier 3 operator can, in principle, approach Tier 1 best-in-class cost levels. The differentiator is not scale per se, but operational discipline, investment prioritisation, and management quality.

The corollary is that large Tier 1 operators sitting at or near their midpoint are underperforming relative to their potential. Scale creates the conditions for efficiency but does not guarantee it.

Mobile Application Platform (Foundational Investment)

A comprehensive mobile application should be deployed as the foundational platform for digital transformation, recognising its cross-cutting impact across all four value chain components.

The mobile app is not merely a customer service channel - it is the delivery platform for efficiency improvements across passage detection (trip confirmation, dispute reduction), back office (inquiry deflection, digital delivery), customer retail (self-service, payment efficiency), and compliance (digital notices, in-app payment). Investment in the mobile app must precede or parallel other digital initiatives.

Operators should approach investment depending on the network size (measured in number of transactions):

Large network operators (>200M txns): Full-featured app with AI chatbot, predictive analytics, and connected vehicle readiness. Investment: $10 - $16M. Target: 70 - 80% adoption within 24 months.

Mid-size operators (50 - 200M txns): Core account management, payment, and compliance features. Chatbot integration in Phase 2. Investment: $5 - $10M. Target: 60 - 70% adoption within 24 months.

Single-asset operators (<50M txns): Consideration of white-label or shared-platform approach to reduce development costs. Investment: $2 - $5M or licensing model. Target: 50 - 60% adoption within 24 months.

Other factors to consider in technology transformations

Compliance Process Optimisation

Implement automated owner lookup and leverage mobile app for payment channel enhancement. Deliverables include: 

API integration with vehicle registration database for owner lookup ($0.50 - $1.50 versus typical $3 - $6), 

early payment discount scheme (25 - 40% discount for 14-day payment) delivered via app push notification, 

in-app violation payment (one-tap payment from notification, 

target 70%+ of payments), 

payment plan self-service via app (reducing agent handling), and 

administrative penalty advocacy where court-based enforcement applies.

Back Office Automation

Increase automated processing from typical 85% to >95%. Deliverables include: 

machine learning for transaction classification edge cases, 

automated exception handling for common scenarios, 

real-time exception alerting system, and 

a performance dashboard with component-level metrics.

Workforce Transition Planning

Digital transformation and automation initiatives will materially reduce labour requirements across all value chain components. Operators should develop workforce transition plans that address retraining and redeployment opportunities (e.g., transitioning call centre staff to digital channel management or exception handling), phased implementation aligned with natural attrition where possible, and communication strategies that maintain staff engagement during transition.

For Asset Owners and Government Agencies

Operational initiative support

The focus of this report is on operational costs and efficiencies, but several calls to action for asset owners and government agencies have been highlighted that can provide entry points to inspire, support or accelerate operational initiatives.

Concessionaire Performance Framework

Develop enhanced performance requirements using value chain benchmarks. Deliverables include: 

component-level KPIs for concessionaire agreements, 

benchmarking methodology for annual performance reviews, 

incentive/penalty framework aligned with efficiency targets, and

best practice sharing mechanisms across the portfolio.

Shared Services Evaluation

Assess opportunities for shared services across tolling assets and with other government service channels. Deliverables include: 

business case for customer service consolidation, 

government service centre integration feasibility, 

shared back office operations assessment, and 

implementation roadmap.

Technology Procurement Standards

Establish next-generation video-only (ANPR) as the preferred detection technology for all new assets based on emerging evidence. Deliverables include: 

technology specification standard requiring 99.5%+ automation and near-zero leakage (with minimum performance thresholds for adverse conditions including heavy rain, night-time, and low-angle sun), 

business case framework for tag-based infrastructure retirement on legacy assets, 

updated procurement specifications mandating next-gen ANPR standards, 

environmental benefit quantification (tag elimination), 

customer journey simplification requirements (no tag pickup, no tag replacement), and 

contractual performance guarantee mechanisms that protect the asset owner if automation targets are not achieved in operations.

Privacy and Data Governance Framework

ANPR deployment involves the capture, processing, and storage of vehicle images and licence plate data at scale. Asset owners should establish clear data governance frameworks addressing image retention periods and deletion protocols, data access controls and audit trails, privacy impact assessments for new ANPR deployments, compliance with applicable privacy legislation (including, in Australia, the Privacy Act 1988 and any state-level requirements), and public communication about data collection purposes and safeguards. These requirements should be incorporated into procurement specifications and concessionaire agreements.

Long-Term Strategic Initiatives (36+ Months)

Connected Vehicle Integration

Pilot vehicle-based tolling to further reduce infrastructure costs. This initiative is at an early stage and should be monitored rather than committed to as a near-term investment. The technology landscape is evolving rapidly, with competing approaches (smartphone-based, factory-fitted OBU, V2X communication) and uncertain adoption timelines. Deliverables include: 

monitoring of OEM integration programmes and industry standards, 

pilot programme design for evaluation when technology maturity permits, 

API standards for interoperability, and 

incentive structure for adoption.

Interoperability and Standardisation

Explore opportunities for interagency networks or shared infrastructure. In the Australian context, interoperability faces specific barriers including fragmented state-level regulatory frameworks, multiple commercial operators with competing interests, and established proprietary tag systems. 

A national interoperability assessment should evaluate governance structures (drawing on international clearing house models), technical standards alignment across jurisdictions, and commercial frameworks that enable cost sharing without undermining concession agreements. 

Deliverables include: 

market entry assessment with barriers for new, independent retailers identified and evaluated

technology strategy to consider future proofing of system against connected vehicle integration etc.

reciprocity agreement framework, 

technical standards alignment, 

shared infrastructure opportunities, and 

national tolling interoperability assessment.
