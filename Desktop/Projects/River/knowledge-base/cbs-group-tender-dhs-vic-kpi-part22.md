Executive summary
The Maintenance Guidance for Victorian Health Facilities – Performance Monitoring has been
developed by the Department of Health (the department) to supplement the updated Maintenance
Guidance for Victorian Health Facilities published in 2024. This document is designed to assist
healthcare facilities in monitoring maintenance activities of engineering assets effectively.
Its primary goal is to establish consistent guidance for maintenance management and reporting
across Health Services. It promotes a systematic approach to monitoring, adaptable to
organisations of varying sizes and asset management maturity levels.
The guidance aims to enhance maintenance capabilities while aligning with the Asset Management
Accountability Framework (AMAF) requirements for consistency in asset management.
Maintenance is an ongoing activity that requires continuous monitoring and timely updates.
The introduction of Key Performance Indicators (KPIs) marks a significant advancement in
strengthening asset management and maintenance practices across all Victorian Health Services.
By offering a structured, measurable, and scalable framework, these KPIs enable Health Services
to proactively assess and improve their maintenance operations.
This initiative fosters a culture of continuous improvement, encouraging all Health Services—
regardless of their current asset management maturity—to benchmark performance, identify gaps,
and implement targeted enhancements. Ultimately, the KPI framework promotes greater
consistency, transparency, and accountability in maintenance practices. It supports alignment with
AMAF and other relevant standards, ensuring that all facilities are better equipped to deliver safe,
reliable, and efficient healthcare environments.

1 Introduction
1.1 Background
The Department of Health (the department) has been established to deliver a world-class health
system that leads to better health outcomes for Victorians. The department’s infrastructure plays a
pivotal role in enabling the provision of a wide range of vital services, including aged care, public
hospitals, Aboriginal and Indigenous services, illness prevention and recovery, mental health,
rehabilitation, and community facilities. It also assumes responsibility for monitoring and facilitating
the department and Health Services’ compliance with the Asset Management Accountability
Framework (AMAF). This document reflects the requirements of the AMAF introduced by the
Department of Treasury and Finance (DTF) in 2016.
Operating under a partnership service model, in accordance with the Public Health and Wellbeing
Act 2008, the department oversees the operational facilities managed by independent Health
Service providers. The department acknowledges the need for improvement in maintaining these
facilities and monitoring assets performance. To address this challenge, the Maintenance
guidance for Victorian health facilities, Performance monitoring has been developed to serve
as non-prescriptive guideline and support for hospitals and Health Services in asset maintenance
monitoring. This document supplements the Maintenance guidance for Victorian health facilities
published in 2024.


1.2 Objectives
The objectives of this document are to define a clear and strategic foundation for maintenance
performance monitoring of engineering assets across Victorian health facilities. These objectives
align with the Department of Health’s asset management priorities and are intended to guide the
development and implementation of Key Performance Indicators (KPIs) that support safe, efficient,
and resilient healthcare environments.
The following objectives have been identified as critical to the success of maintenance and asset
management practices:
1. Reliability & Resilience
   Ensure high system uptime, eliminate or reduce single points of failure, and implement robust
   maintenance strategies that support continuity of care. This includes planning for resilience
   through robustness, redundancy, resourcefulness, response, and recovery mechanisms.
2. Compliance & Risk Management
   Maintain full compliance with regulatory statutory, regulatory, and contractual obligations. This
   includes health and safety, environmental, and legal requirements, and supports risk mitigation
   through structured frameworks and emergency preparedness.
3. Quality and Performance
   Deliver high-quality maintenance services that support clinical operations and extend asset
   longevity. Performance should be continuously monitored and improved through data-driven
   insights and formal review mechanisms.
4. Cost Management
   Promote cost-effective maintenance practices that balance performance and risk. This includes
   lifecycle costing, budget adherence, and efficient, value-for-money strategies that ensure
   financial sustainability.

5. Asset & Data Lifecycle Management
    Embed a whole-of-life approach to asset management, ensuring that assets are planned,
    acquired, operated, maintained, and recycled / retired in a way that maximises value and
    performance. This includes the integration of accurate, complete, and standardised asset data
    to support forecasting, investment planning, and long-term service outcomes.
6. Service Delivery & Customer Service
   Ensure maintenance services are responsive, efficient, and aligned with stakeholder needs.
   Feedback mechanisms and service level agreements should be used to drive continuous
   improvement.
7. Communication
   Establish clear and structured communication channels across all levels of maintenance and
   asset management and other relevant stakeholders. This supports transparency, accountability,
   and timely decision-making.
8. Maintenance Personnel Performance
    Monitor and support the performance of maintenance personnel, including in-house teams and
    contractors. This includes role clarity, training, and alignment with asset management plans and
    KPIs.
9. Governance & Asset Management Integration
   Strengthen the integration of governance frameworks with asset management systems and
   processes. This includes aligning with AMAF requirements, standardising procedures, and
   ensuring that leadership, controls, and strategic oversight are embedded into operational
   delivery and performance monitoring.


1.3 How to use this document
This document is intended to support Health Services (HS) in monitoring and improving the
performance of their maintenance management practices. It assumes that most HS already have
maintenance strategies in place, whether based on statutory requirements, manufacturer
recommendations, minimum essential life safety maintenance, or risk-based approaches.
Rather than replacing existing practices, this document provides a structured framework to enhance
them. Its primary focus is on measuring and improving maintenance performance through the use of
Key Performance Indicators (KPIs).
Health Services can apply this document in the following ways:
•   Aligning with AMAF: Use the guidance to support compliance with the Asset Management

3 Performance monitoring framework
3.1 Benefits of performance monitoring
The introduction of Key Performance Indicators (KPIs) provides a structured and measurable
approach to improving maintenance performance across Victorian health facilities. The benefits of
implementing KPIs include:
    •   Enhanced Visibility and Accountability
        KPIs offer a transparent view of maintenance performance, enabling Health Services and
        the Department of Health to monitor progress, identify gaps, and hold stakeholders
        accountable for outcomes.
    •   Alignment with Strategic Objectives
        Each KPI is mapped to one or more of the nine strategic objectives. This ensures that
        performance monitoring directly supports roader goals such as reliability, compliance, cost
        efficiency, and service quality.
    •   Support for Continuous Improvement
        By tracking trends over time, KPIs help Health Services identify areas for improvement,
        implement corrective actions, and measure the impact of those changes.
    •   Scalability Across Maturity Levels
        The KPI framework is designed to be scalable and adaptable. Health Services at different
        stages of asset management maturity can implement KPIs progressively, starting with
        foundational metrics and advancing to more complex indicators as capability grows.
    •   Data-Driven Decision Making
        KPIs provide evidence-based insights that support informed decision-making around
        maintenance planning, resource allocation, and risk mitigation.
    •   Benchmarking and Standardisation
        The use of consistent KPIs across the sector enables benchmarking between facilities and
        promotes standardisation of maintenance practices, supporting equity and efficiency.
    •   Regulatory and Policy Compliance
        KPIs help demonstrate compliance with the Asset Management Accountability Framework
        (AMAF) and other relevant standards, reinforcing good governance and risk management.
To realise the benefits outlined above, the KPIs adopted should demonstrate sound design
principles and exhibit the characteristics of well-constructed indicators. These characteristics, such
as strategic alignment, clarity, relevance, and measurability, were considered in the development of
this framework and are further detailed in Appendix 04: Characteristics of “Good” KPIs.


3.1.1 Asset performance
When unexpected asset failures occur, or when asset performance is below expectations (for
example excessive failure rate, low functional performance), Health Services should undertake a
structured approach to identify the cause of such failures. This can be identified through
performance monitoring, as outlined in Maintenance guidance for Victorian health facilities
Section 2.5, to identify failure events that require review.
Active monitoring of failure rates across sites and asset types, combined with reporting on critical
failure (in accordance with a criticality framework, see Maintenance guidance for Victorian health
facilities Section 4) should be undertaken to enable the appropriate actions and responses to the
failures. This will enable identification of those failures that are either high-rate failures or critical
failures that should be addressed.

3.1.2 Maintenance strategy
Maintenance strategies and the asset performance should be monitored and reviewed on an
ongoing basis to identify opportunities or requirements for improvement. Health Services may
consider monitoring metrics that relate to cost, risk, and performance to ensure meeting the core
maintenance and organisational objectives.
Establishing metrics or key performance indicators (KPIs) is necessary to monitor the performance
of maintenance strategies. Tracking performance against the KPIs requires access to and analysis
of relevant data.
Some examples of KPIs for monitoring the performance of maintenance strategies are provided in
3.1.4 below, along with guidance regarding the minimum data required. It is assumed that Health
Services have an existing asset register containing basic asset information (including list of all
assets and asset IDs, description, date of purchase, purchase cost, asset class related through an
asset hierarchy, asset location, asset owner, current asset condition) to support performance
monitoring. The acceptable ranges of these KPIs will vary for each Health Service and should be
applicable to the size and scale of the organisation.


3.1.3 Maintenance management
Monitoring maintenance management should be undertaken to ensure the execution of
maintenance is scheduled and in compliance with standards, regulatory requirements, and industry
best practice. Where a contractor agreement is in place for maintenance, the KPIs should be
reviewed in tandem to ensure alignment.


3.1.4 Portfolio strategic oversight
Effective portfolio-level asset management requires structured oversight of maintenance execution
to ensure alignment with organisational standards, regulatory obligations, and industry best
practices. This includes not only verifying that maintenance activities are scheduled and completed
as planned, but also that they contribute meaningfully to the overall performance objectives of the
asset base.
Where maintenance services are delivered through contracted partners, performance should be
continuously evaluated against agreed Key Performance Indicators (KPIs). These KPIs must be
reviewed in conjunction with contract terms to ensure strategic alignment and drive accountability.
To support this, a suite of standardised KPIs, such as Planned Maintenance Percentage (PMP),
and Mean Time to Repair (MTTR), should be monitored across all sites. These indicators provide
visibility into the effectiveness of the maintenance function and enable benchmarking across the
portfolio. Guidance on minimum data requirements for these KPIs is outlined in Table 33.1.4.

3.2 Asset management maturity
The application of KPIs should be carefully aligned with the “Asset Management” maturity level of
each site. While Category 1 sites are generally expected to demonstrate a higher degree of maturity
than Category 3 sites due to the nature and complexity of their operations this does not mean that
lower-category sites cannot achieve strong asset management performance.
However, it is essential to recognise that not all KPIs are universally applicable or practical across
all maturity levels. Some may be too complex or resource-intensive to implement effectively in
certain contexts. Therefore, KPIs should be selected and applied in a way that is both meaningful
and achievable, enabling operators to extract real value from their assets without being discouraged
by overly sophisticated metrics.
Further detail on how maturity levels inform KPI selection and target setting is provided in Targets
and Benchmarking. For the underlying maturity framework and self-assessment methodology, refer
to Appendix 05: Asset Management Maturity.


3.3 Maturity levels
The Maintenance guidance for Victorian health facilities refers to three maturity levels
(foundation, established and advanced). These levels will be expanded and evolve over time in line
with the advancing maturity of the engineering and building management sector. Regular reviews
and updates to performance monitoring of maintenance standards are crucial to align with the
maturing landscape of engineering systems and building management practices in Health Services.

KPI 1: Mean Time To Repair (MTTR)
Purpose
MTTR measures the average time taken to repair an asset following a failure. Within DH
maintenance operations, it reflects the responsiveness and efficiency of maintenance teams in
restoring critical systems such as HVAC, electrical infrastructure, fire detection systems, and water
supply pumps. MTTR may refer to the time taken to restore service, which can include temporary
fixes, or to fully remediate the issue with a permanent repair, depending on organisational
definitions. In this case, MTTR will be measured based on full restoration of the asset. This KPI
applies only to critical assets and is intended for use at Foundation Level 0 maturity, where
monitoring is ad-hoc and focused on essential systems.

Benefits & Outcomes
   • Minimises downtime, ensuring continuity of patient care.
   • Enhances maintenance team performance and accountability.
   • Supports resource planning and contractor performance evaluation.
   • Enables benchmarking across facilities and asset types.

Main Objectives Alignment
Reliability & Resilience, Quality and Performance, Service Delivery & Customer Service,
Maintenance Personnel Performance.

Performance Insight
Lower MTTR values indicate faster recovery and better operational resilience. Monthly trends are
typically monitored against a 4-hour target (See also “Target / Benchmark” below). Persistent delays
may signal process inefficiencies or resource constraints.

KPI 2: Reactive Maintenance SLA Compliance
Purpose
Measures the percentage of reactive (unplanned) asset-related incidents resolved within the defined
Service Level Agreement (SLA) timeframes. This KPI ensures timely response and resolution of
maintenance issues, which is critical in hospital environments where delays can impact patient care
and safety. Reactive Maintenance Response Time works especially well when SLA targets are
defined for reactive maintenance. For example:
    • Critical issues may require a response within 30 minutes.
    • High-priority issues within 1 hour.
    • Routine issues within 24 hours.
This structure supports prioritisation, risk reduction, and service continuity, especially important in
hospital environments where delays can directly impact patient care.

Service Level Agreement (SLA) targets are defined based on a combination of internal policy
frameworks and contractual obligations with external service providers. These targets may vary
depending on:
    • Asset type: Critical assets often have stricter SLA timeframes due to their direct impact on
        patient care.
    • Location: Site-specific operational requirements or infrastructure limitations may influence
        SLA expectations.
In cases where maintenance is fully outsourced and no formal documentation exists, health
services may baseline their SLA strategies using current service provision and adapt them to the
asset’s operational context, by ensuring SLA targets are both realistic and take into account factors
such as asset location, risk exposure, and critical functionality assessments, aligning service
expectations with operational priorities and patient safety requirements.

Benefits & Outcomes
   • Improves responsiveness and service reliability.
   • Supports prioritisation of incidents based on criticality.
   • Enhances operational resilience and risk management.
   • Enables performance benchmarking and contractor accountability.

Reliability & Resilience, Compliance & Risk Management, Quality and Performance, Service
Delivery & Customer Service, Communication, Maintenance Personnel Performance.

Performance Insight
High SLA compliance rates indicate effective and timely resolution of reactive maintenance
incidents. Monthly tracking helps identify delays, resource gaps, and process inefficiencies.

KPI 3: Maintenance Work Order Backlog
Purpose
Tracks the number of pending maintenance work orders, reflecting responsiveness and workload
management.
Benefits & Outcomes
    •   Indicates maintenance efficiency and resource sufficiency.
    •   Supports prioritisation and scheduling.
    •   Reduces risk of delayed maintenance and asset failure.

Main Objectives Alignment
Reliability & Resilience, Compliance & Risk Management, Quality and Performance.
Performance Insight
Lower backlog percentages reflect better responsiveness and maintenance efficiency. According to
industry standards, monthly reviews are essential not only for tracking performance but also for
identifying bottlenecks, such as excessive work demand, inefficient execution, or insufficient
resources, that contribute to backlog accumulation. These reviews help uncover resource
gaps (e.g., lack of skilled personnel, delays in spare parts or supplier availability) and enable
proactive planning to address them. By analysing trends in deferred vs. unmanaged backlog,
organisations can assess risk exposure and determine whether backlog is being effectively
managed or if it's introducing unmanaged risk.
Minimum Maturity Level

    •   Pending Work Orders:
        These are reactive or preventive maintenance tasks that have been logged but not yet

    •   Total Work Orders:
        The sum of all maintenance work orders generated during the reporting period, including
        both completed and pending tasks.

KPI 4: Repeated Failures
Purpose
Measures the recurrence of asset failures, indicating unresolved issues or ineffective maintenance
strategies. This KPI acts as a feedback loop for reliability improvement.


Benefits & Outcomes
    •   Highlights assets requiring deeper investigation.
    •   Supports targeted interventions and RCA (Root Cause Analysis).
    •   Improves asset reliability and performance.

Main Objectives Alignment
Reliability & Resilience, Quality and Performance.


Performance Insight
Monthly tracking helps identify problematic assets. Lower counts of repeated failures reflect
effective maintenance. Repeated failures trigger RCA investigations for assets with high recurrence,
ensuring that systemic issues are properly understood and addressed, assesses whether RCA has
worked, and helps shift maintenance strategies from reactive to proactive. Its integration into
performance tracking strengthens the case for structured problem-solving and continuous
improvement.
