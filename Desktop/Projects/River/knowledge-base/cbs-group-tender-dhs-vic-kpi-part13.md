Executive Summary
Situation:
The Victorian Department of Health oversees 77 public health services operating under maintenance models ranging
from fully in-house to fully outsourced. This diversity has produced inconsistent asset performance, fragmented
reporting, and limited ability to benchmark services, allocate capital effectively, or identify where clinical and safety
risks are emerging.
Without standardised maintenance KPIs, the Department cannot systematically determine where building stock and
facilities are deteriorating, where essential services infrastructure — HVAC, medical gases, backup power and nurse
call systems — is at risk, or where maintenance teams lack adequate resources to maintain safe, reliable operations.
To address this, the Department has developed a comprehensive KPI framework comprising 12 KPIs across two
implementation phases, supported by a 7-level maturity model (Level 0: Ad Hoc through Level 7: Transforming), as
detailed in the September 2025 Maintenance Standards Guidelines Performance Monitoring document.
Complication:
To realise the benefit of the Department’s KPI Framework, it must be ‘operationalised’ - from policy into operational
practice across 77 organisations — each with different CMMS capabilities, workforce capacity, building ages, service
profiles, and competing clinical priorities.
Challenge: How can the Department implement its KPI framework in a way that is practical, scalable, and
enduring — achieving widespread adoption and value realisation for the services and the Department. How can
this benefit be realised while minimising the additional reporting burden on health service maintenance teams
that are already operating under resource constraints?
The CBS Group Approach:
CBS Group brings a senior team drawing upon the practical and pragmatic application of KPIs, ensuring asset
performance is actively managed in the context of service delivery and of maintenance constraints.

    •   Our team understands the challenges in the field; the data limitations, the service delivery pressure, and the
        bandwidth challenges these teams face.
    •   At the other end, we understand the Department’s KPI framework, the problems it is designed to solve, and are
        well placed to ensure that these ambitions are met.
    •   CBS brings unrivalled professional asset management expertise to this engagement, coupled with deep
        experience in health services delivery and facility management.
    •   Equally importantly, we bring change management to this engagement, acknowledging how important it is to
        communicate clearly, understand constraints, and demonstrate value to this complex stakeholder group. In
        similar engagements, we have drawn upon our Collaboration Practice to apply the ISO44001 practices to
        ensure trust and transparency in contexts like this one.
Our proprietary CAPITAL framework has been successfully deployed to implement performance metrics across diverse
organisations with varying maturity levels, delivering substantial validated savings while sustaining or improving asset
performance and service delivery.
Our Melbourne-based team of senior asset management, systems engineering, and change management specialists
will apply a proven methodology to the Department's health infrastructure context through three core commitments:

    •   segmenting health services by operational maturity — not size or geography — to ensure proportionate, relevant
        support
    •   co-designing implementation pathways with health services so the framework is owned, not imposed
    •   integrating KPI data capture with existing maintenance systems to minimise administrative burden on
        operational teams

Worked Example — MTTR Implementation in a Regional Health Service
The following worked example illustrates how KPI 1 (Mean Time to Repair) would be implemented end-to-end in a
hypothetical Level 2 maturity regional health service, showing CBS Group's practical approach.
Facility Profile: Goulburn Valley District Hospital (hypothetical). A 120-bed regional health service with a maintenance
team of 3 FTE (1 supervisor, 2 technicians), supplemented by external contractors for specialist trades. The facility is
approximately 45 years old with two major refurbishments. The maintenance team currently uses MEX (basic
configuration) for work order management, with inconsistent data entry practices. Current maturity assessment: Level 2
(Emerging — some formal processes established but inconsistently applied).
Phase 1: Discovery (What We Find): During the site assessment, CBS identifies the following: MEX is installed but work
orders are not consistently closed out with completion timestamps; approximately 60% of reactive work orders have
both a "reported" and "completed" timestamp, while 40% are missing one or both; no distinction is made between asset
criticality levels in work order priority; and the maintenance supervisor tracks urgent clinical equipment failures
mentally but does not record response times systematically.
Phase 2: Data Analysis (What We Learn): From the 60% of work orders with usable timestamps, CBS extracts 12
months of MTTR data and calculates: overall MTTR across all assets of approximately 14.2 hours; however, this figure
masks significant variation — general building maintenance MTTR is approximately 22 hours (acceptable), while
essential services MTTR is approximately 4.8 hours (needs validation against AS 1851 and clinical requirements). CBS
also identifies that 35% of reactive work orders are repeat failures on the same assets, suggesting underlying reliability
issues that Repeated Failures (KPI 4), Planned Maintenance Task Completion Rate (KPI 5), and the ensuing root cause
analyses will help address.
Phase 3: Co-Design (What We Agree): In the co-design workshop (attended with other Level 2 cohort services), CBS
works with the maintenance supervisor to establish the following: MTTR will be measured in three tiers aligned to asset
criticality — Critical (essential services: theatre ventilation, medical gases, backup power, fire systems, nurse call) with
a target MTTR of less than 2 hours; High (clinical environment: ward HVAC, hot water, lifts, sterilisation) with an example
target MTTR of less than 8 hours; and Standard (non-clinical: general lighting, office HVAC, grounds) with a target MTTR
of less than 48 hours.
To capture the data, CBS configures two changes in MEX: a mandatory "Asset Criticality" field on the work order form
(pre-populated from the asset register with a dropdown of Critical/High/Standard); and a workflow rule that prevents
work order closure without both "Reported" and "Completed" timestamps. These changes require approximately 2
hours of MEX configuration and 30 minutes of training for each technician.
Phase 4: Pilot (What Happens): During the 8-week pilot: the data capture rate improves from 60% to 92% within 4
weeks (the mandatory fields make it easier to comply than to skip); the maintenance supervisor reports that the
criticality tiering "makes sense" and reflects how the team already prioritises informally; the fortnightly check-in
identifies that the "Completed" timestamp is being entered when the technician finishes work, not when the asset is
returned to service — CBS adjusts the field label to "Asset Returned to Service" to capture the correct endpoint; and the
additional data entry burden is measured at approximately 3 minutes per work order, equating to approximately 45
minutes per week — well within the 2-hour monthly target.

Phase 5: Outcomes: After the pilot, the health service has: a functioning MTTR measurement system integrated into
existing MEX workflows; baseline MTTR data segmented by asset criticality, enabling meaningful performance tracking;
visibility of the repeat-failure pattern (feeding into KPI 4: Repeated Failures), which the maintenance supervisor uses to
build a business case for targeted asset replacement; and a practical, sustainable reporting process that adds minimal
burden to the maintenance team.
The Department has: comparable MTTR data from this service that can be benchmarked against other Level 2 cohort
services; visibility of essential services response performance against clinical risk thresholds; and evidence of
improvement trajectory that informs both the service's maturity advancement and the Department's capital allocation
decisions.

Worked Example — OEE Adaptation for Health Essential Services
Overall Equipment Effectiveness (OEE) is a Phase 2 KPI that requires adaptation for health infrastructure. In
manufacturing, OEE = Availability × Performance × Quality. In a health context, CBS adapts this as Essential Services
Effectiveness (ESE):
ESE = Availability × Performance × Compliance
Availability measures the proportion of time essential services systems are operational and available for clinical use —
calculated as (Total scheduled hours minus unplanned downtime hours) divided by Total scheduled hours. For a
hospital operating theatre ventilation system scheduled 24/7, 8 hours of unplanned downtime in a month (720 hours)
yields an Availability of 98.9%.
Performance measures whether the system delivers to its design specification when operational — for example,
whether the HVAC system maintains the required temperature and pressure differentials in a theatre environment, or
whether the medical gas system delivers at the required pressure and flow rates. This is measured through periodic
performance testing against AS 1851 (fire systems), AS 2896 (medical gas), or design specifications.
Compliance replaces the manufacturing "Quality" component and measures the proportion of scheduled maintenance
tasks completed to specification and on time — directly linking to KPI 5 (Planned Maintenance Task Completion Rate)
and mandatory maintenance frequencies under the relevant Australian Standards.
Practical implementation: For a Level 3 (Developing) health service with basic CMMS capability, CBS would initially
measure ESE for the top 5 essential services systems only (backup power, fire detection and suppression, medical
gases, theatre ventilation, and nurse call). Data sources include: CMMS downtime logs for Availability, existing essential
services testing records for Performance, and planned maintenance completion records for Compliance. This focused
approach provides a meaningful ESE measure without requiring data across all building systems — the scope can be
expanded as the health service matures.

4.1.1.     Phase 1: Discovery and Assessment (Weeks 1–4)
This phase establishes the evidence base for a targeted implementation. Our approach is designed to engage with, and
rapidly build a clear, evidence-based understanding of the current maintenance environment across the 77 Victorian
health services.
CBS will conduct a systematic assessment of each health service's maintenance model, systems, processes, and data
capabilities. The assessment is structured around four dimensions aligned to the Department's maturity model:

 Organisational           governance structures, management commitment, workforce skills and capacity,
 readiness                maintenance team structure and resourcing levels.
 Operational              examining the maturity of asset management practices, and the operational realities that
 readiness                influence performance including maintenance management processes, planned versus
                          reactive maintenance mix, work order management, contractor management arrangements.
 Data capability          CMMS maturity (system type, configuration, data completeness), ability to extract KPI data
                          from existing systems, data quality and consistency, reporting capability.
 Resource availability    workforce capacity to support KPI implementation alongside operational duties, training
                          needs, system upgrade requirements.

Leveraging the Department's stakeholder details to plan engagement, we will conduct targeted engagement with
operational leads, engineering managers and asset management teams to validate findings and ensure the local context
is fully understood.
The initial assessment will be conducted through a combination of structured interviews with facilities managers and
maintenance leads (60–90 minutes, on-site or video), and a self-assessment questionnaire, followed up with system
walkthroughs to understand data capture workflows, and document review (maintenance plans, asset registers, recent
performance reports where available).
Key deliverables: Discovery Report documenting the current state across the 77 health services; Stakeholder
Engagement& Communications Plan; Data Readiness Assessment for each pilot service; and Risk Register with
mitigation strategies.

4.1.2.     Phase 2: Data Analysis and Benchmarking (Weeks 3-8)
Overlapping with late Phase 1 to maintain momentum, this phase gathers, validates, and analyses maintenance data
from participating health services. The overlapping timeline reflects that data collection can commence for early-
assessed services while discovery continues for later services. Our team will undertake rigorous data validation and
cleansing to ensure accuracy and comparability across health services.
CBS will benchmark current performance against the Department's six (6) Foundational KPIs and relevant standards,
including alignment with ISO 55001 (Asset Management) and ISO 41001 (Facility Management) where applicable. This
analytical rigour ensures that KPI targets are grounded in engineering evidence, not arbitrary benchmarks. A critical
output is the maturity segmentation — classifying health services into cohorts using assessment criteria mapped to the
Department's 7-level Asset Management Maturity model. This segmentation, combined with the organisational and
operational readiness information gathered in Phase 1 will provide the Department meaningful information to determine
the ongoing pilot group composition, ensuring the pilot group is representative, scalable and positioned to generate
meaningful insights for sector wide implementation.

For health services with limited data, we will establish minimum viable baselines using available information
supplemented by structured estimation where necessary. This ensures every health service has a starting point for
measurement, even if initial data is imperfect — recognising that perfect data is not a prerequisite for beginning to
improve.
A critical challenge in benchmarking across the 77 health services is the diversity of building stock — from recently
constructed facilities to buildings over 100 years old, with varying configurations, service profiles, and asset condition.
CBS will normalise benchmarking data using a peer group methodology based on three dimensions: facility complexity
(determined by service profile — tertiary/specialist, general acute, subacute/rehabilitation, community health), asset
age band (grouped into post-2010, 1980–2010, pre-1980 cohorts reflecting distinct construction standards and asset
lifecycles), and maintenance model (in-house, partially outsourced, fully outsourced). This segmentation produces
meaningful peer groups where like is compared with like, preventing misleading benchmarks that penalise older
facilities or reward lower-complexity services. Where a health service spans multiple building ages or configurations, we
will benchmark at the building or precinct level where data permits, aggregating to the health service level for portfolio
reporting.
Key deliverables: Benchmarking Report with performance baselines and peer group normalisation methodology;
Maturity Segmentation Model mapping each pilot health service to the Department's 7-level maturity model; pilot group
definition and selection rationale (proposing 15 health services spanning maturity, facility types and, where possible,
equitable metro/regional representation); and Gap Analysis identifying priority improvement areas for each cohort –
Foundational, Established and Advanced.

4.1.3.      Phase 3: Engagement and Co-Design (Weeks 6-12)
This phase is the critical bridge between analysis and implementation, focusing on direct engagement and collaborative
design to ensure the KPI framework is practical, adoptable and aligned with operational realities. Our codesign
approach ensures that health services feel ownership of the solution, that implementation is grounded in real world
practice, will provide enhanced visibility and accountability, provide data driven decision making and add ongoing value
to their service through the benefits realisation.
CBS will facilitate stakeholder engagement and co-design through the following structured mechanisms:
Codesign workshops (2-2.5 hours) –with each service including executive stakeholders, asset / facility managers, key
engineering and maintenance team members and other key stakeholders, structured around the practical question:
"How will these KPIs work in your facility?". The workshops will incorporate:
   i.    Context setting: overview of the KPI framework purpose, what the Department needs, and how KPIs will support
         investment decisions and operational improvement. Facilitated discussion on current maintenance challenges

4.2.2.       Actions to ensure requirements and timelines are met
Dependency management and contingency. The most critical external dependency is the Department's provision of
stakeholder details and introductions to pilot health services at the start of Phase 1. If introductions are delayed, CBS
will mitigate by: commencing desktop analysis (document review, data collection scoping, assessment framework
design) during Week 1 without requiring health service access; conducting initial stakeholder mapping using publicly
available information and the Department's existing health service directory; and compressing the site assessment
phase from 3 weeks to 2 weeks once introductions are made, with additional team resource temporarily assigned to
maintain the overall timeline. A delay of up to 2 weeks can be absorbed within the existing programme structure. If
delays exceed 2 weeks, CBS will propose a revised programme to the Department with adjusted milestones. Similarly, if
Department review periods for critical deliverables (D6, D10, D17) extend beyond the 5-business-day review cycle, CBS
will flag the schedule impact in the fortnightly progress report and propose mitigation.
Concurrent phase management. All phases overlap to run concurrently by design, enabling an efficient 22-week
programme. CBS manages this through deliberate team allocation: during the Phase 2/3 overlap (Weeks 6–8), Perry and
Harper lead the data and benchmarking workstream while Caranguian and Ellwood lead the engagement and co-design
workstream — the two streams are distinct in personnel and activities, converging only at defined integration points (the
maturity segmentation output from Phase 2 feeds directly into Phase 3 co-design workshop design). During the Phase
4/5 overlap (Weeks 16–18), Ellwood leads the final pilot monitoring while Caranguian begins full implementation
planning — again, distinct personnel on distinct activities with defined integration points. Kate Heath manages cross-
phase coordination through the fortnightly progress reporting cycle. Jeff Dusting's strategic oversight role includes
monitoring phase interactions and resolving any resource conflicts.
Critical path. The critical path runs through: Department of Health (DH) stakeholder introductions (Week 1) → site
assessments complete (Week 4) → Maturity Segmentation Model approved by DH (Week 6) → co-design workshops
complete (Week 11) → Implementation Action Plans approved by DH (Week 12) → pilot commencement (Week 13) → Pilot
Evaluation Report (Week 18) → Final Implementation Framework approved by DH (Week 21). The two items most likely to
cause programme delay are DH approval of the Maturity Segmentation Model (which gates the co-design workshop
content) and DH approval of the Implementation Action Plans (which gates pilot commencement). CBS will mitigate
these by submitting draft deliverables 1 week before the formal submission date, enabling informal DH review and
feedback before the formal review cycle begins.
