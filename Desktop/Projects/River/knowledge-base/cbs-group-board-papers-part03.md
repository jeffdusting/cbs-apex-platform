5. DISCLOSURE OBLIGATIONS

5.1 Statutory Disclosure (s191/s192)

A director who has a material personal interest in a matter relating to the affairs of the Company must, as soon as practicable after becoming aware of the interest, disclose the nature and extent of that interest to the other directors. Disclosure may be given:

(a) orally at a board meeting, to be recorded in the minutes of that meeting (s191); or

(b) by Standing Notice in writing, tabled at the next board meeting (s192).

Penalty for non-disclosure: Section 191(1) creates a strict liability offence carrying a penalty of up to 50 penalty units (currently A$22,200) per director per undisclosed matter.

5.2 When Disclosure Is Required

Disclosure is required whenever a director has, or may reasonably be perceived to have, a material personal interest in a matter before the Board. This includes, without limitation:

(a) any transaction, arrangement, or dealing involving a Portfolio Company in which the director holds a directorship, investment, or other material interest;

(b) any matter that confers or may confer a financial benefit (as defined in s229) on a related party;

(c) any matter in which a director's spouse, de facto partner, or close family member has a material interest;

(d) any matter in which a director has a conflicting duty to another entity (including a directorship obligation to a Portfolio Company);

(e) approval of the Company's remuneration, equity grants, or benefits to directors or their associates;

(f) any contract or proposed contract in which a director has a direct or indirect pecuniary interest; and

(g) any matter where the director's judgment may reasonably be perceived as influenced by interests other than the Company's interests.

5.3 Standing Notices

Where a director's interest in a matter is of an ongoing nature (for example, a directorship of another entity, a shareholding, or a spousal relationship), the director should give a Standing Notice under section 192. A Standing Notice must:

(a) be in writing;

(b) set out the nature and extent of the interest;

(c) include explicit acknowledgment of the director's obligations under this Policy;

(d) include a statement that the notice extends to any matter in which the interests may reasonably be perceived to influence the director's judgment;

(e) be tabled at the next board meeting after it is given; and

(f) be updated whenever the nature or extent of the interest materially changes.

A Standing Notice has ongoing effect and covers all matters within its stated scope. It does not need to be re-given at each meeting, but the Chair should note at the commencement of each meeting whether any Standing Notices are in effect and whether any updates are required.

5.4 Timing and Form

On appointment: Each new director must complete a disclosure form and provide a Standing Notice (if applicable) before attending their first board meeting.

Annually: Each director must confirm annually that their Standing Notice remains current or provide an updated notice.

As conflicts arise: Directors must disclose new conflicts immediately upon becoming aware of them.

At meetings: The Chair will ask for conflict declarations at the start of each meeting for matters on the agenda.

6. PROCEDURES FOR MANAGING CONFLICTS

6.1 General Procedure

When a conflict of interest or material personal interest is identified in relation to a matter before the Board, the following procedure applies:

Step 1 — Disclosure. The conflicted director discloses the interest (if not already covered by a Standing Notice). The disclosure is recorded in the minutes.

Step 2 — Assessment. The Board (or, where available, the disinterested directors) assesses the nature and severity of the conflict. The assessment considers:

whether the interest is direct or indirect;

whether it is financial or non-financial;

the materiality of the interest and the transaction;

whether it could objectively influence the director's judgment;

whether there is a reasonable perception of conflict; and

whether Independent Advice or other safeguards are required.

Step 4 — Record. The minutes of the meeting must record the conflict management approach taken, including:

which strategy was applied and why;

if s194 reliance, the commercial rationale for the decision;

if Independent Advice obtained, the conclusions and where it is filed;

cross-reference to Register of Interests entry; and

for Material Transactions, confirmation of arm's length terms or member approval obtained.

Step 5 — Register Update. The Company Secretary updates the Register of Interests and Related Party Transaction Register (Annexure C).

6.2.3 Materiality Guidance

To assist in applying the conflict management framework, the Board has adopted the following materiality guidance:

Minor conflicts (typically managed via monitoring):

Transactions under A$10,000 on arm's length terms

Standard commercial hospitality or gifts of trivial value (under A$250)

Matters where the director's interest is remote or purely theoretical

Material conflicts (require recusal, Independent Advice, or heightened safeguards):

Transactions exceeding A$50,000

Any transaction involving non-standard or preferential terms

Any transaction where the director has competing fiduciary duties (including concurrent directorships)

Director remuneration or equity grants

Enforcement, waiver, or variation of existing related party contracts

Portfolio Company investment, valuation, or exit decisions

When in doubt, directors should err on the side of disclosure and seek guidance from the Chair or Company Secretary.

7. CHAPTER 2E — RELATED PARTY TRANSACTIONS

The Company must comply with Chapter 2E of the Act when giving a financial benefit to a related party. The Board must consider, for each proposed related-party transaction:

(a) whether the transaction constitutes a "financial benefit" to a "related party" within the meaning of sections 228–229;

(b) whether an exception under sections 210–216 applies — in particular:

s210: arm's length exception (terms no more favourable than reasonably expected if dealing at arm's length)

s211: reasonable remuneration exception

s212: small amounts exception (less than A$5,000 and reasonable in the circumstances)

(c) if no exception applies, whether member approval has been or can be obtained under section 208;

(d) the documentation required to evidence the exception relied upon (for example, Independent Advice, market comparables, valuation report, or legal opinion); and

(e) for Material Transactions (over A$50,000), mandatory Independent Advice to confirm arm's length terms or commercial reasonableness.

7.2 Independent Advice Requirements

The following transactions require Independent Advice:

(a) any related-party transaction exceeding A$50,000;

(b) any related-party transaction involving non-standard or preferential terms;

(c) any material variation, waiver, or enforcement decision relating to existing related-party contracts;

(d) any transaction where reliance on the s210 arm's length exception is proposed; and

(e) any transaction where the Board determines Independent Advice would strengthen the governance record.

Independent Advice must be filed in the Company's records, cross-referenced in the minutes, and made available to members upon request.

11. CONSEQUENCES OF BREACH

A breach of this policy may result in:

(a) the relevant board resolution being voidable (s195);

(b) criminal liability under section 191(1) (strict liability, up to 50 penalty units / A$22,200 per director);

(c) civil liability for breach of fiduciary duties or the statutory duty of good faith (s181) and duty not to improperly use position (s182);

(d) remedial action determined by the Board, which may include requiring the director to resign from the conflicting position, divest the conflicting interest, or (in serious cases) recommending removal from the Board; and

(e) where a breach involves potential Corporations Act contravention, consideration of whether a report to ASIC or other regulator is required.

The Chair must report any material breach to the Board at the next meeting. The Board will determine appropriate action and, if applicable, notify members.

3.3 Constitution V3.1 — Key Provisions Affecting CBS

The January resolutions (Resolution 1) already authorised CBS’s participation in the Recapitalisation, which included the adoption of Constitution V3.1. This section provides a detailed summary of the provisions that specifically affect CBS’s position as a 4.00% Common Shareholder, for the board’s information and record.

(a) CBS’s Share Class and Rights

CBS holds 40,000 Common Shares (post-split). Common Shares carry one vote per share, pro rata dividend rights, and pro rata participation on winding up. CBS no longer holds “Class B” shares — the new two-class structure comprises F Shares (held by CobaltBlu) and Common Shares (held by CBS and, after Series A, by Series A investors).

(b) Founder Protective Provisions (Clause 20)

While CobaltBlu (the “F Share Majority”) holds ≥30% of all shares on a Fully Diluted Basis, clause 20.2 requires the F Share Majority’s prior written consent for specified major decisions, including:

(i) amendments to the Constitution adversely affecting F Share rights;

(ii) issues of shares ranking senior to or pari passu with F Shares (other than ESOP or Qualifying Financing);

(iii) disposal of all or substantially all Company assets;

(iv) merger, consolidation, or sale of the Company;

(v) voluntary winding up; and

(vi) change in principal business.

Implication for CBS: CobaltBlu has effective veto rights over these matters while it holds ≥30%. CBS’s 4.00% holding does not carry equivalent protective provisions.

(c) Founder Director Appointment (Clause 16.2)

While the F Share Majority holds ≥20% on a Fully Diluted Basis, CobaltBlu has the right to appoint one Founder Director to the Water Roads board. CBS does not currently have a board appointment right under the Constitution, though Jim Ellwood attends as an observer.

(d) F Share Conversion (Clause 4.4)

F Shares automatically convert to Common Shares on a 1:1 basis upon a Qualifying Financing (broadly, a bona fide equity raising of at least A$[●] on arm’s-length terms). After conversion, all shareholders hold the same class (Common), and the Founder Protective Provisions continue based on percentage thresholds, not share class.

(e) Pre-emptive Rights (Clause 8)

All shareholders (including CBS) have pro rata pre-emptive rights on new share issues, subject to carve-outs for the ESOP and Qualifying Financing. CBS has waived pre-emptive rights in respect of the Recapitalisation and Series A under the CBS Consent and Waiver Deed V7.

(f) Transfer Restrictions (Clause 9) and Tag-Along / Drag-Along (Clauses 11–12)

Shares are subject to pre-emptive transfer restrictions (right of first refusal to existing shareholders). Tag-along (clause 11) and drag-along (clause 12) provisions apply to all shareholders. CBS has waived these rights in connection with the Recapitalisation under the Consent Deed.

(g) Related Party Transactions (Clause 18)

The Constitution requires board disclosure and management of related party transactions. This is relevant to CBS given Jeffrey Alan Dusting’s cross-directorships.

(h) ESOP Pool (Clause 26)

The Constitution authorises an ESOP pool of up to 10% of fully diluted share capital. ESOP issuances will dilute all shareholders (including CBS) proportionally. No ESOP options will be granted until the Qualifying Financing strike price is established (clause 26.6).

(i) SHA Supremacy (Clause 22.2)

If a Shareholders Agreement is entered into in connection with the Series A, its terms prevail over the Constitution to the extent of any inconsistency. CBS’s rights under the Constitution may therefore be modified by the SHA.
