0) What exists today (anchors for assumptions)

Barangaroo ferry hub is in place and already serves F3 (Parramatta River) and F4 (Pyrmont Bay) services, integrating directly into the CBD’s western edge. The wharf opened in June 2017 and is designed as a major public transport gateway. Transport for NSWBarangarooWikipedia

Wynyard Walk gives a ~6-minute level, weather-protected walk from Wynyard Station to Barangaroo with capacity for ~20,000 people per hour, which is crucial when comparing water vs. rail access times to Barangaroo. NSW Government

F3 Parramatta River already provides a Rhodes-catchment option via nearby wharves (e.g., Meadowbank / Sydney Olympic Park). Typical Meadowbank ↔ Barangaroo ferry time is ~40–41 min, with ~20-minute headways in many periods. Rome2Rio+1Transit

Rhodes Wharf: the NSW Government has a live proposal to deliver a new ferry wharf and interchange at Rhodes linked to the Rhodes Place Strategy; location and timing are being progressed with the City of Canada Bay and DPHI. This provides the physical anchor for a direct Rhodes–Barangaroo water metro. Transport for NSWPlanning NSW

Other relevant constraints: low-tide disruptions affect only the upper river (Parramatta–Rydalmere) and not Rhodes–Barangaroo, which improves reliability versus a full Parramatta–CBD run. Parramatta Female Factory Friends

1) Proposed operating concept (for modelling)

Headway & span: every 6 minutes for 16 hours/day (6am–10pm) ⇒ 160 one-way trips per direction/day (320 total/day). (960 min ÷ 6 = 160).

Stop pattern: high-frequency Rhodes ⇄ Barangaroo spine (express or very limited stops) to deliver a step-change in end-to-end time vs. today’s all-stops F3.

Indicative in-vehicle time: ~35 minutes one way (express or 1–2 intermediate stops). This is 5–10 minutes quicker than today’s Meadowbank–Barangaroo (~40–41 min) and materially quicker for many Rhodes residents because it removes the surface access to Meadowbank/SOP and avoids train interchange plus the Wynyard Walk. Rome2Rio

Fleet need (order-of-magnitude): Round-trip cycle ≈ 2 × 35 min + turnarounds (≈ 6 min total) ≈ 76 min. To hold a 6-minute headway requires ~76/6 ≈ 13 vessels in line service (12–15 depending on dwell, speed controls—e.g., 15-knot transit zone near the Harbour Bridge—and actual one-way time). NSW Government+1

Vessel capacity reference: Sydney’s Emerald-class carries ≈400 pax; the new Parramatta River Class carries ≈200 pax and is designed for future electrification; TfNSW publishes official fleet capacities. A water-metro could target the 200–400 pax range with dual-side, fast turnarounds. Wikipedia+1Transport for NSW Open Data

Why Barangaroo works as a CBD end-point: Barangaroo is already a major employment node and interchanges directly to rail/Metro via Wynyard Walk (≈6 minutes), so a ferry that lands in-precinct competes well on total door-to-door time

1 EXECUTIVE SUMMARY
This technical brief provides a comprehensive analysis of the hydrofoil technology proposed for Water Roads
Sydney Harbour operations, including detailed performance specifications, operational capabilities, and
validation planning.

The analysis demonstrates that advanced hydrofoil technology from Candela and Artemis represents a
transformative approach to urban water transport, delivering superior performance, efficiency, and
environmental benefits compared to conventional ferry systems.

2 HYDROFOIL TECHNOLOGY OVERVIEW

2.1 Fundamental Hydrofoil Principles
Hydrofoil technology represents a revolutionary advancement in marine propulsion, utilising underwater wings
(foils) to lift the vessel hull above the water surface during operation. This fundamental design principle delivers
transformative improvements in speed, efficiency, and environmental impact compared to conventional
displacement or planing hull vessels.

The physics of hydrofoil operation centres on the generation of hydrodynamic lift through carefully designed
underwater wing surfaces. As the vessel accelerates, water flow over the hydrofoil creates lift forces that
progressively raise the hull above the water surface. Once "on foil," the vessel experiences dramatically reduced
drag, enabling higher speeds with significantly lower energy consumption.

This principle, successfully demonstrated in racing yachts and military applications, has been refined for
commercial passenger transport through advanced materials, control systems, and electric propulsion
integration.

The Water Roads implementation leverages two complementary hydrofoil technologies from global leaders
Candela and Artemis Technologies. These manufacturers have pioneered the integration of hydrofoil design
with electric propulsion systems, creating vessels specifically optimised for urban water transport applications.

The combination of proven hydrofoil principles with cutting-edge electric propulsion and advanced control
systems delivers unprecedented performance in speed, efficiency, comfort, and environmental impact.

2.3 Competitive Technology Analysis
•    Conventional Ferry Comparison:

Conventional Ferry Comparison:
Traditional ferry systems utilise displacement or semi-displacement hull designs that push through water rather
than rising above it. This fundamental design limitation creates significant drag forces that increase
exponentially with speed, requiring substantial engine power and fuel consumption to achieve modest speed
improvements. Conventional ferries typically operate at 15-20 knots maximum speed, with energy
consumption rates of 150-200 kWh per nautical mile for passenger capacities comparable to hydrofoil vessels.

The hydrofoil advantage becomes pronounced at higher speeds, where conventional vessels experience
dramatic increases in energy consumption and operational costs. While conventional ferries may achieve
acceptable efficiency at low speeds, their performance degrades rapidly as speed increases. Hydrofoil vessels
maintain consistent efficiency across their operational speed range, enabling sustained high-speed operation
with lower energy consumption than conventional vessels operating at significantly lower speeds.




•    Alternative High-Speed Marine Technologies:

Alternative High-Speed Marine Technologies:
Several alternative technologies compete in the high-speed marine transport market, including air-cushion
vehicles (hovercraft), surface-effect ships, and advanced catamaran designs. Each technology presents distinct
advantages and limitations compared to hydrofoil systems.

Hovercraft technology enables operation over various surfaces but requires significant energy for air cushion
maintenance, resulting in higher operational costs and noise levels. Surface-effect ships achieve high speeds
but require calm water conditions and specialised infrastructure. Advanced catamarans deliver improved
stability and passenger comfort but cannot match hydrofoil efficiency at high speeds.

Hydrofoil technology offers the optimal balance of speed, efficiency, environmental impact, and operational
flexibility for urban water transport applications. The ability to operate in various sea conditions while
maintaining high speed and low energy consumption positions hydrofoil technology as the preferred solution
for metro-style water transport services.

Water Roads
Water Roads is an innovative urban mobility solution designed to transform how cities move by
leveraging their often underutilized waterways to create high-capacity, zero-emission transit
networks. It specifically focuses on implementing electric water transport systems using electric
hydrofoiling ferries, such as the Candela P-12 and Artemis EF-12 Escape vessels. The project is
designed to operate with a high frequency, metro-style service, offering a "show-up-and-go"
experience. For instance, in Sydney, it is planned to launch a zero-emission, high-frequency marine
urban transport system with electric hydrofoil vessels in 2026, with an initial pilot route connecting
Rhodes to Barangaroo [CRM, 258, 577]. The concept also includes innovative "Deep Power" charging
infrastructure, which involves water-based battery storage barges with solar arrays, aimed at
eliminating land-side power constraints.

Why Water Roads Matters: A Blue
Ocean Strategy Approach
Water Roads matters because it applies a Blue
Ocean Strategy to redefine urban transport,
moving beyond the "red ocean" of competing
traditional land and water-based services.
Instead of fighting over existing demand, Water
Roads aims to create uncontested market
space by transforming waterways into new,
highly efficient transport assets.
Transforming Urban Waterways
  Underutilized Assets
  Urban waterways represent vast untapped potential for efficient transportation
  networks in congested cities.



  Zero-Emission Technology
  Electric hydrofoiling vessels provide sustainable transport solutions with minimal
  environmental impact.



  Metro-Style Frequency
  High-frequency schedules transform water transport from occasional service to
  reliable daily commuting option.
Value Innovation Framework
This strategic approach is best understood through the lens of a Value Innovation framework,
particularly the Eliminate-Reduce-Raise-Create (ERRC) Grid:



Eliminate
   Diesel dependency: Unlike traditional ferries that pollute air and water, Water Roads uses electric
   vessels, eliminating diesel emissions.
   Low-frequency schedules: Traditional ferries are often low-frequency (e.g., every 40 minutes),
   which Water Roads eliminates by offering metro-style, high-frequency service (e.g., every 5-10
   minutes).
   Wake limitations: Electric hydrofoiling ferries glide over water, eliminating significant wake, which
   allows for higher speeds in harbor zones without environmental damage.
Reduce: Costs and Environmental
Impact
Reduce

  Operating costs: Electric hydrofoil vessels use significantly less energy (85% less energy than
  traditional ferries) and are projected to have 85% lower operating costs than traditional ferries,
  leading to an estimated cost of AUD 0.15/km compared to AUD 0.70/km for other modes.
  CAPEX via modular infrastructure: The "Deep Power" charging solution, with its water-based
  battery storage barges and solar arrays, is patent-pending and designed to be rapidly deployed,
  reducing capital expenditure compared to extensive land acquisition for new roads or rail. It can
  achieve 30% faster charging with 40% less capital investment.
  Congestion costs: By moving commuters from roads and railways to waterways, Water Roads
  directly reduces urban congestion costs, which are quantified at $8 billion/year in Sydney. The
  Rhodes to Barangaroo route alone is projected to deliver $4.7 million in reduced road congestion
  benefits.
  Journey times: Travel time on the Rhodes to Barangaroo route is projected to be cut by 50% (from
  60+ minutes by road/rail to 20 minutes by Water Roads).

Create: New Transport
Paradigms
    Metro-style marine transit
    This creates an entirely new category of urban transport that combines the
    speed and efficiency of a metro with the environmental benefits and unique
    advantages of water travel.


    New routes on underutilized waterways
    Water Roads identifies and targets "Blue Transit Deserts"4areas adjacent to
    water bodies that suffer from congested land transportation despite having
    navigable waterways. This creates new, viable transport corridors where land-
    based expansion is costly and difficult.


    Deep Power charging infrastructure
    This patent-pending water-based solution is a novel approach to charging
    electric vessels, leveraging water cooling for efficiency and reducing land-side
    power constraints.
