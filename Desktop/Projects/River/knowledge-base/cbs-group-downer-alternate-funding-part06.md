We have completed a review of the FE04 HVAC Energy Efficiency Report and updated the proposal accordingly.

Context

Situation

Downer has a number of innovations and improvements they want to propose to their clients for mutual benefit.   One recent example is the development of a suite of enhancements for the TfNSW Rolling Stock fleet that included improvements with measurable benefits in reduced maintenance, improved passenger experience & safety, and energy usage.  The HVAC Energy Efficiency initiative is a well-developed initiative with a great deal of pre-existing modelling already completed.

Complication

As is often the case, these innovation and improvement initiatives struggle to progress from concept to project execution.   Often this is due to challenges securing the up-front capital to fund the project, typically because;

the benefits are accrued by others – other departments, customers, the public, etc.

the benefits in operation expense cannot be accrued to create the required capital

Question

The focus upon CO2 emissions reductions has led to the establishment of a number of funding mechanisms designed to provide capex support for energy reduction initiatives.  Are we able to leverage these funding mechanisms to secure the upfront capital needed to mobilise the Sydney Fleet, HVAC Energy Efficiency inoitiative.   And if so, are we able to extend the learnings to determine what the boundaries are for other similar Downer initiatives.

Answer

We propose that Downer engage CBS Group to investigate the feasibility of these funding sources – using of the Fleet Enhancement HVAC Energy Efficiency Initiative (FE04) as the focus for the initial works, providing the basis for subsequent application of these mechanisms in other contexts.

Approach

We propose a 3 stage approach to the engagement as shown in the table below.

For ERF:

Reverse auction format used to get a contract. Done through online bidding platform AusTender.

Three forms of contract which you can choose from at auction registration:

‘Standard’ contracts which has a delivery timeframe of 7 years (10 years for some).

‘Short-term’ contract which requires multiple Kyoto ACCU deliveries within a period of less than seven years. A project can have a maximum of three short-term contracts associated with it throughout the project’s crediting period and may cover part of the project’s abatement within the contracted period.

‘Immediate delivery’ contract in which the nominated ACCUS will be delivered within 10 days of auction. You can only bid for this contract if you have the required number of ACCUs already present in your account. You have a maximum of 10 immediate delivery contracts per project under the projects crediting period, and the minimum volume for immediate delivery contracts is 10% of the forward abatement estimate of the project or 15,000 ACCUs (whichever is lower).

ACCUs are considered ‘delivered’ under carbon abatement contract when they have been transferred from your ANREU account into the Clean Energy Regulator’s ANREU delivery account. To receive payment, an invoice must be sent to the Clean Energy Regulator

Payment is made to the bank account for your contract within 20 business days from our receipt of your invoice and the units have been delivered to the Clean Energy Regulators account.

From the above information, it appears funding cannot be upfront, and the ACCUs must be accredited and delivered before any form of payment. However, by utilising the immediate delivery contract (or short-term) you could increase the speed of payment – instead of receiving a lump sum at the end of the project accreditation period, you could cash in ACCUs periodically as the project progresses.

For ESS:

The energy saver for the point of the IHEAB ESS method can be either, the original energy saver which is the purchaser, or the nominated energy saver which is someone the original energy saver has nominated as the energy saver by completing a Nomination Form.

There are 3 main methods:

‘Deemed Energy Savings Methods’ which uses default factors and equations to calculate energy savings and includes the proposed IHEAB method. 

‘Metered Baseline Methods’ which calculates energy savings based on actual measured energy consumption before and after an activity has been implemented.

‘Project Impact Assessment with Measurement and Verification Method’ in which energy savings are calculated using a ‘measurement and verification’ approach which is ‘signed off’ by a measurement and verification professional (usually a third party). Energy saving certificates can be forward created for up to 10 years or created annually for this method.

Test reports for products you want to fit (to save energy) need to be submitted to IPART from an accredited laboratory or equivalent.

Three types of contracts; spot contract, forward contract, option contract (may need to hold an Australian Financial Services License when dealing with some contracts). These contracts are determined between an Accredited Certificate Provider and a buyer and there are currently no standard contracts or a recognised exchange for trading. For further information, refer: https://www.ess.nsw.gov.au/Home/About-ESS/Overview-of-the-ESS/Energy-Savings-Certificates

Audits will cost the company, and should be detracted from scheme potential earnings estimates along with non-refundable $2,500 application fee
