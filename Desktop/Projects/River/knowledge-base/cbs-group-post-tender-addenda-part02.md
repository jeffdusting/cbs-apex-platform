Methodology

The Tenderer is to provide details of its proposed methodology (maximum three (3) x A4 pages, minimum font size 10) for the delivery of the Services.

CBS Response:  Refer next page

CBS Group will deliver Systems Integration Services for FFP through a structured, phase-aligned methodology tailored to the Program's six work packages and aligned with TfNSW technical standards. Our approach draws directly from our SI lead roles on HCMT ($2.3B), QTMP ($7.1B) and various other large, complex rail projects, adapting proven frameworks to FFP's specific context minimisramp-up time. Our team members bring over 15 years' experience with Sydney Trains and TfNSW, providing deep understanding of network operations, governance processes, Government Gateway reviews and stakeholder expectations.

1. Three-Environment Integration Framework

FFP comprises three integration environments, each requiring tailored SI approaches while maintaining program coherence:

(1) Rolling Stock Environment — The Future Fleet trains and simulators represent the highest integration complexity, requiring comprehensive interface management, verification and validation, and safety assurance. This environment drives the program's core SI requirements and demands rigorous traceability from customer requirements through to verification evidence. Our HCMT experience delivering SI for 65 high-capacity trains directly informs this approach.

(2) Heavy Infrastructure Environment — The Production Facility, Maintenance Centre and Stabling Yard are interdependent facilities requiring coordinated SI oversight. Our approach focuses on production process integration and operational handover. system interfaces between rolling stock and support facilities asset performance outcomes regardless of specific facility configuration.

(3) Enabling Works Environment — AHMC Augmentation and Infrastructure Enabling Works are discrete packages requiring proportionate SI oversight. We will establish integration themes without over-engineering simpler scope elements, applying lessons from QTMP where proportionate effort for enabling works maintained assurance while preserving cost-effectiveness.

2. Phase-Aligned Delivery

Core Services (Design, –): establish the foundational SI framework including: SI StrategyInterface Management Strategy(per TS 01465) Program-level Requirements Architecture Safety Assurance Plan (per TS 04981) Operational Integration Strategy (per TS 01473)

Optional Services  will  

Optional Services  will provide RFT response evaluation, technical clarification support, and integration risk assessment of proposals. 

3. Systems Integration Management Plan Structure

Our SIMP will establish scope boundaries and integration responsibilities across all six work packages, supported by a system architecture defining the interface hierarchy. Key SIMP elements include:

Requirements Verification Framework: A Verification Cross-Reference Index (VCRI) will link each requirement to its verification evidence and method. Verification methods (analysis, inspection, demonstration, test) will be assigned per requirement type, ensuring appropriate rigour while maintaining efficiency. The V&V approach will define independence requirements per TS 01465, with ISA oversight of safety-critical verifications.

Interface Management: Interface agreements will be formalised through Interface Control Working Groups (ICWGs) with defined escalation for unresolved issues. Each interface will progress through defined maturity stages: Identified → Specified → Agreed → Verified. We will facilitate fortnightly interface workshops between Technical Advisors, structured around specific interface themes.

Configuration and Coordination: Configuration management per TS 01455 and coordination protocols with Technical Advisors, ISA and ONRSR will complete the SIMP structure. This aligns with TS 01465, TS 01471 (Asset Information Management), RISSB AS 7473, and ISO/IEC 15288:2023.

4. Integration Risk Identification and Escalation

CBS Group will maintain a Integration Risk Register from mobilisation, identifying risks across technical, schedule and interface dimensions. Integration risks will be identified through structured interface workshops, design review participation, and contribution to program HAZOPs and HAZIDs.

Key integration risks we anticipate for FFP include: interface maturity variance across work packages with different procurement timelines; Production Facility scope uncertainty affecting maintenance support assumptions; T4 corridor access constraints for brownfield connections; fleet/infrastructure commissioning sequencing dependencies; and stakeholder alignment across TfNSW, Sydney Trains and delivery partners.

Risks will be escalated through established governance: integration risks affecting single work packages to the FFP Project Director; cross-package risks to IPE NAC; and safety-critical risks to Transport Network ROAMS Committee. We will coordinate with the Risk Management Services Advisor to ensure alignment with program-level frameworks.

5. Quality Assurance

All deliverables will be subject to a two-tier review process:

Internal Technical Review: Peer review by team members not directly involved in production, ensuring technical accuracy and completeness against contract requirements and TfNSW standards.  

Senior Quality Assurance Review: Strategic review by CBS Group leadership drawing on current TfNSW AMSS experience, ensuring alignment with TfNSW expectations and industry practice, and providing strategic challenge for alignment to whole-of-life outcomes. 

We will maintain a Deliverables Register tracking status against contract milestones, with fortnightly internal reporting and proactive engagement with the Principal's Representative on progress and emerging issues.

6. Resource Continuity and Mobilisation

Our proposed team (Section 10) includes personnel who have worked together across HCMT and QTMP, providing established working relationships and consistent delivery capability. The team also has current TfNSW experience.  This continuity and TfNSW familiarity reduces coordination overhead and accelerates effective team performance from mobilisation. All Key People are senior engineering consultants with current or recent rail project experience.

Mobilisation approach: Week 1 will focus on team induction, security clearances, system access, document repository familiarisation, and stakeholder introductions. In parallel, the team will start the detailed review of existing FFP documentation and identify the required stakeholder workshops.  Full team operational capability is targeted for end of Week 1, enabling the workshops, Technical Advisor coordination meetings, and draft SI Strategy and Architecture outlines to begin in Week 2. This rapid mobilisation is required to have the initial draft SI deliverables available for TfNSW input by end of Week 4, and is possible and de-risked with the experienced CBS team. 

In delivering the SI Services, the CBS Group team will:

Establish Effective Governance Early:  Robust early governance is mandatory for complex programs, protecting cost, scope, and schedule before irreversible decisions lock in technical risk, safety risk, and operability outcomes. We foster a "Best for Project" culture through collaborative governance forums with clear boundaries, communication lines, and escalation pathways for early issue escalation and resolution.

Establish Effective Systems Architecture:  The system architecture provides the foundation for governance, interface management, and SI/SA service delivery. Our approach applies proportionate integration rigour recognising different work package complexities, through:

Stable Intermediate Forms: Baseline systems at key maturity points, allowing different development rates while maintaining interface control.

Careful Consideration of Control: Triage control levelsby-system, only what is needed to deliver outcomes; avoid both under-control (losing system benefits) and over-control (lack of effectiveness).

CBS Group configures appropriate tools (DOORS NG, Relatics, Capella) ensuring traceability and visibility.

Define and Allocate Interfaces:  Complex programs typically fail at interfaces. We establish frameworks controlling physical, functional, information, and organisational interfaces with clear responsibilities, accountable decision-making, and defined escalation paths.  We maintain project-wide interface registers cross-linked to IRS/ICDs, configuration baselines, and design approvals, providing "single source of truth" for interface definition, ownership, status, and risk across all packages and phases.  

FFP Interface Management:  FFP's multiple independent delivery contracts over extended timeframes require structured engagement plans with internal/external projects. We coordinate through early workshops, continuous visibility via regular meetings, and proactive tracking/escalation of interface risks with solutions-focused, best-for-project mindset.

Establish Effective Assurance Regime:  Our methodology aligns SI and SA activities with FFP's lifecycle phases, TfNSW gates, and milestones, providing progressive assurance as constituent systems develop at different times. Our assurance approach will include:  

Program-Level Goal Structured Notation: Define success criteria and allocate constituent system goals, identifying required interface interactions to achieve higher-level program goals.

Scope, Risk, and Interface Allocation: Using GSN and System Architecture, delegate Hazard Log, Human Factors, RAM, Security, EMI/EMC, and operational integration targets through requirements derivation.

Change Assurance Procedures: Establish controlled flexibility for FFP's size and complexity.

These frameworks significantly reduce integration risk through years of operational phases.

Plan Transition to Operations:  Early operational integration (OI) aligns people, systems, processes, and technology from project commencement. Recognising Sydney Trains will operate and maintain FFP assets, we will conduct monthly OI workshops, develop scalable Deployment and Operational Integration Strategy accommodating future fleet replacements, identify operational dependencies/constraints, and plan staged commissioning minimiing disruption. Our OI Strategy defines milestones, resource requirements, training pathways, and operational procedure management aligned with asset delivery timelines.

1. Understanding of the Project and Services

The Future Fleet Program represents a generational investment in NSW's suburban rail capacity, procuring new rolling stock to replace the Tangara fleet from the early 2030s. The program will deliver trains that remain in service through the 2060s, supported by a potential Production Facility, new Maintenance Centre, Stabling Yard, Auburn HMC augmentation, and Infrastructure Enabling Works. FFP's six work packages create a complex integration challenge requiring coordination across multiple procurement phases while maintaining interfaces with the operational Sydney Trains network.

The SI Services establish the integration framework during  Design (Core Services) supporting Gateway 2 and FBC, maintain technical coordination through 

Key deliverables include:

Core Services: , System Architecture,RVTM Framework,Safety Assurance Plan, Program Hazard Log, Operational Integration trategy, and 

Optional Services: support documentation.

2. Key Objectives

We understand the key SI objectives as:  defining an interface architecture supporting multiple procurement packages with varying timelines (per Services Table 3); .

The Operational Integration Strategy will address driver training requirements, service introduction sequencing, and depot procedure interfaces to ensure seamless transition from Tangara to Future Fleet operations. This includes coordination with Sydney Trains Engineering and Timetabling for possession planning, maintenance access arrangements, and network interface management during fleet transition.

3. Key Challenges, Opportunities and Risks

Challenges:

Compressed Timeline: The Core Services timelinerequires focused prioritisation of critical-path deliverablesefficient mobilisation. Our established frameworks from HCMT and QTMP enable rapid deployment without learning curve delays.

Production Facility Uncertainty: The Production Facility work package is identified as 'potential', creating facility configuration uncertainty. We will address this through needs-based facility definition—establishing functional requirements (maintenance access, equipment clearances, service intervals) independent of specific facility solutions, enabling SI continuity as scope is confirmed. This approach proved effective on QTMP where manufacturing facility scope evolved during definition.

Brownfield Integration: Integration with the operational T4 Eastern Suburbs & Illawarra Line presents specific risks: T4 possession constraints for Maintenance Centre and Stabling Yard connections; live network signalling interfaces requiring staged commissioning; and traction power augmentation coordination with existing 1500V DC infrastructure. Our SI approach will ensure these interfaces are defined early and coordinated through Sydney Trains CCB.

Opportunities: 

Our team's directly applicable experience from leading SI on HCMT and QTMP enables rapid deployment of proven frameworks, minimising ramp-up time. As an example, our HCMT interface register template alone will save approximately 3-4 weeks of development time during Core Services, enabling earlier TfNSW review and iteration cycles. Early interface definition during Core Services will reduce downstream integration risk and inform procurement strategy.

Risks:

Interface proliferation: Multiple procurement packages risk creating excessive interface complexity. Our approach and assurance frameworks mitigates this by using the interface criticality to apply the appropriately tailored management.   

Stakeholder alignment: The possible difficulties in achieving consensus and resolving problems across the many stakeholders will be mitigated through CBS Group’s ISO 44001 aligned collaborative approaches.  

4. Lessons Learned from Previous Experience

From HCMT (SI Lead): Early establishment of interface baselines prevents downstream integration issues that are costly and time-consuming to resolve during delivery. Clear delineation between rolling stock and infrastructure SI responsibilities is essential to avoid gaps and overlaps. Our ONRSR engagement addressed high-capacity signalling interfaces and fire/egress compliance—directly relevant to FFP's regulatory pathway. Operational stakeholder engagement from project outset improves integration outcomes and reduces late-stage change.

From QTMP (SI Lead): Manufacturing facility SI requires focus on production process integration rather than operational interfaces. Proportionate SI effort for enabling works avoids over-engineering while maintaining assurance. Our experience with evolving facility scope—where the Production Facility configuration changed during definition—directly informs our approach to FFP's Production Facility uncertainty.

5. Collaborative Working Approach

CBS Group will embed within the TfNSW FFP team at 680 George Street. We will participate in the FFP Integrated Project Team structure, attending weekly SI coordination meetings, monthly Program Control Group sessions, and Gateway review preparation workshops. Our embedded presence enables informal coordination and rapid response to emerging integration issues.

We will establish clear coordination protocols with the Technical Advisor – Rolling Stock, Technical Advisor – Infrastructure, Technical Advisor – Power Modelling, and the Independent Safety Assessor. We will support IPE NAC, Transport Network ROAMS Committee, and Sydney Trains CCB assurance and approval pathways.

Our team members bring over 15 years' continuous engagement with Sydney Trains and TfNSW—from the Sydney Growth Trains project through to recent work with Asset Management Branch (now Prioritisation and Asset Management) on governance and assurance frameworks, and Digital Systems projects. This includes direct CCB experience: configuration change submissions for signalling modifications, asset register updates, and maintenance procedure interfaces.

Our collaborative approach is underpinned by ISO 44001 principles—CBS Group has accredited ISO 44001 facilitators and has led collaborative relationship frameworks on long-term projects including the Western Harbour Tunnel 30-year Construction and Asset Management contracts.

6. Value and Efficiency

CBS Group's specialist model delivers value through focused expertise rather than large team overhead. Our directly applicable experience from HCMT and QTMP means frameworks, templates, and approaches can be adapted rather than developed from scratch—reducing both cost and schedule risk. This efficiency translates to more senior practitioner time on value-adding activities (stakeholder engagement, technical problem-solving, risk identification) rather than document production.

Our approach delivers senior expertise at every interaction, not junior resources supervised remotely. All nominated Key People have direct experience on comparable programs and will be actively engaged in FFP delivery.

7. Customer-Centred Design

FFP's ultimate customer is the travelling public. Our SI approach will ensure passenger experience requirements—accessibility, comfort, journey time reliability—are captured in the requirements architecture and traced through to verification. This alignment ensures FFP delivers measurable passenger benefits from day one of revenue operations, supporting TfNSW's customer-centred design principles and Future Transport outcomes including 30-minute city accessibility targets.
