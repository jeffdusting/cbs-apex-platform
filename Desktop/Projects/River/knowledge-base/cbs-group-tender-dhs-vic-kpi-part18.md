# Virtual Evaluation Panel — Briefing, Re-Evaluation and Remediation

## Part 1: Panel Briefing — What the Previous Assessment Missed

### 1.1 Evaluation Criteria Structure Discovery

The RFP evaluation criteria table (Section 2, Table 3) reveals critical information the panel did not previously consider.

**Undisclosed weightings.** The evaluation criteria table has a "Weighting" column, but all weighting cells are blank. The RFP evaluation guidance notes: "Weightings are optional and a decision concerning the use of these must be made by the Engagement Manager. If weightings are used, they must be disclosed." Since the weightings are not disclosed, one of two conclusions follows: either weightings exist but are deliberately undisclosed (unusual for Victorian Government procurement), or the Department has not assigned specific weightings and will apply equal weighting or evaluator judgement. For our assessment, we model both an equal-weight scenario and a methodology-heavy scenario, given Criterion 3 is the only criterion with detailed sub-criteria spelled out in the evaluation table.

**Criterion 3 discrepancy — confirmed as two separate requirements.** The evaluation criteria table describes Criterion 3 as "Methodology and project delivery approach" with 11 detailed sub-criteria. However, the response template (Section 3, Table 11) describes Criterion 3 as "Commitment to sector-wide collaboration, knowledge sharing, and capacity building." These are substantively different requirements. The most conservative reading is that both must be addressed. Our current response addresses both as separate sections (3.3 Collaboration and 3.4 Methodology).

**Only Criterion 3 has sub-criteria in the evaluation table.** This strongly suggests the methodology/collaboration criterion (however defined) is the most important criterion to evaluators. The 11 sub-criteria are: sound understanding of key issues and risks; identification of critical activities and outputs; sequencing of activities; effective communication of key activities and steps; collaboration and lateral thinking ensuring value-add to deliverables; culture of transparency and cooperation; robust project plan with milestones, timelines and resource allocation; clear understanding of objectives, issues and risks; transparent stakeholder communication; risk identification and mitigation strategies; and leveraging best-practice frameworks and innovative approaches.

### 1.2 DH Scoring Rubric

The previous assessment used a generic 1–5 scale. The DH specifies a **0–5 scale** with precise descriptors that the panel must now use.

| Score | Label | Key Differentiator |
|---|---|---|
| **5** | Superior | Comprehensively documented with **all claims fully substantiated** |
| **4** | Excellent | Convincing and credible; overall claims are **supported** |
| **3** | Adequate | Credible but **not completely convincing** |
| **2** | Marginal | Has **minor omissions**; marginal capability |
| **1** | Poor | Unconvincing; significant flaws |
| **0** | Unacceptable | Did not provide requested information |

The critical insight: moving from 4 (Excellent) to 5 (Superior) requires every claim to be fully substantiated with evidence — named projects, quantified outcomes, verifiable credentials. This is an extremely high bar.

### 1.3 PAS Procurement Context

The Department has issued this RFP to fewer than 5 PAS panel members. Under the Victorian PAS SPC framework, the number of quotes depends on agency procurement policy, with guidance to seek broader quotations for complex or high-value services. The Administrative Guidelines on Engaging Professional Services require Secretary-level approval for engagements exceeding $150,000 (incl GST).

Selecting fewer than 5 suppliers strongly suggests the estimated engagement value is in the **$150,000–$500,000 (incl GST)** range. Our fee must sit credibly within a **$250,000–$350,000 (incl GST)** envelope to be competitive without appearing under-resourced.

## Part 5: Resourcing and Fee Issues

Current plan: ~203 person-days → estimated fee $500k–$600k incl GST. This exceeds the likely procurement band ($150k–$500k) for an RFP issued to <5 suppliers.

The weekly plan cannot be cleanly split into phase-based pricing because phases overlap. The RFP pricing table needs clear day counts per person per phase. These need reconciliation.

Listed as "Senior Consultant" but is CBS Group's Managing Director. Should be corrected to Director-level PAS position title.

## 1. Executive Summary

**Situation:** The Victorian Department of Health oversees 77 public health services operating under diverse maintenance models — from fully in-house teams to fully outsourced arrangements. This diversity has produced inconsistent asset performance outcomes and fragmented reporting, limiting the Department's ability to benchmark performance, allocate capital effectively, intervene where clinical and safety risks are emerging, or hold health services accountable against common standards.

**Complication:** Without standardised maintenance KPIs, the Department cannot systematically identify where aging building stock is deteriorating, where essential services infrastructure (HVAC, medical gases, backup power, nurse call systems) is at risk, or where maintenance teams are struggling with inadequate resources. The Department has invested in developing a comprehensive KPI framework — 12 KPIs across two implementation phases, supported by a 7-level maturity model (Level 0: Ad Hoc through Level 7: Transforming) detailed in the September 2025 Maintenance Standards Guidelines Performance Monitoring document. However, translating this framework from policy into operational practice across 77 organisations — each with different CMMS capabilities, workforce capacity, building ages, service profiles, and competing clinical priorities — is a real implementation challenge.

**Question:** How can the Department implement its KPI framework in a way that is practical, scalable, and enduring — achieving adoption rather than compliance paperwork — while minimising the additional reporting burden on health service maintenance teams that are already operating under resource constraints?

**Answer:** CBS Group brings Australia's most proven methodology for phased KPI implementation across complex, multi-stakeholder asset management environments. Our proprietary CAPITAL framework has been successfully deployed to implement performance metrics across diverse organisations with varying maturity levels, delivering over $1 billion in validated savings for Transport for NSW across road tunnel assets managed by multiple contractors with fundamentally different capabilities. We propose a team of senior asset management, systems engineering, and change management specialists — led from our Melbourne office — who will apply this proven methodology to the Department's specific health infrastructure context. Our approach is designed around three commitments: segmenting health services by actual maturity (not size or geography) to ensure support is proportionate and relevant; co-designing implementation pathways with health services so they own the framework rather than having it imposed; and integrating KPI data capture with existing maintenance systems and workflows to minimise administrative burden on operational teams.

Our team includes professionals who have worked within Australia's leading health facility management organisations — Broadspectrum (now Ventia), Downer, and Cushman Wakefield — bringing direct understanding of how hospital maintenance is contracted, delivered, and reported at the operational level. Nicole Caranguian designed and built asset management systems for health facilities including the Royal Adelaide Hospital and Royal Adelaide Children's Hospital; Kate Heath developed KPI frameworks for competitive health FM contracts at Broadspectrum. This is not transferred methodology — it is direct health infrastructure experience.
